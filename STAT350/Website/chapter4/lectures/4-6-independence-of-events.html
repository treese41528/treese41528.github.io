4.6. Independence of EventsÔÉÅ

In the previous sections, we explored how knowledge about an event influences the probabilities involving another event. But do events always influence each other? The answer is no, and we say that such events are idnependent. In this section, we‚Äôll dive deeper into the concept of independence.

Road map üß≠

  • Understand what it means for events to be independent.

  • Distinguish between independence and mutually exclusive events.

  • See how independence simplifies probability calculations.

  • Explore different types of independence: pairwise vs. mutual.

  • Connect independence to the concepts covered in previous chapters.

4.6.1. IndependenceÔÉÅ

We say two events \(A\) and \(B\) are independent if the occurrence of one event does not affect the probability of the other. Formally, events \(A\) and \(B\) are independent if

\[P(A|B) = P(A) \text{ and } P(B|A) = P(B).\]

The first equation means that knowing \(B\) occurs provides no additional information about \(A\). Likewise, the second equation says that knowing \(A\) occurs does not provide any update on the probability of \(B\).

Since independence is a symmetric relationship between events, the two conditions are equivalent‚Äîone implies the other.

Additional Equivalent Expressions

Independence of \(A\) and \(B\) also implies the pairwise independence of \(A'\) and \(B\), \(A\) and \(B'\), and \(A'\) and \(B'\).

Special Multiplication RuleÔÉÅ

When two events are independent, the general multiplication rule simplifies to:

\[P(A \cap B) = P(A) P(B).\]

Why Is This True?ÔÉÅ

The general multiplication rule says that for any pair of events \(A\) and \(B\),

\[P(A \cap B) = P(A|B) P(B).\]

Independence of \(A\) and \(B\) implies \(P(A|B) = P(A)\), which allows us to replace \(P(A|B)\) with \(P(A)\) in the general addition rule.

‚ÄºÔ∏è Avoid the common mistake ‚ÄºÔ∏è

This is a special-case rule which can only be used when the independence of A and B has been mathematically shown. When unsure, always begin with the general version (Revisit Section 4.3).

4.6.2. Independence vs. Mutual ExclusitivityÔÉÅ

It‚Äôs important to distinguish between independence and mutual exclusivity, as these concepts are often confused but are fundamentally different. Recall their definitions:

  • Mutually exclusive (or disjoint) events cannot occur simultaneously. Their intersection is empty.

  • Independent events provide no information about each other. Knowing that one occurs does not change the probability of the other.

These concepts are in fact incompatible in general. Let us see why through two events \(A\) and \(B\). For generality, assume that their probabilities are both non-zero.

  1. If \(A\) and \(B\) are mutually exclusive, then they cannot be independent.

    If \(A\) and \(B\) are mutually exclusive, then \(P(A \cap B) = 0\). Using the conditional probability formula,

    \[P(A|B) = \frac{P(A \cap B)}{P(B)} = \frac{0}{P(B)} = 0.\]

    But \(P(A) > 0\), so \(P(A|B) = 0 \neq P(A)\). This means \(A\) and \(B\) are not independent.

  2. If \(A\) and \(B\) independent, then they cannot be mutually exclusive.

    If \(A\) and \(B\) are independent, then

    \[P(A \cap B) = P(A)P(B) > 0\]

    since both \(P(A)\) and \(P(B)\) are non-zero. This means that \(A\) and \(B\) have a non-empty intersection. In other words, they are not mutually exclusive.

4.6.3. Pairwise Independence vs. Mutual IndependenceÔÉÅ

When dealing with more than two events, we can define different levels of independence.

Pairwise Independence

A collection of events \(\{A_1, A_2, \cdots, A_n\}\) is pairwise independent if all pairs of events are independent.

For example, four events \(\{A_1, A_2, A_3, A_4\}\) are pairwise independent if

\[\begin{split}P(A_1|A_2) = P(A_1) \quad &\text{and} \quad P(A_1 \cap A_2) = P(A_1)P(A_2)\\ P(A_1|A_3) = P(A_1) \quad &\text{and} \quad P(A_1 \cap A_3) = P(A_1)P(A_3)\\ & \vdots\end{split}\]

for all pairs.

Mutual Independence

Mutual indepdendence of events is a stronger condition where the special multiplication rule holds for all combinations of events, not just pairs. For four events to be mutually independent, they must be pairwise independent and satisfy

\[\begin{split}P(A_1 \cap A_2 \cap A_3) &= P(A_1)P(A_2)P(A_3)\\ P(A_1 \cap A_2 \cap A_4) &= P(A_1)P(A_2)P(A_4)\\ &\vdots\end{split}\]

for all triplets, and

\[P(A_1 \cap A_2 \cap A_3 \cap A_4) = P(A_1)P(A_2)P(A_3)P(A_4).\]

There exist cases where events are pairwise independent but not mutually independent.

Exampleüí°: Circuit Reliability

Consider two electrical systems with different circuit configurations.

  • System 1: Four parallel paths (labeled 1, 2, 3, 4) connect points A and B.

  • System 2: Three paths (labeled 1, 2, 3) connect points A and B, but with more complex connectivity.

Each path contains mutually independent switches that are each activated with probability 0.3. The system functions if current can flow from point A to point B. We want to calculate the probability that each system will function.

System 1

  • Let \(L_i\) denote the event that the \(i\) th line is on, for each \(i=1,2,3,4\).

  • We are given that \(P(L_i)=0.3\) for all \(i\). This also implies \(P(L_i')=0.7\) for all \(i\).

  • Let \(F_1^+\) denote the event that system 1 is functioning.

For the system to function, at least one path must be on. In other words, Line 1 or Line 2 or Line 3 or Line 4 must be on. This gives:

\[P(F_1^+) = P(L_1 \cup L_2 \cup L_3 \cup L_4)\]

Using the complement rule and De Morgan‚Äôs law:

\[\begin{split}P(F_1^+) &= 1 - P((L_1 \cup L_2 \cup L_3 \cup L_4)')\\ &= 1 - P(L_1' \cap L_2' \cap L_3' \cap L_4')\end{split}\]

Since the lines operate independently, we can use the special multiplication rule:

\[\begin{split}P(F_1^+) &= 1 - P(l_1') P(l_2') P(l_3') P(l_4')\\ &= 1 - (0.7)^4 = 1 - 0.2401 = 0.7599.\end{split}\]

System 2

  • Let \(L_i\) now denote the event that the \(i\) th line is on in the second system, for each \(i=1,2,3\).

  • Let \(F_2^+\) denote the event that system 1 is functioning.

The first steps are identical to the first system.

\[\begin{split}P(F_2^+) &= P(L_1 \cup L_2 \cup L_3) = 1 - P((L_1 \cup L_2 \cup L_3)')\\ &= 1 - P(L_1' \cap L_2' \cap L_3') = 1- P(L_1')P(L_2')P(L_2')\end{split}\]

Now we need to calculate the probability that each line is not functioning.

  • \(P(L_1') = 1 - P(\text{both switches are on}) = 1-(0.3)^2 = 0.91\)

  • \(P(L_2') = 1 - 0.3 = 0.7\)

  • \(P(L_3') = 1 - P(\text{all three switches are on}) = 1-(0.3)^3 = 0.973\)

Putting it all together,

\[P(F_2^+) = 1 - (0.91 \times 0.7 \times 0.973) \approx 0.3802.\]

The difference in reliability between the two systems (76% vs. 38%) highlights how parallel paths increase reliability compared to series connections.

4.6.4. Bringing It All TogetherÔÉÅ

Key Takeaways üìù

  1. Independence means that knowing one event occurs doesn‚Äôt change the probability of another event.

  2. Independence allows us to use the special multiplication rule: \(P(A \cap B) = P(A) P(B)\).

  3. Mutually exclusivity and independence generally do not occur simultaneously.

  4. Pairwise independence means all pairs of events are independent, while mutual independence requires that all combinations of events follow the special multiplication rule.

  5. Independence often leads to powerful simplifications in computation, but its should use should always follow mathematical justification.

ExercisesÔÉÅ

  1. Basic Independence: A standard deck of 52 cards has 4 suits (hearts, diamonds, clubs, spades) and 13 ranks (Ace, 2-10, Jack, Queen, King). You draw one card randomly. Let A be the event ‚Äúthe card is a heart‚Äù and B be the event ‚Äúthe card is a King.‚Äù Are events A and B independent? Mathematically justify your answer.

  2. Independence vs. Mutual Exclusivity: Give an example from everyday life of two events that are

    1. both independent and not mutually exclusive,

    2. mutually exclusive but not independent, and

    3. neither independent nor mutually exclusive.

  3. Circuit Reliability: Consider a circuit with three components in series. Each component works independently with probability 0.9. Calculate the probability that the circuit functions (all components must work for the circuit to function).

  4. Weather Prediction: Suppose there‚Äôs a 30% chance of rain on Saturday and a 40% chance of rain on Sunday. If the weather on these two days is independent, what is the probability that

    1. it rains on both days,

    2. it rains on at least one day,

    3. it doesn‚Äôt rain at all over the weekend?

  5. Pairwise vs. Mutual Independence: Three fair coins are tossed. Let A = ‚Äúfirst coin shows heads,‚Äù B = ‚Äúsecond coin shows heads,‚Äù and C = ‚Äúan odd number of heads appears.‚Äù Show that these events are pairwise independent but not mutually independent.
