.. admonition:: Question 1.1  (2 pts)
   :class: note

   An automotive engineer is studying how tire compound (soft, medium, hard) and suspension
   stiffness (low, high) affect braking distance. Because vehicle weight is known to influence
   braking performance, the engineer groups 30 test vehicles into five blocks based on weight
   class (subcompact, compact, midsize, full-size, SUV). Within each weight class, the 6
   vehicles are randomly assigned so that each of the six treatment combinations is applied to
   exactly one vehicle. The braking distance (in meters) from 100 km/h is recorded.

   **True or False:** In this randomized block design, the blocks are defined by all
   combinations of tire compound, suspension stiffness, and vehicle weight class, resulting in
   30 total blocks.

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Answer: FALSE**

      The blocks are defined **only** by the weight class variable, giving **5 blocks** (one
      per weight class). The tire compound and suspension stiffness are the **treatment
      factors**, not blocking variables. Within each weight class block, the six treatment
      combinations (3 tire compounds × 2 suspension stiffnesses) are randomly assigned to the
      six vehicles. The 30 "cells" are experimental units, not blocks.

.. admonition:: Question 1.2  (2 pts)
   :class: note

   In the context of hypothesis testing and confidence intervals, let :math:`C` denote the
   confidence level of an interval, :math:`\alpha` denote the complementary significance
   level (so that :math:`C + \alpha = 1`), and :math:`\beta` denote the probability of a
   Type II error.

   **True or False:** Since :math:`C + \alpha = 1` and power :math:`+ \beta = 1`, it follows
   that :math:`C = \text{power}`.

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Answer: FALSE**

      Although :math:`C = 1 - \alpha` and power :math:`= 1 - \beta`, that does **not** imply
      :math:`C = \text{power}` because :math:`\alpha \neq \beta` in general.

      - :math:`\alpha` is the probability of rejecting :math:`H_0` when :math:`H_0` is
        **true** (Type I error rate).
      - :math:`\beta` is the probability of failing to reject :math:`H_0` when :math:`H_a`
        is **true** (Type II error rate).

      These are probabilities computed under different distributions and are generally
      unequal, so :math:`1 - \alpha \neq 1 - \beta`.

.. admonition:: Question 1.3  (2 pts)
   :class: note

   An aerospace engineer constructs a confidence interval for the mean drag coefficient
   :math:`\mu` of a new wing design from a random sample of fixed size :math:`n`.

   **True or False:** The confidence level :math:`C` is one of the factors that determines
   the width of the confidence interval.

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Answer: TRUE**

      The width of a confidence interval is :math:`2 \times t^*_{\alpha/2,\,\text{df}} \cdot
      s / \sqrt{n}` (or with :math:`z^*` for known :math:`\sigma`). The confidence level
      :math:`C = 1 - \alpha` determines the critical value :math:`t^*_{\alpha/2}` (or
      :math:`z^*_{\alpha/2}`): a higher confidence level produces a larger critical value and
      therefore a wider interval. Along with the sample size :math:`n` and the variability
      :math:`s` (or :math:`\sigma`), the confidence level is indeed one of the factors that
      determines the width.

.. admonition:: Question 1.4  (2 pts)
   :class: note

   A quality engineer is planning a hypothesis test to detect whether the mean diameter of
   manufactured bolts has shifted from the specification value :math:`\mu_0 = 10.00` mm. She
   is evaluating the statistical power of the test at a specific alternative value
   :math:`\mu_a` that belongs to the alternative hypothesis.

   **True or False:** The statistical power of a hypothesis test may decrease when the sample
   size :math:`n` is increased.

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Answer: FALSE**

      Increasing :math:`n` decreases the standard error :math:`\sigma / \sqrt{n}`, which
      tightens the sampling distribution of :math:`\bar{X}` under the alternative
      :math:`\mu_a`. With a tighter distribution centered at :math:`\mu_a`, more of the
      distribution falls in the rejection region, so power **increases** (or, at minimum,
      does not decrease). There is no mechanism by which a larger sample size reduces statistical
      power for a fixed :math:`\alpha`, fixed :math:`\sigma`, and fixed :math:`\mu_a`.

.. admonition:: Question 1.5  (2 pts)
   :class: note

   A mechanical engineer tests the fatigue life of :math:`n = 40` aluminum alloy specimens
   and constructs a 95% confidence interval for the true mean fatigue life :math:`\mu` (in
   cycles). The computed interval is :math:`(22.5,\; 22.7)`.

   **True or False:** It is incorrect to say that :math:`22.5 \leq \mu \leq 22.7` with 0.95
   probability, since the inequality does not involve any random variables.

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Answer: TRUE**

      Once the interval has been computed from the observed data, the endpoints 22.5 and 22.7
      are **fixed numbers**, and :math:`\mu` is also a fixed (unknown) constant. There is no
      randomness left — :math:`\mu` is either inside the interval or it is not. The "95%
      confidence" refers to the **long-run procedure**: if we repeated the sampling and
      interval construction many times, 95% of the resulting intervals would capture
      :math:`\mu`. Assigning 0.95 probability to this particular realized interval is a
      common misinterpretation.

.. admonition:: Question 1.6  (2 pts)
   :class: note

   A researcher wishes to test :math:`H_0\!: \mu \leq \mu_0` versus
   :math:`H_a\!: \mu > \mu_0` at significance level :math:`\alpha = 0.01`. Before conducting
   the one-sided test, she constructs a 99% **two-sided** confidence interval for :math:`\mu`
   from the same data and observes that :math:`\mu_0` falls inside the interval.

   **True or False:** The researcher can conclude that the one-sided test at
   :math:`\alpha = 0.01` will fail to reject :math:`H_0`.

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Answer: FALSE**

      A 99% **two-sided** CI corresponds to a **two-sided** test at :math:`\alpha = 0.01`.
      If :math:`\mu_0` falls inside the two-sided CI, the two-sided test fails to reject —
      but that does not determine the outcome of the **one-sided** test.

      The one-sided upper test at :math:`\alpha = 0.01` uses a critical value
      :math:`z_{0.01}` (or :math:`t_{0.01}`), which is **less extreme** than the two-sided
      critical value :math:`z_{0.005}` (or :math:`t_{0.005}`). It is therefore possible for
      the sample mean to exceed :math:`\mu_0` by enough to reject the one-sided test while
      still falling within the wider two-sided interval. The correct duality is: a one-sided
      test at :math:`\alpha` corresponds to a one-sided **confidence bound** at
      :math:`C = 1 - \alpha`, not to a two-sided CI.

.. admonition:: Question 2.1  (3 pts)
   :class: note

   A random sample of size :math:`n` is drawn from a population with mean :math:`\mu` and
   finite standard deviation :math:`\sigma`. The population distribution is heavily
   right-skewed. Which of the following statements is **FALSE**?

   | (A) Regardless of sample size, :math:`E[\bar{X}] = \mu`.
   | (B) For sufficiently large :math:`n`, the sampling distribution of :math:`\bar{X}` is approximately normal.
   | (C) As the sample size :math:`n` becomes sufficiently large, the observations :math:`X_1, X_2, \ldots, X_n` become approximately normal.
   | (D) The standard deviation of :math:`\bar{X}` decreases as :math:`n` increases, regardless of whether :math:`\bar{X}` is approximately normal.
   | (E) A larger sample size :math:`n` is needed for the CLT to provide a good approximation here than would be needed if the population were only mildly skewed.

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Answer: (C)**

      The Central Limit Theorem applies to the **sampling distribution of**
      :math:`\bar{X}`, not to the individual observations. Regardless of how large the
      sample size is, each observation :math:`X_i` is still drawn from the same heavily
      right-skewed population — increasing :math:`n` does not change the distribution of the
      individual :math:`X_i`'s.

      The other statements are all true:

      - (A) :math:`E[\bar{X}] = \mu` always holds by the linearity of expectation.
      - (B) The CLT guarantees approximate normality of :math:`\bar{X}` for large :math:`n`.
      - (D) :math:`\text{SD}(\bar{X}) = \sigma / \sqrt{n}` decreases as :math:`n` increases.
      - (E) More extreme skewness requires a larger :math:`n` for the CLT approximation to
        be adequate.

.. admonition:: Question 2.2  (3 pts)
   :class: note

   A Purdue scouting intern for the Indianapolis Colts is evaluating 40-yard dash times from
   :math:`n = 9` prospective NFL running backs at the 2026 combine. Historical records
   indicate that 40-yard dash times for running backs are normally distributed with a known
   population standard deviation of :math:`\sigma = 0.0853` seconds. The intern's sample
   yields :math:`\bar{x} = 4.4639` seconds. To establish the slowest acceptable mean sprint
   time for recruitment, the intern constructs a one-sided upper confidence bound at
   :math:`\alpha = 0.03`. Which R expression gives the correct critical value?

   | (A) ``qnorm(0.03, lower.tail = FALSE)``
   | (B) ``qt(0.03, df = 8, lower.tail = FALSE)``
   | (C) ``qnorm(0.03/2, lower.tail = FALSE)``
   | (D) ``qt(0.03/2, df = 8, lower.tail = FALSE)``
   | (E) None of the above

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Answer: (A)**

      Two key features determine the correct R expression:

      1. **z or t?** The population standard deviation :math:`\sigma = 0.0853` is **known**,
         so we use the **normal (z)** distribution, not the t-distribution. This eliminates
         (B) and (D).

      2. **Full** :math:`\alpha` **or** :math:`\alpha/2`? A **one-sided** confidence bound
         places the entire :math:`\alpha` in one tail, so we need
         :math:`z_\alpha = z_{0.03}`, not :math:`z_{\alpha/2}`. This eliminates (C).

      Therefore, ``qnorm(0.03, lower.tail = FALSE)`` is correct.

.. admonition:: Question 2.3  (3 pts)
   :class: note

   A biomedical engineer wants to compare three physical therapy protocols for post-surgical
   knee recovery. She recruits 60 patients and knows from prior studies that age strongly
   affects recovery outcomes. She divides patients into four age groups (18–30, 31–45, 46–60,
   61+), and within each age group, randomly assigns an equal number of patients to each of
   the three protocols. Recovery is measured by range of motion (in degrees) at 8 weeks. Which
   of the following statements is **FALSE**?

   | (A) The experimental design is a Randomized Block Design, with age group as the blocking variable.
   | (B) There are three treatments and four blocks in this experiment.
   | (C) The purpose of blocking by age is to reduce the variability arising from age differences, making it easier to detect treatment effects.
   | (D) The blocking ensures that the randomization of patients to protocols is no longer necessary within each age group.
   | (E) If age had no effect on recovery, a Completely Randomized Design would have been equally effective.

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Answer: (D)**

      Blocking does **not** replace randomization — it **supplements** it. Within each age
      block, patients are still **randomly assigned** to the three protocols. Randomization
      within blocks is essential to ensure unbiased treatment comparisons and to guard against
      lurking variables other than the blocking factor.

      The other statements are all correct:

      - (A) This is indeed an RBD with age as the blocking variable.
      - (B) There are 3 treatments (the three protocols) and 4 blocks (the four age groups).
      - (C) Blocking reduces the influence of the blocked variable (age) on unexplained
        variability, increasing the ability to detect treatment effects.
      - (E) If the blocking variable has no effect on the response, blocking adds no benefit,
        and a CRD would perform equally well (and may even be slightly better due to the
        loss of degrees of freedom from blocking).

.. admonition:: Question 2.4  (3 pts)
   :class: note

   The table below summarizes the properties of two independent populations A and B.

   .. list-table::
      :header-rows: 1
      :widths: 30 35 35

      * -
        - Population A
        - Population B
      * - Distribution family
        - Normal
        - Normal
      * - Mean
        - :math:`\mu_A = \mu_B = \mu`
        - :math:`\mu_A = \mu_B = \mu`
      * - Standard Deviation
        - :math:`\sigma_A = 4.5`
        - :math:`\sigma_B = 3.9`
      * - Sample size
        - :math:`n_A = 44`
        - :math:`n_B = 52`

   Which of the following statements about the sampling distributions of :math:`\bar{X}_A`
   and :math:`\bar{X}_B` is **FALSE**?

   | (A) :math:`P(\bar{X}_A = 4.5) = P(\bar{X}_B = 3.9)`
   | (B) The pdf of :math:`\bar{X}_A` and :math:`\bar{X}_B` have the same value when evaluated at :math:`\mu`. That is, :math:`f_{\bar{X}_A}(\mu) = f_{\bar{X}_B}(\mu)`.
   | (C) :math:`P(\bar{X}_A \leq \mu - 1) > P(\bar{X}_B \leq \mu - 1)`
   | (D) :math:`P\!\left(\bar{X}_A > \mu + \frac{4.5}{\sqrt{44}}\right) = P\!\left(\bar{X}_B > \mu + \frac{3.9}{\sqrt{52}}\right)`

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Answer: (B)**

      Both :math:`\bar{X}_A` and :math:`\bar{X}_B` are continuous random variables (normal),
      so their distributions have the following properties:

      - **(A) TRUE.** For any continuous random variable,
        :math:`P(\bar{X} = c) = 0` for every constant :math:`c`. So both sides equal 0.

      - **(B) FALSE.** The height of the normal pdf at the mean is
        :math:`f(\mu) = \dfrac{1}{\sigma_{\bar{X}}\sqrt{2\pi}}`, which depends on the
        standard error. We have
        :math:`\sigma_{\bar{X}_A} = 4.5/\sqrt{44} \approx 0.6784` and
        :math:`\sigma_{\bar{X}_B} = 3.9/\sqrt{52} \approx 0.5408`. Since the standard errors
        are unequal, the pdf heights at :math:`\mu` are unequal:
        :math:`f_{\bar{X}_A}(\mu) \approx 0.5881 \neq 0.7376 \approx f_{\bar{X}_B}(\mu)`.

      - **(C) TRUE.** :math:`\bar{X}_A` has a larger standard error, so its distribution is
        more spread out and places more probability in the tails. Therefore
        :math:`P(\bar{X}_A \leq \mu - 1) > P(\bar{X}_B \leq \mu - 1)`.

      - **(D) TRUE.** Each probability standardizes to
        :math:`P(Z > 1)` because :math:`\mu + \sigma_{\bar{X}} / \sigma_{\bar{X}} = \mu + 1`
        standard error. Specifically,
        :math:`P\!\left(\bar{X}_A > \mu + \sigma_A/\sqrt{n_A}\right) = P(Z > 1)` and
        :math:`P\!\left(\bar{X}_B > \mu + \sigma_B/\sqrt{n_B}\right) = P(Z > 1)`.

.. admonition:: Question 2.5  (3 pts)
   :class: note

   An industrial engineer tests whether a process improvement has increased the mean
   production rate above the current standard of :math:`\mu_0 = 200` units/hour. The
   population standard deviation is known to be :math:`\sigma = 18` units/hour. She collects
   a sample of :math:`n = 36` observations and conducts an upper-tailed :math:`z`-test at
   :math:`\alpha = 0.05`. The engineer wants to know the probability that this test will
   correctly detect an increase if the true mean has shifted to :math:`\mu_a = 206`
   units/hour.

   The following R outputs are provided:

   .. code-block:: r

      > qnorm(0.05, lower.tail = FALSE)
      [1] 1.644854
      > qnorm(0.025, lower.tail = FALSE)
      [1] 1.959964

   | (A) ``pnorm(204.9346, mean = 206, sd = 3, lower.tail = FALSE)``
   | (B) ``pnorm(204.9346, mean = 206, sd = 18, lower.tail = FALSE)``
   | (C) ``pnorm(205.8799, mean = 206, sd = 3, lower.tail = FALSE)``
   | (D) ``pnorm(204.9346, mean = 200, sd = 3, lower.tail = FALSE)``
   | (E) ``pnorm(204.9346, mean = 206, sd = 3, lower.tail = TRUE)``

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Answer: (A)**

      **Step 1 — Find the rejection boundary.** This is a one-sided upper-tailed test at
      :math:`\alpha = 0.05`, so the critical value is
      :math:`z_{0.05} = 1.644854` (not :math:`z_{0.025}`). The rejection boundary in the
      original scale is:

      .. math::

         \bar{x}_\text{crit} = \mu_0 + z_\alpha \cdot \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}
         = 200 + 1.644854 \times \frac{18}{\sqrt{36}}
         = 200 + 1.644854 \times 3 = 204.9346

      **Step 2 — Compute the power.** Power is the probability of rejecting :math:`H_0`
      when :math:`\mu = \mu_a = 206`:

      .. math::

         \text{Power} = P(\bar{X} > 204.9346 \mid \mu = 206)

      Under :math:`\mu_a = 206`, :math:`\bar{X} \sim N(206,\; 3^2)` where
      :math:`\sigma/\sqrt{n} = 18/\sqrt{36} = 3`. In R:

      ``pnorm(204.9346, mean = 206, sd = 3, lower.tail = FALSE)``

      The other options are incorrect because:

      - (B) uses :math:`\sigma = 18` instead of the standard error
        :math:`\sigma/\sqrt{n} = 3`.
      - (C) uses the wrong cutoff (based on :math:`z_{0.025}` for a two-sided test).
      - (D) centers at :math:`\mu_0 = 200` instead of the alternative :math:`\mu_a = 206`.
      - (E) computes the lower tail, which gives :math:`\beta` (the probability of failing
        to reject), not the power.

Problem 3  (26 points) — AirBuds Battery Life
-----------------------------------------------

.. admonition:: Problem 3 Setup
   :class: important

   PineApple is assessing the battery life consistency of their next-generation AirBuds. It
   is known that the battery life of a new left AirBud follows a fairly symmetric distribution
   with a true mean operation time of :math:`\mu_L = 540` minutes and a standard deviation of
   :math:`\sigma_L = 81` minutes.

   The company will randomly select a batch of :math:`n_L = 53` left AirBuds from their
   manufacturing lines and record their battery lifetimes while playing the same audio on
   repeat. Let :math:`\bar{X}_L` denote the random variable representing the average battery
   life of such a randomly selected batch.

.. admonition:: Question 3a  (5 pts)
   :class: note

   State the approximate distribution of :math:`\bar{X}_L`. Include the name of its
   distribution family and its parameters (mean and standard error) in both symbolic and
   numerical forms.

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      .. math::

         \bar{X}_L \;\stackrel{\text{approx}}{\sim}\;
         \text{Normal}\!\left(\mu_L = 540,\;\;
         \sigma_{\bar{X}_L} = \frac{81}{\sqrt{53}} \approx 11.1262\right)

      - **Mean:** :math:`\mu_{\bar{X}_L} = \mu_L = 540` minutes.
      - **Standard error:** :math:`\sigma_{\bar{X}_L} = \sigma_L / \sqrt{n_L}
        = 81 / \sqrt{53} \approx 11.1262` minutes.

.. admonition:: Question 3b  (3 pts)
   :class: note

   What important theorem justifies your approximation in Part (a)?

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      The **Central Limit Theorem (CLT)**. The original population is described as fairly
      symmetric and the sample size :math:`n_L = 53` is reasonably large. By the CLT, the
      sampling distribution of :math:`\bar{X}_L` is approximately normal regardless of the
      shape of the population distribution, provided :math:`n` is sufficiently large and the
      population has finite mean and variance.

.. admonition:: R Output for Parts (c) and (d)
   :class: tip

   The following R calculations are provided. Default behavior is ``lower.tail = TRUE``.

   .. code-block:: r

      > pnorm(-2.2469)
      [1] 0.0123
      > pnorm(-2.1424)
      [1] 0.0161
      > pnorm(-1.8363)
      [1] 0.0332
      > pnorm(-1.7976)
      [1] 0.0361
      > pnorm(0.6121)
      [1] 0.7298
      > pnorm(0.8988)
      [1] 0.8157

.. admonition:: Question 3c  (6 pts)
   :class: note

   What is the probability that the sample mean lifetime falls between 520 and 550 minutes?

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      Standardize both endpoints using :math:`\sigma_{\bar{X}_L} = 81/\sqrt{53}`:

      .. math::

         P(520 < \bar{X}_L < 550)
         = P\!\left(\frac{520 - 540}{81/\sqrt{53}} < Z < \frac{550 - 540}{81/\sqrt{53}}\right)

      .. math::

         = P(-1.7976 < Z < 0.8988)

      .. math::

         = \Phi(0.8988) - \Phi(-1.7976) = 0.8157 - 0.0361 = \boxed{0.7796}

.. admonition:: Question 3d  (12 pts)
   :class: note

   Suppose the testing software is programmed to automatically discard any batch of 53
   AirBuds if their sample mean is lower than 515 minutes, thus a batch is successfully
   retained and recorded if their sample mean is at least 515 minutes. Given that a batch is
   successfully recorded, what is the probability that its sample mean falls between 520 and
   550 minutes?

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      This is a **conditional probability** problem. We need:

      .. math::

         P(520 < \bar{X}_L < 550 \mid \bar{X}_L \geq 515)
         = \frac{P(520 < \bar{X}_L < 550)}{P(\bar{X}_L \geq 515)}

      The numerator was computed in part (c). Note that the event
      :math:`520 < \bar{X}_L < 550` is a subset of :math:`\bar{X}_L \geq 515`, so the
      intersection simplifies to just the numerator event.

      **Numerator:** :math:`P(520 < \bar{X}_L < 550) = 0.7796` (from part c).

      **Denominator:** Standardize 515:

      .. math::

         P(\bar{X}_L \geq 515) = P\!\left(Z \geq \frac{515 - 540}{81/\sqrt{53}}\right)
         = P(Z \geq -2.2469) = 1 - \Phi(-2.2469) = 1 - 0.0123 = 0.9877

      **Conditional probability:**

      .. math::

         \frac{0.7796}{0.9877} = \boxed{0.7893}

Problem 4  (26 points) — eBay Broken Laser Pointer
-----------------------------------------------------

.. admonition:: Problem 4 Setup
   :class: important

   In an effort to consolidate the conceptual understanding of interval estimation (confidence
   intervals and bounds) in STAT 350, an interactive trivia-based classroom activity was
   implemented during the Fall semester. Students were asked to estimate the sale price (in US
   dollars) of the first item ever sold on eBay, which was a broken laser pointer. A random
   sample of :math:`n = 16` student point estimates yielded a sample mean of
   :math:`\bar{x} = 13.22` and a sample standard deviation of :math:`s = 4.3`. Typically,
   this type of guessing data tends to be mildly skewed due to psychological anchoring in
   human decision-making.

.. admonition:: Question 4a  (2 pts)
   :class: note

   Does the information provided above justify the construction of standard confidence
   intervals or bounds? Give one statistical justification for your answer.

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      Multiple valid responses were accepted:

      **Yes, justified:** The sample is described as a random sample, satisfying the
      independence assumption. Although the data are mildly skewed and :math:`n = 16` is a
      small-to-moderate sample size, the :math:`t`-procedure is robust to mild departures
      from normality. With only mild skewness, the sampling distribution of :math:`\bar{X}`
      should be approximately normal even at this moderate sample size, so constructing a
      :math:`t`-based confidence interval or bound is reasonable.

      **No (independence concern):** The estimates were collected during a classroom
      activity, so students may have influenced each other's guesses through verbal or
      nonverbal cues. Even though the problem states a random sample, the shared environment
      may violate the independence assumption required for :math:`t`-procedures.

      **No (normality concern):** Independence is satisfied by the random sampling, but the
      population is stated to be mildly skewed and :math:`n = 16` may not be large enough for
      the CLT to guarantee an approximately normal sampling distribution.

.. admonition:: Question 4b  Setup
   :class: important

   Suppose we assume the conditions are met to conduct statistical inference. The actual
   selling price of the broken laser pointer was $14.83. Suspecting that students tend to
   underestimate, the instructor would like to establish, with 90% confidence, a confidence
   **upper bound** for the true mean of all student guesses across STAT 350 during the fall
   semester.

.. admonition:: Question 4b.i  (3 pts)
   :class: note

   Which R output below provides the correct critical value for this computation?

   | (A) ``qnorm(0.05, lower.tail = FALSE)`` → 1.644854
   | (B) ``qt(0.1, df = 15, lower.tail = FALSE)`` → 1.340606
   | (C) ``qnorm(0.1, lower.tail = FALSE)`` → 1.281552
   | (D) ``qt(0.05, df = 15, lower.tail = FALSE)`` → 1.75305

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Answer: (B)**

      Two key features determine the correct critical value:

      1. **z or t?** The population standard deviation :math:`\sigma` is **unknown** (only
         :math:`s = 4.3` is available), so we must use the :math:`t`-distribution with
         :math:`\text{df} = n - 1 = 15`. This eliminates (A) and (C).

      2. **Full** :math:`\alpha` **or** :math:`\alpha/2`? A one-sided confidence **bound**
         places the entire :math:`\alpha = 0.10` in one tail, so we need
         :math:`t_{0.10,\,15}`, not :math:`t_{0.05,\,15}`. This eliminates (D).

      Therefore, ``qt(0.1, df = 15, lower.tail = FALSE)`` = 1.340606 is correct.

.. admonition:: Question 4b.ii  (5 pts)
   :class: note

   Compute and interpret the 90% confidence upper bound in the context of the problem.

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      The 90% confidence upper bound has the form:

      .. math::

         \left(-\infty,\;\; \bar{x} + t_{\alpha,\,\text{df}} \cdot \frac{s}{\sqrt{n}}\right)

      Substituting:

      .. math::

         \left(-\infty,\;\; 13.22 + 1.340606 \times \frac{4.3}{\sqrt{16}}\right)
         = \left(-\infty,\;\; 13.22 + 1.340606 \times 1.075\right)
         = \left(-\infty,\;\; 14.6612\right)

      **Interpretation:** We are 90% confident that the true mean :math:`\mu` of all student
      guesses regarding the value at which the broken laser pointer was sold across STAT 350
      during the fall semester is at most **$14.6612**.

.. admonition:: Question 4c  (2 pts)
   :class: note

   In general, :math:`t`-intervals/bounds are [______] their corresponding
   :math:`z`-intervals/bounds (assuming the same confidence level, standard error, and sample
   size). Select the correct option.

   | (A) narrower than
   | (B) wider than
   | (C) the same width as

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Answer: (B) wider than**

      The :math:`t`-distribution has heavier tails than the standard normal, so for any given
      confidence level, the :math:`t` critical value :math:`t^*_{\alpha/2,\,\text{df}}`
      exceeds the corresponding :math:`z` critical value :math:`z^*_{\alpha/2}`. This
      produces a wider interval or bound, reflecting the additional uncertainty from
      estimating :math:`\sigma` with :math:`s`.

.. admonition:: Question 4d  Setup
   :class: important

   Recently, a digital marketing analyst claimed that the true mean price for vintage
   RadioShack laser pointers is $225. The prices of these vintage pointers are known to be
   normally distributed with a population variance of :math:`\sigma^2 = 64`. A Purdue
   engineering historian believes the true mean price is different. He collects a random sample
   of :math:`n = 12` prices from e-commerce platforms, yielding a sample mean of
   :math:`\bar{x} = 210` and a sample standard deviation of :math:`s = 15`. Use a
   significance level :math:`\alpha = 0.02` for all inference calculations below.

.. admonition:: Question 4d.i  (4 pts)
   :class: note

   Provide the first two steps of the four-step hypothesis testing procedure.

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Step 1 — Parameter of interest:**

      Let :math:`\mu` represent the true mean price (in US dollars) for vintage RadioShack
      laser pointers.

      **Step 2 — Hypotheses:**

      .. math::

         H_0: \mu = 225

      .. math::

         H_a: \mu \neq 225

      In words: the null hypothesis states that the true mean price is $225 (as claimed by
      the analyst), and the alternative states that the true mean price differs from $225.

.. admonition:: Question 4d.ii  (3 pts)
   :class: note

   Compute the appropriate test statistic.

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      Since the population variance :math:`\sigma^2 = 64` is **known**
      (:math:`\sigma = 8`), we use the :math:`z`-test statistic — even though
      :math:`s = 15` is provided, we use :math:`\sigma` because it is given:

      .. math::

         z_\text{TS} = \frac{\bar{x} - \mu_0}{\sigma / \sqrt{n}}
         = \frac{210 - 225}{8 / \sqrt{12}}
         = \frac{-15}{2.3094}
         = \boxed{-6.4952}

.. admonition:: Question 4d.iii  (3 pts)
   :class: note

   Which of the following R outputs is appropriate for computing the :math:`p`-value for this
   test?

   | (A) ``2*pt(abs(t_ts), df=11, lower.tail=FALSE)`` → 0.005294732
   | (B) ``2*pnorm(abs(z_ts), lower.tail=FALSE)`` → 8.292839e-11
   | (C) ``pnorm(abs(z_ts), lower.tail=FALSE)`` → 4.14642e-11
   | (D) ``pt(abs(t_ts), df=11, lower.tail=FALSE)`` → 0.002647366

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Answer: (B)**

      Since :math:`\sigma` is known, the test statistic is a :math:`z`-statistic, so we need
      ``pnorm``, not ``pt``. This eliminates (A) and (D). Because the test is **two-sided**
      (:math:`H_a: \mu \neq 225`), we double the one-tail probability. This eliminates (C),
      which computes only one tail.

      Therefore: ``2*pnorm(abs(z_ts), lower.tail=FALSE)`` = :math:`8.2928 \times 10^{-11}`

.. admonition:: Question 4d.iv  (4 pts)
   :class: note

   State your decision and provide a conclusion in the context of the problem.

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Decision:** Since the :math:`p`-value :math:`= 8.2928 \times 10^{-11}` is less than
      :math:`\alpha = 0.02`, we **reject** :math:`H_0`.

      **Conclusion:** The data give strong support
      (:math:`p`-value :math:`= 8.2928 \times 10^{-11}`) to the claim that the true mean
      price for vintage RadioShack laser pointers differs from the $225 claimed by the
      analyst.

Problem 5  (26 points) — Soybean Yield: Soil Amendments
---------------------------------------------------------

.. admonition:: Problem 5 Setup
   :class: important

   Researchers at a Purdue agricultural extension are evaluating two soil amendments, the
   standard treatment (S) and a new organic treatment (N), to determine whether the new
   treatment increases soybean yield (measured in bushels per acre). They selected 18 farm
   plots in Tippecanoe County. Each plot is split in half: one half receives Amendment S and
   the other half receives Amendment N. After the growing season, soybean yield is recorded
   for each half-plot.

   The researchers calculate the difference for each plot: :math:`D = N - S`. They have
   verified that the distribution of these differences is approximately normal.

   .. list-table::
      :header-rows: 1
      :widths: 25 25 25 25

      * -
        - Amendment N
        - Amendment S
        - :math:`D = N - S`
      * - :math:`n`
        - 18
        - 18
        - 18
      * - Sample Mean
        - 54.8
        - 51.2
        - 3.6
      * - Sample Std Dev
        - 7.3
        - 6.9
        - 5.4

.. admonition:: Question 5a  (2 pts)
   :class: note

   Which testing procedure is appropriate for this experiment?

   | (A) Two-sample independent :math:`t`-test
   | (B) Two-sample paired :math:`t`-test

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Answer: (B) Two-sample paired** :math:`t`-**test**

.. admonition:: Question 5b  (6 pts)
   :class: note

   Explain what specific characteristic(s) in the experimental design motivated your choice
   of testing procedure in part (a).

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      Each of the 18 farm plots is **split in half**, with one half receiving Amendment N and
      the other half receiving Amendment S. The two measurements from each plot share the
      **same underlying conditions** — identical soil composition, moisture level, sunlight
      exposure, and other plot-specific environmental factors — creating a **natural pairing**.

      Because the two observations within each plot are not independent of each other (they
      share a common plot), a paired :math:`t`-test is appropriate. This design reduces the
      effect of plot-to-plot variability by analyzing the **within-plot differences**
      :math:`D = N - S` rather than comparing the two groups as if they were independent.

.. admonition:: Question 5c  (4 pts)
   :class: note

   Provide the first two steps of the four-step hypothesis testing procedure. Use a
   significance level of :math:`\alpha = 0.03`.

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Step 1 — Parameter of interest:**

      Let :math:`\mu_D` represent the true mean difference in soybean yield (in bushels per
      acre) between land treated with the new organic treatment (N) and the standard treatment
      (S), where :math:`D = N - S`.

      **Step 2 — Hypotheses:**

      Since the research question asks whether the new treatment **increases** yield, this is
      a one-sided (upper-tailed) test:

      .. math::

         H_0: \mu_D \leq 0 \qquad (\text{or equivalently } H_0: \mu_D = 0)

      .. math::

         H_a: \mu_D > 0

      In words: the null states that the new organic treatment does not increase soybean
      yield compared to the standard; the alternative states that it does increase yield.

      .. admonition:: Grading Note
         :class: info

         A two-sided formulation (:math:`H_0: \mu_D = 0` vs. :math:`H_a: \mu_D \neq 0`) was
         also accepted, with corresponding adjustments to the p-value computation in part (e).

.. admonition:: Question 5d  (6 pts)
   :class: note

   Calculate the test statistic for this experiment. Show your work.

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      Using the paired differences :math:`D = N - S` with :math:`\bar{d} = 3.6`,
      :math:`s_D = 5.4`, and :math:`n_D = 18`:

      .. math::

         t_\text{TS} = \frac{\bar{d} - 0}{s_D / \sqrt{n_D}}
         = \frac{3.6}{5.4 / \sqrt{18}}
         = \frac{3.6}{1.2728}
         = \boxed{2.8284}

      Degrees of freedom: :math:`\text{df} = n_D - 1 = 17`.

.. admonition:: Question 5e  (3 pts)
   :class: note

   Select the most appropriate R command to compute the :math:`p`-value for this specific
   test.

   | (A) ``pt(test_statistic, df = 17, lower.tail = TRUE)``
   | (B) ``2*pt(abs(test_statistic), df = 17, lower.tail = FALSE)``
   | (C) ``pt(test_statistic, df = 17, lower.tail = FALSE)``
   | (D) ``pt(test_statistic, df = 34, lower.tail = FALSE)``
   | (E) ``pt(test_statistic, df = 30.27, lower.tail = FALSE)``
   | (F) ``pnorm(test_statistic, lower.tail = FALSE)``

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Answer: (C)** for the one-sided test, **or (B)** for the two-sided formulation.

      The choice must be consistent with the hypotheses stated in part (c):

      - If :math:`H_a: \mu_D > 0` (one-sided upper): use
        ``pt(test_statistic, df = 17, lower.tail = FALSE)`` → **(C)**.
      - If :math:`H_a: \mu_D \neq 0` (two-sided): use
        ``2*pt(abs(test_statistic), df = 17, lower.tail = FALSE)`` → **(B)**.

      Key points for eliminating other options:

      - (A) computes the lower tail — wrong direction for an upper-tailed test.
      - (D) uses :math:`\text{df} = 34`, which would apply to an independent two-sample
        pooled test (:math:`n_1 + n_2 - 2`), not a paired test.
      - (E) uses :math:`\text{df} = 30.27`, which suggests a Welch (unpooled) test — not
        appropriate for paired data.
      - (F) uses ``pnorm`` (normal distribution), but :math:`\sigma` is unknown, so a
        :math:`t`-distribution with :math:`\text{df} = 17` is required.

.. admonition:: Question 5f  (5 pts)
   :class: note

   The :math:`p`-value for the correct test was found to be 0.0058. Using a significance
   level of :math:`\alpha = 0.03`, state your formal decision and write a conclusion in the
   context of the problem.

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Decision:** Since the :math:`p`-value = 0.0058 is less than :math:`\alpha = 0.03`,
      we **reject** :math:`H_0`.

      **Conclusion (one-sided formulation):** The data give strong support
      (:math:`p`-value = 0.0058) to the claim that the true mean difference in soybean yield
      between land treated with the new organic treatment (N) and the standard treatment (S)
      is positive, indicating that the new organic treatment has a beneficial effect on crop
      yield.

      **Conclusion (two-sided formulation):** The data give strong support
      (:math:`p`-value = 0.0058) to the claim that the true mean difference in soybean yield
      between the new organic treatment and the standard treatment differs from zero.

      .. admonition:: Grading Note
         :class: info

         The conclusion must be consistent with the hypothesis formulation chosen in
         part (c). If a one-sided alternative was stated, the conclusion should reference
         the direction of the effect; if two-sided, it should reference a difference from
         zero.
