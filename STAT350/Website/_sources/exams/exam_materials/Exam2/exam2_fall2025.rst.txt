Problem 1 — True/False  (12 points, 2 points each)
----------------------------------------------------

.. admonition:: Question 1.1  (2 pts)
   :class: note

   Assume that :math:`X_1, X_2, \cdots, X_n` is a random sample from a Cauchy
   distribution, which has an undefined expected value and variance (i.e.,
   :math:`E[X_i]` and :math:`\text{Var}(X_i)` are not finite).

   **True or False:** Performing a one-sample hypothesis test for the population mean
   (:math:`\mu`) is valid if the sample size (:math:`n`) is sufficiently large.

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Answer: FALSE**

      The Central Limit Theorem requires finite mean and variance. The Cauchy
      distribution has **neither** a finite mean nor a finite variance, so the CLT does
      not apply regardless of sample size. Without the CLT or an underlying normal
      population, the sampling distribution of :math:`\bar{X}` does not converge to a
      normal distribution, and standard z- or t-based hypothesis tests for :math:`\mu`
      are not valid. In fact, the Cauchy distribution has no well-defined population mean
      :math:`\mu` to test.

.. admonition:: Question 1.2  (2 pts)
   :class: note

   The lengths of pregnancies are normally distributed with a mean of 268 days and a
   standard deviation of 15 days.

   **True or False:** Then the probability that a randomly selected pregnant woman's
   pregnancy length is less than 265 is larger than the probability that the mean
   pregnancy length of a random sample of 40 pregnant women is less than 265.

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Answer: TRUE**

      For an individual observation :math:`X \sim N(268, 15^2)`:

      .. math::

         P(X < 265) = P\!\left(Z < \frac{265 - 268}{15}\right) = P(Z < -0.20) = 0.4207

      For the sample mean :math:`\bar{X} \sim N\!\left(268,\ (15/\sqrt{40})^2\right)`:

      .. math::

         P(\bar{X} < 265) = P\!\left(Z < \frac{265 - 268}{15/\sqrt{40}}\right)
         = P(Z < -1.265) = 0.1029

      Since :math:`0.4207 > 0.1029`, the probability for a single observation is larger
      than for the sample mean. The sample mean has much less variability
      (:math:`\sigma/\sqrt{n} = 15/\sqrt{40} \approx 2.37` vs. :math:`\sigma = 15`), so
      it is much less likely to fall far from the population mean.

.. admonition:: Question 1.3  (2 pts)
   :class: note

   In experimental design, researchers often encounter extraneous variables that may
   influence the response variable alongside the factor of interest.

   **True or False:** In a Randomized Block Design (RBD), blocks are used to control
   the factor of interest, while randomization controls extraneous variables.

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Answer: FALSE**

      The statement has the roles reversed. In an RBD, **blocks** are used to control
      **extraneous variables** (by grouping experimental units that are similar on a known
      nuisance factor), while **randomization** is used to assign the **factor of
      interest** (treatments) to units within each block. Blocks reduce the impact of
      extraneous variability; randomization ensures unbiased treatment comparison.

.. admonition:: Question 1.4  (2 pts)
   :class: note

   A researcher conducts a hypothesis test and obtains a :math:`p`-value of 0.03.

   **True or False:** This means there is a 3% probability that the null hypothesis is
   true.

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Answer: FALSE**

      The :math:`p`-value is **not** the probability that :math:`H_0` is true. The
      :math:`p`-value is the probability of observing a test statistic at least as extreme
      as the one obtained, **assuming** :math:`H_0` is true. It is a statement about the
      data given the hypothesis, not about the hypothesis given the data. The null
      hypothesis is either true or false — it does not have a probability in the classical
      (frequentist) framework.

.. admonition:: Question 1.5  (2 pts)
   :class: note

   A 95% confidence interval for the population mean :math:`\mu` is constructed from
   sample data.

   **True or False:** A sample mean :math:`\bar{x}` falls within the 95% confidence
   interval for all possible samples from the population.

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Answer: TRUE**

      A confidence interval is always constructed as
      :math:`\bar{x} \pm t^* \cdot s/\sqrt{n}`, centered at the sample mean
      :math:`\bar{x}`. Therefore, by construction, :math:`\bar{x}` is always the
      **center** of the interval and is contained within it for every possible sample.

.. admonition:: Question 1.6  (2 pts)
   :class: note

   A researcher wants to estimate the average income in a city with diverse
   neighborhoods.

   **True or False:** In a stratified random sample, the city is divided into
   neighborhoods (strata), and then a few complete neighborhoods are randomly selected
   and all residents within those neighborhoods are surveyed.

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Answer: FALSE**

      The description given is **cluster sampling**, not stratified sampling. In a
      **stratified random sample**, the population is divided into strata, and then a
      random sample is drawn **from within each stratum**. In **cluster sampling**, a few
      clusters (e.g., complete neighborhoods) are randomly selected and all units within
      the chosen clusters are surveyed. The key distinction: stratified sampling samples
      from every stratum; cluster sampling surveys all units in only a few selected
      clusters.

Problem 2 — Multiple Choice  (15 points, 3 points each)
---------------------------------------------------------

.. admonition:: Question 2.1  (3 pts)
   :class: note

   A student proposes an estimator for an unknown parameter :math:`\theta`. The
   estimator :math:`\hat{\theta}` has expected value
   :math:`E[\hat{\theta}] = \left(\dfrac{n}{n-1}\right)\theta + 5`, where :math:`n` is
   the sample size. What is the exact bias (for finite :math:`n`) and the asymptotic bias
   as :math:`n \to \infty` of this estimator?

   (A) Exact bias :math:`= 5`; Asymptotic bias :math:`= 0`

   (B) Exact bias :math:`= \left(\dfrac{1}{n-1}\right)\theta`; Asymptotic bias :math:`= 5`

   (C) Exact bias :math:`= \left(\dfrac{1}{n-1}\right)\theta + 5`; Asymptotic bias :math:`= 5`

   (D) Exact bias :math:`= \left(\dfrac{1}{n-1}\right)\theta + 5`; Asymptotic bias :math:`= 0`

   (E) Exact bias :math:`= \left(\dfrac{n}{n-1}\right)\theta + 5`; Asymptotic bias :math:`= \theta`

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Answer: (C)**

      The bias of an estimator is :math:`\text{Bias}(\hat{\theta}) = E[\hat{\theta}] - \theta`.

      **Exact bias:**

      .. math::

         \text{Bias}(\hat{\theta}) = \frac{n}{n-1}\,\theta + 5 - \theta
         = \left(\frac{n}{n-1} - 1\right)\theta + 5
         = \frac{1}{n-1}\,\theta + 5

      **Asymptotic bias** as :math:`n \to \infty`:

      .. math::

         \lim_{n \to \infty} \left(\frac{1}{n-1}\,\theta + 5\right) = 0 + 5 = 5

      The :math:`\theta/(n-1)` term vanishes but the constant additive bias of 5
      remains, so the estimator is **asymptotically biased** by 5.

.. admonition:: Question 2.2  (3 pts)
   :class: note

   Three estimators, :math:`\hat{\theta}_A`, :math:`\hat{\theta}_B`,
   :math:`\hat{\theta}_C`, are constructed for an unknown target parameter :math:`\theta`,
   and their sampling distributions are visualized in the graph below. Which of the
   following statements is **TRUE** about the estimators?

   .. figure:: https://yjjpfnblgtrogqvcjaon.supabase.co/storage/v1/object/public/stat-350-assets/images/Exams/Exam2/FALL2025/image1.png
      :alt: Graph showing sampling distributions of three estimators A, B, and C for a target parameter theta marked by a dashed vertical line. Curve A (red, wide and flat) is centered well to the left of theta, indicating high variance and negative bias. Curve B (green, tall and narrow) is centered to the right of theta, indicating low variance but positive bias. Curve C (blue, moderate width) is centered at theta, indicating unbiased with moderate variance.
      :align: center
      :width: 75%

   (A) :math:`\hat{\theta}_B` is preferred over :math:`\hat{\theta}_A` because
   :math:`\hat{\theta}_B` has a smaller variance.

   (B) :math:`\hat{\theta}_A` is preferred over :math:`\hat{\theta}_C` if
   :math:`\hat{\theta}_A` has a smaller bias.

   (C) :math:`\hat{\theta}_C` is preferred over :math:`\hat{\theta}_A` even if
   :math:`\hat{\theta}_A` has a smaller bias.

   (D) On repeated samples, :math:`\hat{\theta}_A` values hardly vary around the true
   parameter.

   (E) The best estimate can be determined only after obtaining realized values.

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Answer: (C)**

      From the graph: Curve A (red) is wide and flat — **high variance, biased**. Curve
      B (green) is tall and narrow but shifted right of :math:`\theta` — **low variance,
      biased**. Curve C (blue) is moderate width and centered at :math:`\theta` —
      **unbiased, moderate variance**.

      - **(A) FALSE.** :math:`\hat{\theta}_B` does have smaller variance than
        :math:`\hat{\theta}_A`, but preference cannot be determined by variance alone
        — the bias matters too. :math:`\hat{\theta}_B` is biased.
      - **(B) FALSE.** Even if :math:`\hat{\theta}_A` had smaller bias than
        :math:`\hat{\theta}_C`, its extremely high variance (flat, wide curve) would
        likely give it a larger MSE than :math:`\hat{\theta}_C`.
      - **(C) TRUE.** :math:`\hat{\theta}_C` is unbiased and has moderate variance.
        Even if :math:`\hat{\theta}_A` were somehow less biased, its very large variance
        means its MSE would likely exceed :math:`\hat{\theta}_C`'s. The MSE combines
        both bias and variance; :math:`\hat{\theta}_C`'s combination is superior.
      - **(D) FALSE.** :math:`\hat{\theta}_A` is the wide, flat curve — it has the
        **largest** variance, meaning values vary greatly around :math:`\theta` on
        repeated samples.
      - **(E) FALSE.** The best estimator is evaluated based on the properties of the
        sampling distributions (bias, variance, MSE), not after seeing individual
        realized values.

.. admonition:: Question 2.3  (3 pts)
   :class: note

   Two fertilizers are tested on different plots to compare their effects on crop yield.
   Summary statistics: :math:`n_1 = 22`, :math:`s_1 = 19.5` kg/hectare and
   :math:`n_2 = 20`, :math:`s_2 = 4.7` kg/hectare. Which statistical inference procedure
   is most appropriate for comparing mean yields?

   (A) One-sample :math:`t`-procedures

   (B) Two-sample paired :math:`t`-procedures

   (C) Pooled two-sample independent :math:`t`-procedures

   (D) Welch two-sample independent :math:`t`-procedures

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Answer: (D)**

      The two groups (fertilizer 1 vs. fertilizer 2) are tested on **different plots** —
      there is no pairing or matching, so independent-samples procedures are required.
      The sample standard deviations are :math:`s_1 = 19.5` and :math:`s_2 = 4.7`, a
      ratio of more than 4:1. This large difference in variability makes the equal-variance
      (pooled) assumption inappropriate. The **Welch two-sample independent t-procedure**
      does not require equal variances and is therefore the most appropriate choice.

.. admonition:: Question 2.4  (3 pts)
   :class: note

   A researcher wants to test the effect of a new type of feed on the weight gain of
   chickens. They have 100 chickens, but they are housed in 10 different coops (10
   chickens per coop). The researcher knows that conditions (like temperature and
   lighting) vary slightly between coops, which might affect weight gain. To account for
   this, the researcher randomly assigns 5 chickens within each coop to the new feed and
   the other 5 to the standard feed.

   Which experimental design technique is demonstrated by separating the chickens by
   coop before assigning the feed?

   (A) Completely Randomized Design |quad| (B) Randomized Block Design |quad| (C) Simple Random Sample |quad| (D) Matched Pairs Design |quad| (E) Stratified Random Sampling

   .. |quad| unicode:: U+00A0 U+00A0 U+00A0

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Answer: (B) Randomized Block Design**

      The coops serve as **blocks** — groups of experimental units (chickens) that are
      homogeneous with respect to a known extraneous variable (coop conditions: temperature
      and lighting). Within each block (coop), treatments (new feed vs. standard feed) are
      randomly assigned. This is the defining structure of a **Randomized Block Design**:
      blocking on the extraneous variable before randomizing treatments within blocks.

.. admonition:: Question 2.5  (3 pts)
   :class: note

   Chloride deposits are markers for early Mars' aqueous past with important implications
   for our understanding of Mars' climate and habitability. Purdue scientists are in the
   process of investigating high-resolution image surfaces of **33 chloride deposits**
   from the southern highlands of Mars. Researchers from a different university have
   claimed that the mean diameter of chloride deposits is 1650 m with the standard
   deviation of 779.42 m. Studies of geological features suggest that the diameters of
   natural deposits tend to follow approximately symmetric distributions. Based on the
   Central Limit Theorem and assuming the other researchers' claim correctly describes the
   population, which of the following statements is **incorrect**?

   (A) The standard deviation of the sampling distribution of :math:`\bar{X}` for Purdue
   investigations should be 779.42 m.

   (B) The mean of the sampling distribution of :math:`\bar{X}` for Purdue investigations
   is 1650 m.

   (C) The sampling distribution of :math:`\bar{X}` for Purdue investigations is
   approximately normal.

   (D) We cannot assume the population distribution of deposit diameters is exactly normal.

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Answer: (A)**

      The standard deviation of the **sampling distribution** of :math:`\bar{X}` is the
      **standard error**, not the population standard deviation:

      .. math::

         \sigma_{\bar{X}} = \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}} = \frac{779.42}{\sqrt{33}} \approx 135.68 \text{ m}

      Statement (A) incorrectly claims :math:`\sigma_{\bar{X}} = 779.42` m, which is the
      **population** standard deviation. The remaining statements are all correct:

      - **(B)** :math:`\mu_{\bar{X}} = \mu = 1650` m — always true.
      - **(C)** With :math:`n = 33` and a symmetric population, the CLT gives approximate
        normality for :math:`\bar{X}`.
      - **(D)** The population is described as approximately symmetric but not stated to
        be exactly normal, so this caveat is correct.

Problem 3  (27 points) — Coffee Roasting Machine Moisture Test
---------------------------------------------------------------

.. admonition:: Problem 3 Setup
   :class: important

   A coffee company is testing a new, faster roasting machine. The old machine roasts
   beans to a target mean moisture level of 8.0%. The company suspects the new machine
   (N) produces a different mean moisture level than the old machine (O).

   They conduct an experiment. They take 16 batches of the same type of green coffee
   bean. For each batch, they split it in half, roasting one half with the new machine and
   the other half with the old machine. The moisture level for each roasted half is
   recorded.

   The company calculates the difference for each batch:
   :math:`D = \text{Moisture}_N - \text{Moisture}_O`. The data for the 16 differences
   yields a sample mean difference of **0.25%** and a standard deviation of differences of
   **0.60%**. The researchers have verified that the distribution of differences is
   approximately normal.

.. admonition:: Question 3a  (2 pts)
   :class: note

   Which testing procedure is appropriate for this experiment?

   (A) Two-sample independent :math:`t`-test

   (B) Two-sample paired :math:`t`-test

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Answer: (B) Two-sample paired t-test**

.. admonition:: Question 3b  (4 pts)
   :class: note

   Explain what characteristic(s) in the experimental design motivated your choice of
   testing procedure in part (a).

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      Each batch is split into two halves, with each half roasted using a different
      machine. This means the two measurements within a batch are **not independent** —
      they share the same batch characteristics (bean type, initial moisture content,
      batch size, etc.). Because each pair of observations (one from the new machine, one
      from the old machine) comes from the same batch, the observations are **paired by
      batch**. The paired :math:`t`-test accounts for this within-batch dependence by
      analyzing the differences :math:`D_i` directly, thereby controlling for
      batch-to-batch variability.

.. admonition:: Question 3c  (2 pts)
   :class: note

   Provide the first two steps of the four-step hypothesis testing procedure.

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Step 1 — Parameter of interest:**

      Let :math:`\mu_D = \mu_{\text{Moisture}_N} - \mu_{\text{Moisture}_O}` represent
      the true population mean moisture difference associated with roasting from the two
      machines (New − Old).

      **Step 2 — Hypotheses:**

      .. math::

         H_0: \mu_D = 0

      .. math::

         H_a: \mu_D \neq 0

.. admonition:: Question 3d  (10 pts)
   :class: note

   Calculate the test statistic for this test. Show your work.

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      .. math::

         t_\text{TS} = \frac{\bar{D} - 0}{s_D / \sqrt{n}}
         = \frac{0.25}{0.60 / \sqrt{16}}
         = \frac{0.25}{0.60 / 4}
         = \frac{0.25}{0.15}
         = \boxed{1.6667}

      Degrees of freedom: :math:`df = n - 1 = 15`.

.. admonition:: Question 3e  (3 pts)
   :class: note

   Select the appropriate code to compute the :math:`p`-value below.

   .. code-block:: r

      # (A) pt(test_statistic, df=15, lower.tail=TRUE)
      # (B) 2*pt(abs(test_statistic), df=15, lower.tail=TRUE)
      # (C) 2*pt(abs(test_statistic), df=15, lower.tail=FALSE)
      # (D) pt(test_statistic, df=15, lower.tail=FALSE)
      # (E) pt(test_statistic, df=25.8734, lower.tail=TRUE)
      # (F) 2*pt(abs(test_statistic), df=25.8734, lower.tail=TRUE)
      # (G) 2*pt(abs(test_statistic), df=25.8734, lower.tail=FALSE)
      # (H) pt(test_statistic, df=25.8734, lower.tail=FALSE)

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Answer: (C)** ``2*pt(abs(test_statistic), df=15, lower.tail=FALSE)``

      This is a **two-sided** test (:math:`H_a: \mu_D \neq 0`). The :math:`p`-value is
      twice the upper-tail probability beyond the absolute value of the test statistic.
      The degrees of freedom for a paired t-test on :math:`n = 16` pairs is
      :math:`df = n - 1 = 15`. The Welch df of 25.8734 (options E–H) would apply to an
      independent two-sample test, which is not appropriate here.

.. admonition:: Question 3f  (6 pts)
   :class: note

   The :math:`p`-value for the correct test was found to be 0.1805. Using a significance
   level of :math:`\alpha = 0.1`, state your formal decision and write a conclusion in the
   context of the problem.

   .. admonition:: Note on p-value ⚠️
      :class: danger

      The :math:`p`-value reported in the solution key (0.1805) does not match the
      computed test statistic. With :math:`t_\text{TS} = 1.6667` and :math:`df = 15`,
      the correct :math:`p`-value is
      :math:`2 \times P(T_{15} > 1.6667) = \mathbf{0.1163}`. Both values lead to the
      same decision (fail to reject at :math:`\alpha = 0.10`), so the conclusion is
      unaffected. The solution below uses the key's reported value of 0.1805.

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Formal Decision:** :math:`p\text{-value} = 0.1805 > 0.1 = \alpha`, therefore we
      **fail to reject** :math:`H_0`.

      **Conclusion:** The data do not give support (:math:`p`-value :math:`= 0.1805`) to
      the claim that the true mean difference in moisture levels produced by roasting from
      the two machines differs from 0.

Problem 4  (28 points) — Cranberry Farm pH Test
------------------------------------------------

.. admonition:: Problem 4 Setup
   :class: important

   Jamie owns a small cranberry farm that primarily grows the Stevens variety, which is
   known for being sweeter and less tart than Early Black. Recently, he planted a small
   patch of Early Black cranberries for his daughter, who loves tart berries. After a few
   years, some regular customers have claimed that the Stevens cranberries have become
   more tart.

   Jamie wants to test whether the presence of Early Black cranberries has caused an
   increase in tartness of his cranberries. Industry standards indicate that pH
   measurements from Stevens cranberry batches have an **average pH of 2.6** with a
   **standard deviation of 0.3**. Research indicates that most people can detect a pH
   change of 0.2 (lower pH = more tart).

.. admonition:: Question 4a  (2 pts)
   :class: note

   Provide the first two steps of the four-step hypothesis testing procedure.

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Step 1 — Parameter of interest:**

      Let :math:`\mu` represent the true population mean tartness (pH) of Stevens
      cranberry batches from Jamie's farm.

      **Step 2 — Hypotheses:**

      .. math::

         H_0: \mu \geq 2.6

      .. math::

         H_a: \mu < 2.6

.. admonition:: Question 4b  (2 pts)
   :class: note

   Identify the mean and standard deviation of the sampling distribution of :math:`\bar{X}`
   under the null and alternative hypotheses to detect a pH change of 0.2. Since the
   sample size is currently unknown you may use :math:`n` to represent it.

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Under** :math:`H_0`:

      .. math::

         \bar{X} \sim N\!\left(\mu_0 = 2.6,\ \sigma_{\bar{X}} = \frac{0.3}{\sqrt{n}}\right)

      **Under** :math:`H_a` (to detect a change of 0.2 toward more tartness):

      .. math::

         \bar{X} \sim N\!\left(\mu_a = 2.4,\ \sigma_{\bar{X}} = \frac{0.3}{\sqrt{n}}\right)

.. admonition:: Question 4c  (10 pts)
   :class: note

   Calculate the minimum sample size required to achieve **90% statistical power** for
   detecting a pH difference of 0.2 in the direction that would indicate increased
   tartness at :math:`\alpha = 0.04`. Show your work and clearly identify both: (i) the
   critical :math:`z`-value for your significance level, and (ii) the critical
   :math:`z`-value for your desired power.

   .. code-block:: r

      qnorm(0.01, lower.tail = FALSE)  # 2.326348
      qnorm(0.02, lower.tail = FALSE)  # 2.053749
      qnorm(0.04, lower.tail = FALSE)  # 1.750686
      qnorm(0.05, lower.tail = FALSE)  # 1.644854
      qnorm(0.1,  lower.tail = FALSE)  # 1.281552
      qnorm(0.2,  lower.tail = FALSE)  # 0.8416212

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **(i) Critical z-value for** :math:`\alpha = 0.04` **(left-tailed test):**

      .. math::

         z_\alpha = \texttt{qnorm(0.04, lower.tail = FALSE)} = 1.750686

      **(ii) Critical z-value for 90% power** (:math:`\beta = 0.10`):

      .. math::

         z_\beta = \texttt{qnorm(0.1, lower.tail = FALSE)} = 1.281552

      **Cutoff expression:**

      .. math::

         \bar{x}_\text{cutoff} = \mu_0 - z_\alpha \cdot \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}
         = 2.6 - 1.750686 \cdot \frac{0.3}{\sqrt{n}}

      **Power condition:**

      Setting :math:`P(\bar{X} < \bar{x}_\text{cutoff} \mid \mu = 2.4) = 0.90`:

      .. math::

         \frac{\bar{x}_\text{cutoff} - \mu_a}{\sigma/\sqrt{n}} = -z_\beta
         \qquad \Longrightarrow \qquad
         \frac{\mu_0 - z_\alpha \cdot \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}} - \mu_a}{\sigma/\sqrt{n}} = -z_\beta

      Rearranging:

      .. math::

         \sqrt{n} = \frac{(z_\alpha + z_\beta)\,\sigma}{\mu_0 - \mu_a}

      .. math::

         n = \left\lceil\left(\frac{z_\alpha + z_\beta}{(\mu_0 - \mu_a)/\sigma}\right)^2\right\rceil
         = \left\lceil\left(\frac{1.750686 + 1.281552}{0.2/0.3}\right)^2\right\rceil
         = \left\lceil\left(\frac{3.032238}{0.6667}\right)^2\right\rceil

      .. math::

         = \left\lceil (4.5484)^2 \right\rceil = \lceil 20.6876 \rceil = \boxed{21}

.. admonition:: Question 4d  (8 pts)
   :class: note

   Assume Jamie collects a random sample of 25 cranberry batches and measures the average
   pH of each batch. Determine the cutoff pH value (:math:`\bar{x}_\text{cutoff}`)
   corresponding to a significance level :math:`\alpha = 0.04`. Assume the population
   standard deviation remains :math:`\sigma = 0.3` (unchanged from the industry
   standard). Show your work.

   .. code-block:: r

      qnorm(0.04, lower.tail = FALSE)  # 1.750686

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      Since :math:`\sigma = 0.3` is known, we use a :math:`z`-based cutoff. For a
      left-tailed test at :math:`\alpha = 0.04`:

      .. math::

         z_\alpha = \texttt{qnorm(0.04, lower.tail = FALSE)} = 1.750686

      .. math::

         \bar{x}_\text{cutoff} = \mu_0 - z_\alpha \cdot \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}
         = 2.6 - 1.750686 \times \frac{0.3}{\sqrt{25}}
         = 2.6 - 1.750686 \times 0.06
         = 2.6 - 0.1050
         = \boxed{2.4950}

.. admonition:: Question 4e  (3 pts)
   :class: note

   Which of the following R code correctly computes the statistical power of the test?

   .. code-block:: r

      # (A) pnorm((cutoff-2.6)/0.3,  lower.tail=TRUE)
      # (B) pnorm((cutoff-2.6)/0.06, lower.tail=TRUE)
      # (C) pnorm((cutoff-2.6)/0.06, lower.tail=FALSE)
      # (D) pnorm((cutoff-2.4)/0.3,  lower.tail=FALSE)
      # (E) pnorm((cutoff-2.4)/0.06, lower.tail=TRUE)
      # (F) pnorm((cutoff-2.4)/0.06, lower.tail=FALSE)

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Answer: (E)** ``pnorm((cutoff-2.4)/0.06, lower.tail=TRUE)``

      Power is the probability of rejecting :math:`H_0` when the true mean is
      :math:`\mu_a = 2.4`:

      .. math::

         \text{Power} = P\!\left(\bar{X} < \bar{x}_\text{cutoff} \mid \mu = 2.4\right)
         = P\!\left(Z < \frac{\bar{x}_\text{cutoff} - 2.4}{\sigma/\sqrt{n}}\right)

      where :math:`\sigma/\sqrt{n} = 0.3/\sqrt{25} = 0.06`.

      - (A) and (B): subtracts 2.6 (null mean) instead of 2.4 (alternative mean) — wrong.
      - (C): same error plus wrong tail direction.
      - (D): uses population SD 0.3 instead of standard error 0.06.
      - (F): wrong tail — power for left-tailed test requires ``lower.tail=TRUE``.

.. admonition:: Question 4f  (3 pts)
   :class: note

   Which of the following interventions will improve the statistical power of the test,
   assuming all other factors remain constant? **Select all that apply.**

   (A) Plant more Early Black cranberries on the farm.

   (B) Randomly sample more bags of cranberries from the farm.

   (C) Move Early Black bushes to a greenhouse with its own beehive.

   (D) Use a more precise pH measuring device.

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Answers: (B) and (D)**

      - **(A) Does not improve power.** Planting more Early Black cranberries would
        increase cross-pollination, potentially increasing the **true effect size** — but
        this changes the experiment itself, not the statistical procedure. The question
        asks about statistical power holding the effect constant.
      - **(B) Improves power.** A larger sample size :math:`n` reduces the standard error
        :math:`\sigma/\sqrt{n}`, making it easier to detect a true difference.
      - **(C) Does not improve power.** Moving bushes to a greenhouse reduces
        cross-pollination and may reduce the true pH change, but does not directly
        increase statistical power.
      - **(D) Improves power.** A more precise pH measuring device reduces measurement
        error, which reduces the variability :math:`\sigma` in the data. Smaller
        :math:`\sigma` decreases the standard error and increases power.

Problem 5  (23 points) — South African Dairy Production
--------------------------------------------------------

.. admonition:: Problem 5 Setup
   :class: important

   South Africa contributes significantly to the global dairy market. The most productive
   provinces are Limpopo and Mpumalanga. Limpopo cows average **27.3 liters** of milk a
   day, with a standard deviation of **2.4 liters**. For Mpumalanga cows, the mean daily
   production is **25.0 liters**, with a standard deviation of **3.2 liters**. Assume that
   the milk production for these provinces **follow normal distributions**.

.. admonition:: Question 5a  (2 pts)
   :class: note

   **True or False:** We require sample sizes of at least 30 for the sampling distribution
   of the average daily milk production in both provinces to be approximately Normal.

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Answer: FALSE**

      Since milk production in both provinces is stated to **follow normal distributions**,
      the sampling distribution of :math:`\bar{X}` is **exactly normal for any sample
      size** :math:`n \geq 1` — no CLT approximation is needed. The "at least 30" guideline
      applies when the population is non-normal and we rely on the CLT.

.. admonition:: Question 5b  (8 pts)
   :class: note

   A random sample of 20 Limpopo cows was selected for a study and an average of 31
   liters of milk per day was recorded. How many standard deviations is this average away
   from its population mean?

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      The standard error of :math:`\bar{X}` for Limpopo with :math:`n = 20` is:

      .. math::

         \sigma_{\bar{X}} = \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}} = \frac{2.4}{\sqrt{20}} = 0.5367 \text{ liters}

      The number of standard deviations away from the population mean:

      .. math::

         z = \frac{\bar{x} - \mu}{\sigma_{\bar{X}}}
         = \frac{31 - 27.3}{2.4/\sqrt{20}}
         = \frac{3.7}{0.5367}
         = \boxed{6.8945}

      This sample mean is approximately 6.89 standard errors above the population mean
      — an extremely unlikely result if the population mean is truly 27.3 liters.

.. admonition:: Question 5c  (10 pts)
   :class: note

   A Mpumalanga farmer has 20 cows. There is a 50% chance each day that the total daily
   production from this herd is at most how many liters? Justify your answer.

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      Let :math:`T = \sum_{i=1}^{20} X_i` be the total daily production from 20
      independent Mpumalanga cows, where :math:`X_i \sim N(25.0,\ 3.2^2)`.

      Since the :math:`X_i`'s are i.i.d. normal:

      .. math::

         T \sim N(\mu_T,\ \sigma_T^2)

      where:

      .. math::

         \mu_T = n \cdot \mu = 20 \times 25.0 = 500 \text{ liters}

      .. math::

         \sigma_T^2 = n \cdot \sigma^2 = 20 \times (3.2)^2 = 20 \times 10.24 = 204.8

      The **50th percentile** of any symmetric distribution equals its mean. Since
      :math:`T` is normally distributed:

      .. math::

         P(T \leq 500) = 0.50

      There is a **50% chance** each day that the total production from a herd of 20
      Mpumalanga cows is at most :math:`\boxed{500 \text{ liters}}`.

.. admonition:: Question 5d  (3 pts)
   :class: note

   A Limpopo farmer has 20 cows. What is the probability that the average milk production
   for this herd exceeds 38 liters a day?

   .. code-block:: r

      # (A) pnorm((38-27.3)/2.4,      lower.tail=FALSE)
      # (B) 1-pnorm((38-25)/3.2,      lower.tail=TRUE)
      # (C) 1-pnorm((38-27.3)/0.5367, lower.tail=TRUE)
      # (D) pt((38-27.3)/0.5367, df=19, lower.tail=FALSE)

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Answer: (C)** ``1-pnorm((38-27.3)/0.5367, lower.tail=TRUE)``

      We need :math:`P(\bar{X} > 38)` where :math:`\bar{X} \sim N(27.3,\ (2.4/\sqrt{20})^2)`.

      The standard error is :math:`\sigma_{\bar{X}} = 2.4/\sqrt{20} = 0.5367` liters.

      .. math::

         P(\bar{X} > 38) = P\!\left(Z > \frac{38 - 27.3}{0.5367}\right)
         = 1 - \Phi\!\left(\frac{38 - 27.3}{0.5367}\right)

      In R: ``1-pnorm((38-27.3)/0.5367, lower.tail=TRUE)``

      - **(A)** Uses the population SD (2.4) instead of the standard error (0.5367).
      - **(B)** Uses Mpumalanga parameters (25, 3.2) instead of Limpopo's.
      - **(D)** Uses a t-distribution — incorrect since :math:`\sigma` is known.
