Problem 1 — True/False  (12 points, 2 points each)
----------------------------------------------------

.. admonition:: Question 1a  (2 pts)
   :class: note

   Consider a sequence :math:`X_1, X_2, \ldots, X_n` of independent and identically
   distributed random variables drawn from a population :math:`f_X(x)` with finite mean
   :math:`\mu`, and finite variance :math:`\sigma^2 > 0`. Define the sample sum as:

   .. math::

      S_n = \sum_{i=1}^{n} X_i,

   where the subscript :math:`n` explicitly indicates that the sum is over :math:`n`
   random variables.

   **True or False:** According to the central limit theorem (CLT), the standardized
   sample sum

   .. math::

      \frac{(S_n - n\mu)}{\sqrt{n} \cdot \sigma}

   is **exactly** distributed as a standard Normal distribution regardless of the shape of
   :math:`f_X(x)`, provided :math:`n \geq 30`.

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Answer: FALSE**

      The CLT states that the standardized sample sum converges **in distribution** to a
      standard Normal as :math:`n \to \infty` — it is an **approximation** that improves
      with larger :math:`n`, not an exact result for any finite :math:`n`. For the
      distribution to be **exactly** normal for finite :math:`n`, the population
      :math:`f_X(x)` would itself need to be normal. The condition :math:`n \geq 30` is a
      common practical guideline, but it does not make the CLT exact.

.. admonition:: Question 1.2  (2 pts)
   :class: note

   In a randomized block design (RBD), treatments are randomly assigned to experimental
   units within distinct blocks.

   **True or False:** This is done to **balance** rather than mitigate or remove the
   impact of extraneous variables on experimental results.

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Answer: FALSE**

      The primary purpose of blocking in a randomized block design is to **mitigate or
      remove** (not merely balance) the impact of extraneous variables. By grouping
      experimental units that are similar on a known source of variability into the same
      block, blocking reduces the unexplained variation in the response — effectively
      removing the block effect from the error. This is a stronger action than simply
      balancing: it actively controls the nuisance variable.

.. admonition:: Question 1.3  (2 pts)
   :class: note

   Denote :math:`\tau_n = \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{n}} \, SD(X_1 + X_2 + \cdots + X_n)`, where
   the :math:`X_i`'s are independent and identically distributed with finite variance
   :math:`\sigma^2`.

   **True or False:** Then it follows that :math:`\tau_3 > \tau_4`.

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Answer: FALSE**

      Since the :math:`X_i`'s are i.i.d. with variance :math:`\sigma^2`:

      .. math::

         SD(X_1 + X_2 + \cdots + X_n) = \sqrt{n}\,\sigma

      Therefore:

      .. math::

         \tau_n = \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}} \cdot \sqrt{n}\,\sigma = \sigma

      for all :math:`n`. So :math:`\tau_3 = \tau_4 = \sigma` — they are equal, not
      :math:`\tau_3 > \tau_4`.

.. admonition:: Question 1.4  (2 pts)
   :class: note

   A researcher is calculating the sample size needed for a confidence interval with a
   fixed margin of error. The population standard deviation is known.

   **True or False:** The required sample size increases as the confidence coefficient
   decreases.

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Answer: FALSE**

      The sample size formula for a CI with known :math:`\sigma` is
      :math:`n = \left(\dfrac{z_{\alpha/2}\,\sigma}{m}\right)^2`, where :math:`m` is the
      desired margin of error. As the confidence coefficient decreases (e.g., from 99% to
      90%), the critical value :math:`z_{\alpha/2}` **decreases**, so the required sample
      size **decreases**, not increases. A lower confidence coefficient requires a smaller
      sample to achieve the same margin of error.

.. admonition:: Question 1.5  (2 pts)
   :class: note

   The functions ``pnorm()`` and ``pt()`` are available functions in R (RStudio),

   **True or False:** they directly provide critical values for constructing confidence
   intervals and bounds.

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Answer: FALSE**

      ``pnorm()`` and ``pt()`` are **cumulative distribution functions** (CDFs) — they
      compute probabilities :math:`P(Z \leq z)` and :math:`P(T \leq t)`, respectively.
      They do **not** directly provide critical values. To obtain critical values for
      constructing confidence intervals, one uses the **quantile functions** ``qnorm()``
      and ``qt()`` instead.

.. admonition:: Question 1.6  (2 pts)
   :class: note

   When conducting a hypothesis test in which the alternative hypothesis is true,

   **True or False:** the probability of rejecting the null hypothesis increases by taking
   a larger sample size.

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Answer: TRUE**

      When :math:`H_a` is true, the probability of rejecting :math:`H_0` is the **power**
      of the test (:math:`1 - \beta`). Increasing the sample size :math:`n` reduces the
      standard error :math:`\sigma/\sqrt{n}`, which tightens the sampling distribution
      under :math:`H_a` relative to the rejection region, directly increasing the power
      of the test.

Problem 2 — Multiple Choice  (15 points, 3 points each)
---------------------------------------------------------

.. admonition:: Question 2.1  (3 pts)
   :class: note

   Which of the following techniques is primarily used to **control or reduce variability**
   arising from extraneous factors in experimental design?

   (A) Blocking |quad| (B) Replication |quad| (C) Randomization |quad| (D) Realism |quad| (E) All of the above

   .. |quad| unicode:: U+00A0 U+00A0 U+00A0

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Answer: (A)**

      **Blocking** is the technique specifically designed to control or reduce variability
      from known extraneous factors by grouping homogeneous experimental units together.
      Replication increases precision but does not directly control extraneous variability.
      Randomization distributes unknown extraneous effects evenly but does not reduce their
      magnitude. "Realism" is not a standard experimental design principle. Blocking is the
      answer that best matches "control or reduce variability from extraneous factors."

.. admonition:: Question 2.2  (3 pts)
   :class: note

   Time, resources, and practicality influence statistical studies. Which of the following
   is essential for drawing generalizable conclusions from a statistical investigation?

   (A) Selecting a representative sample

   (B) Having a sufficiently large sample size

   (C) Using appropriate well-established statistical methods

   (D) Clearly defining the population of interest

   (E) All of the above are essential for drawing generalizable conclusions

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Answer: (E)**

      All four elements are essential. Without a representative sample (A), results cannot
      be extended to the population. Without adequate sample size (B), estimates are too
      imprecise and tests too underpowered to support generalizable conclusions. Without
      appropriate methods (C), the analysis may be invalid. Without a clearly defined
      population (D), it is unclear to whom the conclusions apply. Each element is
      necessary; none is sufficient alone.

.. admonition:: Question 2.3  (3 pts)
   :class: note

   You used Glassgate, a platform where salaries are posted anonymously, to look up salary
   information for a small-sized consulting firm. You found salary data from 13 verified
   junior data scientists.

   Which of the following statements is **NOT** always true regarding the sampling
   distribution of the average salary computed from these 13 junior data scientists?

   (A) If the population distribution has a strong positive skew, the Central Limit Theorem
   cannot be applied.

   (B) Without additional information about the population distribution, the exact sampling
   distribution cannot be determined.

   (C) The mean of the sampling distribution, :math:`\mu_{\bar{X}}`, is equal to the
   population mean :math:`\mu_X`, regardless of sample size.

   (D) The standard deviation of the sampling distribution :math:`\sigma_{\bar{X}}`, is
   always smaller than the population standard deviation :math:`\sigma_X` when the sample
   size exceeds 2.

   (E) The central limit theorem ensures that the sampling distribution of :math:`\bar{X}`
   is approximately Normal.

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Answer: (E)**

      With only :math:`n = 13` observations from a potentially skewed salary distribution,
      the CLT does **not** guarantee approximate normality. The CLT requires :math:`n` to
      be sufficiently large, and for heavily skewed populations, :math:`n = 13` is generally
      not sufficient. Therefore statement (E) is **not** always true.

      - **(A) NOT always true** — but in this context with :math:`n = 13`, CLT indeed
        cannot be safely applied for a strongly skewed population, making (A) true here.
      - **(B) TRUE** — the exact sampling distribution depends on the unknown population
        shape, which is not specified.
      - **(C) TRUE** — :math:`E[\bar{X}] = \mu_X` always holds for any sample size and
        any population with finite mean.
      - **(D) TRUE** — :math:`\sigma_{\bar{X}} = \sigma_X/\sqrt{n} < \sigma_X` for
        :math:`n > 1`.
      - **(E) NOT always true** — CLT requires sufficiently large :math:`n`; :math:`n = 13`
        is not sufficient for a strongly skewed population.

.. admonition:: Question 2.4  (3 pts)
   :class: note

   A delivery company, CargoSwift Logistics, operates small vans that regularly transport
   packages between warehouses. Each trip includes a fixed set of loading equipment (metal
   securing racks, crates, and straps) weighing exactly 30 lbs. The remaining cargo
   consists of sixteen individual packages, each with weights independently and identically
   distributed as follows:

   .. math::

      X_i \sim \text{Uniform}(a = 44,\ b = 56), \quad i = 1, 2, \ldots, 16.

   Recall that :math:`E[X_i] = \dfrac{a+b}{2}` and
   :math:`\text{Var}(X_i) = \dfrac{(b-a)^2}{12}`.

   The total weight :math:`T` for a typical truckload of 16 packages is given by:

   .. math::

      T = 30 + \sum_{i=1}^{16} X_i.

   CargoSwift's delivery vans have a maximum safe weight load capacity of 850 lbs.
   Select the correct code to calculate the **approximate** probability that a randomly
   selected van containing 16 packages would exceed the safe weight load.

   (A) ``pt(1.4434, df = 15, lower.tail = FALSE)``

   (B) ``pnorm(1.4434, lower.tail = FALSE)``

   (C) ``pt(3.6084, df = 15, lower.tail = FALSE)``

   (D) ``pnorm(3.6084, lower.tail = FALSE)``

   (E) ``pt(5.7735, df = 15, lower.tail = FALSE)``

   (F) ``pnorm(5.7735, lower.tail = FALSE)``

   (G) ``punif(850, min = 704, max = 896, lower.tail = FALSE)``

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Answer: (B)**

      **Step 1 — Find the distribution of** :math:`T`:

      .. math::

         E[X_i] = \frac{44 + 56}{2} = 50, \qquad
         \text{Var}(X_i) = \frac{(56-44)^2}{12} = \frac{144}{12} = 12

      Since the fixed weight is a constant:

      .. math::

         E[T] = 30 + 16 \times 50 = 830 \text{ lbs}

      .. math::

         \text{Var}(T) = 16 \times 12 = 192, \qquad SD(T) = \sqrt{192} = 13.8564 \text{ lbs}

      **Step 2 — Apply the CLT:**

      With :math:`n = 16` independent Uniform random variables, the CLT gives
      :math:`T \approx \text{Normal}(830,\ 13.8564)`. Standardize:

      .. math::

         z = \frac{850 - 830}{13.8564} = \frac{20}{13.8564} = 1.4434

      **Step 3 — Select the code:**

      We need :math:`P(T > 850) \approx P(Z > 1.4434)`. Since the CLT gives a normal
      approximation (not a t-distribution), and :math:`\sigma` is known from the Uniform
      distribution, the correct code is:

      ``pnorm(1.4434, lower.tail = FALSE)``

.. admonition:: Question 2.5  (3 pts)
   :class: note

   A nutritionist conducts a study to test whether a new dietary program significantly
   reduces cholesterol levels from a known baseline of 200 mg/dL. They collect data from
   25 participants and observe a sample standard deviation of 18 mg/dL. They plan to
   perform a **lower-tailed** hypothesis test at significance level :math:`\alpha = 0.05`.

   Select the correct lines of R code to calculate the approximate power of this test for
   detecting a reduction in the mean cholesterol to 190 mg/dL:

   .. code-block:: r

      # (A)
      cutoff <- 200 + qt(0.05, df=24, lower.tail=FALSE)*(18/sqrt(25))
      pt((cutoff - 190)/(18/sqrt(25)), df=24, lower.tail=TRUE)

      # (B)
      cutoff <- 190 - qt(0.95, df=24, lower.tail=FALSE)*(18/sqrt(25))
      pt((cutoff - 200)/(18/sqrt(25)), df=24, lower.tail=TRUE)

      # (C)
      cutoff <- 200 - qnorm(0.95, lower.tail=FALSE)*(18/sqrt(25))
      pnorm((cutoff - 190)/(18/sqrt(25)), lower.tail=FALSE)

      # (D)
      cutoff <- 200 + qnorm(0.05, lower.tail=FALSE)*(18/sqrt(25))
      pnorm((cutoff - 190)/(18/sqrt(25)), lower.tail=FALSE)

      # (E)
      cutoff <- 200 - qt(0.05, df=24, lower.tail=FALSE)*(18/sqrt(25))
      pt((cutoff - 190)/(18/sqrt(25)), df=24, lower.tail=TRUE)

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Answer: (E)**

      For a **lower-tailed** t-test (:math:`H_a: \mu < 200`), :math:`\sigma` is unknown,
      so we use the t-distribution with :math:`df = n - 1 = 24`.

      **The cutoff** is the value :math:`\bar{x}_\text{cutoff}` below which we reject
      :math:`H_0`:

      .. math::

         \bar{x}_\text{cutoff} = \mu_0 - t_{0.05,\,24} \cdot \frac{s}{\sqrt{n}}
         = 200 - t_{0.05,\,24} \cdot \frac{18}{\sqrt{25}}

      In R: ``cutoff <- 200 - qt(0.05, df=24, lower.tail=FALSE) * (18/sqrt(25))``

      Note: ``qt(0.05, df=24, lower.tail=FALSE)`` returns the positive value
      :math:`t_{0.05,24} = 1.7109`, so subtracting gives a cutoff below 200.

      **Power** is :math:`P(\bar{X} < \bar{x}_\text{cutoff} \mid \mu = 190)`:

      .. math::

         \text{Power} = P\!\left(T_{24} < \frac{\bar{x}_\text{cutoff} - 190}{18/\sqrt{25}}\right)

      In R: ``pt((cutoff - 190)/(18/sqrt(25)), df=24, lower.tail=TRUE)``

      - **(A)** Wrong: adds instead of subtracts, giving a cutoff *above* 200.
      - **(B)** Wrong: centers cutoff on 190 instead of 200.
      - **(C), (D)** Wrong: use ``pnorm`` instead of ``pt``; :math:`\sigma` is unknown.

Problem 3  (24 points) — SUV Fuel Efficiency
---------------------------------------------

.. admonition:: Problem 3 Setup
   :class: important

   A car manufacturer advertises that its new compact SUV averages 40 miles per gallon
   (mpg). A consumer advocacy group wants to evaluate this claim and believes the true
   average may be lower. They obtain 54 SUVs from the same model year and measure their
   fuel efficiency under combined city/highway driving, simulating what an average driver
   might encounter. After recording each vehicle's mileage under these controlled yet
   representative conditions, the group finds a sample mean of 37.8 mpg. The population's
   standard deviation is unknown. They plan to conduct a hypothesis test at a **3%
   significance level** to assess the manufacturer's claim.

.. admonition:: Question 3a  (3 pts)
   :class: note

   Identify the parameter of interest. Clearly state its symbolic notation and define it
   briefly in the context of this scenario.

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      Let :math:`\mu_\text{MPG}` denote the **true average miles per gallon** of the new
      compact SUV.

.. admonition:: Question 3b  (4 pts)
   :class: note

   Write the null and alternative hypotheses clearly in symbolic notation.

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      .. math::

         H_0: \mu_\text{MPG} \geq 40

      .. math::

         H_a: \mu_\text{MPG} < 40

.. admonition:: Question 3c  (8 pts)
   :class: note

   The consumer advocacy group reports the corresponding confidence bound at 38.43. Using
   this confidence bound and the provided critical values, deduce the value of the sample
   standard deviation. Provide your answer rounded to four decimal places.

   .. code-block:: r

      qnorm(0.03, lower.tail=FALSE)      # = 1.88
      qt(0.03,   df=53, lower.tail=FALSE) # = 1.92
      qnorm(0.015, lower.tail=FALSE)     # = 2.17
      qt(0.015,  df=53, lower.tail=FALSE) # = 2.23

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      Since :math:`\sigma` is unknown and this is a one-sided test at :math:`\alpha = 0.03`
      with :math:`df = n - 1 = 53`, the 97% upper confidence bound takes the form:

      .. math::

         \text{UCB} = \bar{x} + t_{0.03,\,53} \cdot \frac{s}{\sqrt{n}}

      The critical value is ``qt(0.03, df=53, lower.tail=FALSE)`` :math:`= 1.92`.

      Substituting the known values:

      .. math::

         38.43 = 37.8 + 1.92 \cdot \frac{s}{\sqrt{54}}

      Solving for :math:`s`:

      .. math::

         s = \frac{(38.43 - 37.8) \cdot \sqrt{54}}{1.92}
         = \frac{0.63 \times 7.3485}{1.92}
         = \boxed{2.4112}

.. admonition:: Question 3d  (2 pts)
   :class: note

   Is the reported confidence bound of 38.43 in part (c) an upper bound or a lower bound?
   Please mark the correct option.

   (A) Upper Bound |quad| (B) Lower Bound

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Answer: (A) Upper Bound**

      The hypothesis test is left-tailed (:math:`H_a: \mu_\text{MPG} < 40`), and duality
      between hypothesis tests and confidence regions requires a **one-sided upper
      confidence bound** — providing the largest plausible value for
      :math:`\mu_\text{MPG}`. The bound of 38.43 mpg lies above :math:`\bar{x} = 37.8`
      mpg, consistent with an upper bound.

.. admonition:: Question 3e  (7 pts)
   :class: note

   Using the results of parts (c) and (d), obtain the result of the hypothesis test and
   write the formal contextual conclusion.

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      The 97% upper confidence bound of 38.43 mpg is **below** the null value
      :math:`\mu_0 = 40`. By the duality between confidence bounds and hypothesis tests,
      this means the null value falls **outside** the 97% upper confidence bound, so we
      **reject** :math:`H_0` at :math:`\alpha = 0.03`.

      The data give support (:math:`p\text{-value} < 0.03`) to the claim that the true
      average MPG of the new compact SUVs is **less than 40 MPG**.

Problem 4  (23 points) — Special Agent Gibbs: Veterans Mental Stability
------------------------------------------------------------------------

.. admonition:: Problem 4 Setup
   :class: important

   Special Agent Gibbs decided to pursue his career in academia specializing in national
   security, post-traumatic stress, and investigation strategies. As part of his research,
   Gibbs requested access to a sensitive dataset containing information on veterans. Due to
   privacy and security considerations, the custodians of the dataset could not release it
   directly to Gibbs. Instead, they provided Gibbs with detailed descriptions of the
   available variables and asked him to submit clearly defined research questions. Their
   analyst team would then run analyses on the secure data and provide Gibbs with
   appropriate statistical summaries, test statistics, and supporting details. Assume none
   of the population standard deviations are known.

.. admonition:: Question 4a  (3 pts)
   :class: note

   Five of Gibbs' research questions happen to be lower-tailed hypotheses,
   :math:`H_a: \mu < \mu_0`. Which one of the following test statistics would be most
   likely to reject :math:`H_0`? Assume the same degrees of freedom for all five test
   statistics.

   (A) :math:`t_{ts} = -2.25` |quad| (B) :math:`t_{ts} = -1.02` |quad| (C) :math:`t_{ts} = 0.02` |quad| (D) :math:`t_{ts} = 1.56` |quad| (E) :math:`t_{ts} = 3.02`

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Answer: (A)**

      For a lower-tailed test, we reject :math:`H_0` when the test statistic is
      sufficiently **negative** (far into the left tail). The :math:`p`-value equals
      :math:`P(T \leq t_{ts})`, which is smallest when :math:`t_{ts}` is most negative.
      Among the five options, :math:`t_{ts} = -2.25` is the most negative, giving the
      smallest :math:`p`-value and the greatest evidence to reject :math:`H_0`.

.. admonition:: Question 4b  (3 pts)
   :class: note

   Gibbs believes that veterans' financial status may vary by different factors, such as
   location, household composition, and health status, so he asked the analyst team if
   they can control these extraneous variables. Without direct access to the dataset
   himself, which of the following strategies is most appropriate for the analyst team to
   handle these extraneous variables based on Gibbs' request?

   (A) Discard any cases which are deemed extreme by Gibbs to obtain a consistent dataset.

   (B) Partition the data into blocks (or strata) aligned with these extraneous variables,
   thereby reducing their confounding influence during analysis.

   (C) Randomly select a small number of veterans broadly representative of the entire
   population, simplifying the analysis.

   (D) Ignore extraneous variables since a small bias is acceptable if it results in
   reduced variance.

   (E) Control all extraneous variables from the beginning by requiring veterans to live
   according to randomly assigned conditions.

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Answer: (B)**

      Since the analyst team is working with an existing dataset (not a new experiment),
      experimental interventions like random assignment (E) are not possible. The most
      appropriate observational approach is to **partition (block/stratify) the data** by
      the known extraneous variables — location, household composition, and health status
      — which controls their confounding influence during analysis. Discarding extreme
      cases (A) would introduce bias; a small subsample (C) would reduce precision;
      ignoring confounders (D) would bias results.

.. admonition:: Question 4c  (3 pts)
   :class: note

   Gibbs identified two variables: social isolation level (SIL, categorical) and mental
   stability score (MSS, numerical). SIL has four categories: socially active, somewhat
   socially active, somewhat isolated, and completely isolated. Specifically, Gibbs wants
   to see if the mean difference in MSS between somewhat isolated and completely isolated
   groups is **greater than 20 points**.

   The analysts, acting on Gibbs' request, paired 50 veterans from the somewhat isolated
   group with 50 veterans from the completely isolated group. Pairing was done based on
   demographics and veteran history to control possible extraneous factors. After matching,
   the difference in mental stability scores was computed for each pair as
   :math:`D = \text{Somewhat Isolated} - \text{Completely Isolated}`.

   Which of the following hypothesis testing procedures is appropriate to answer Gibbs'
   question?

   (A) One-sample :math:`t`-test

   (B) Two-sample Independent :math:`t`-test

   (C) Two-sample Matched Pairs :math:`t`-test

   (D) None of the above

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Answer: (C) Two-sample Matched Pairs** :math:`t`**-test**

      The analysts explicitly paired veterans across the two groups based on demographics
      and veteran history, creating dependent pairs. With the difference :math:`D`
      computed for each pair, the appropriate procedure is a **matched pairs (paired)
      t-test**. Note that while a paired t-test is mathematically equivalent to a
      one-sample t-test on the differences, the context here is explicitly a two-sample
      matched pairs design.

.. admonition:: Question 4d  (4 pts)
   :class: note

   State clearly the null and alternative hypotheses using the appropriate mathematical
   notations.

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      With :math:`D = \text{Somewhat Isolated} - \text{Completely Isolated}`:

      .. math::

         H_0: \mu_D \leq 20

      .. math::

         H_a: \mu_D > 20

.. admonition:: Question 4e  (3 pts)
   :class: note

   Gibbs wants to find the :math:`p`-value of a test statistic, :math:`t_{ts} = 2.14`.
   Which of the following R code statements returns the correct :math:`p`-value?

   .. code-block:: r

      # (A) pt(2.14, df = 50, lower.tail = FALSE)
      # (B) pt(2.14, df = 50, lower.tail = TRUE)
      # (C) pt(2.14, df = 49, lower.tail = FALSE)
      # (D) pt(2.14, df = 49, lower.tail = TRUE)
      # (E) pt(2.14, df = 99, lower.tail = FALSE)
      # (F) pt(2.14, df = 99, lower.tail = TRUE)

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Answer: (C)** ``pt(2.14, df = 49, lower.tail = FALSE)``

      This is a **right-tailed** test (:math:`H_a: \mu_D > 20`), so the :math:`p`-value
      is the upper-tail probability, requiring ``lower.tail = FALSE``. The degrees of
      freedom for a paired t-test on :math:`n = 50` pairs is :math:`df = n - 1 = 49`.

      - (A) uses ``df = 50`` — off by one.
      - (D) uses ``lower.tail = TRUE`` — wrong tail direction for a right-tailed test.
      - (E) uses ``df = 99`` — that would apply to an unpaired two-sample test.

.. admonition:: Question 4f  (7 pts)
   :class: note

   The calculated :math:`p`-value is 0.01868. At a significance level of
   :math:`\alpha = 0.02`, state your formal decision and conclusion in the context of the
   problem.

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Decision:** The :math:`p`-value :math:`= 0.01868 \leq 0.02 = \alpha`, therefore
      we have sufficient evidence to **reject** :math:`H_0`.

      **Conclusion:** The data give some support (:math:`p`-value :math:`= 0.01868`) to
      the claim that the true average mental stability score (MSS) is higher for veterans
      classified as Somewhat Isolated versus Completely Isolated — specifically, that the
      mean difference exceeds 20 points.

Problem 5  (31 points) — Delivery Courier Mileage: California vs. Non-California

.. admonition:: Problem 5 Setup
   :class: important

   The rapid growth of food delivery services has dramatically increased the number of
   two-wheeled couriers on city streets. While this surge has benefited local businesses
   and consumers, it has also led to increased traffic congestion and safety concerns,
   prompting some cities to consider stricter delivery regulations (MassLive, 2025).

   California cities, in particular, are often at the forefront of adopting innovative
   transportation policies and stricter environmental and safety regulations. To better
   understand the potential impact of these regulations and inform future policy decisions,
   a food delivery analytics firm seeks to compare the total monthly mileage traveled by
   two-wheeled delivery couriers employed by a major third-party platform. Mileage is
   measured as the combined total distance traveled by all couriers within each city over
   a four-week period in late summer 2024. Using the summary statistics below, the firm
   aims to determine if there is a significant difference in the average total monthly
   mileage between California and non-California cities.

        - Non-California cities

.. admonition:: Question 5a  (4 pts)
   :class: note

   State the assumptions necessary for the hypothesis test on the average total monthly
   mileage between California and non-California cities.

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      As this is a two-independent sample procedure, we have the following assumptions:

      **Independence:**

      - The cities in each group (California and non-California) constitute independent,
        random samples.
      - Total monthly mileage measurements within each city are independent of one
        another.

      **Normality:** The total monthly mileage in each group is approximately normally
      distributed. This is especially important given the small sample sizes
      (:math:`n = 11` per group).

.. admonition:: Question 5b  (4 pts)
   :class: note

   As part of your summer internship with the food delivery analytics firm, you have been
   asked to determine if there is a significant difference in average total monthly mileage
   between California and non-California cities. Using :math:`\alpha = 0.03`, perform the
   first two steps of the four-step hypothesis test procedure.

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Step 1 — Parameters of interest:**

      Let :math:`\mu_\text{California}` and :math:`\mu_\text{non-California}` be the
      parameters of interest, representing the population mean total monthly mileage of
      two-wheeled couriers across California and non-California cities, respectively.

      **Step 2 — Hypotheses:**

      .. math::

         H_0: \mu_\text{California} - \mu_\text{non-California} = 0

      .. math::

         H_a: \mu_\text{California} - \mu_\text{non-California} \neq 0

.. admonition:: Question 5c  (8 pts)
   :class: note

   Calculate the appropriate test statistic for comparing the mean total mileage between
   California and non-California cities. Clearly show the steps and formula used.

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      Since the population standard deviations are unknown and not assumed equal, we use
      the **Welch two-sample t-test**:

      .. math::

         t_\text{TS} = \frac{\bar{x}_\text{California} - \bar{x}_\text{non-California}}
         {\sqrt{\dfrac{s_\text{California}^2}{n_\text{California}} +
         \dfrac{s_\text{non-California}^2}{n_\text{non-California}}}}

      Substituting values:

      .. math::

         t_\text{TS} = \frac{752{,}962 - 824{,}387.6}
         {\sqrt{\dfrac{697{,}033.6^2}{11} + \dfrac{918{,}850.2^2}{11}}}
         = \frac{-71{,}425.6}{\sqrt{44{,}168{,}712{,}684.5 + 76{,}753{,}244{,}549.1}}

      .. math::

         = \frac{-71{,}425.6}{\sqrt{120{,}921{,}957{,}233.6}}
         = \frac{-71{,}425.6}{347{,}738.3}
         = \boxed{-0.2054}

.. admonition:: Question 5d  (3 pts)
   :class: note

   Without additional assumptions, which of the following represents the most appropriate
   degrees of freedom for the test statistic found in part (c)?

   (A) 10 |quad| (B) 20 |quad| (C) 18.646 |quad| (D) None of the above

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Answer: (C) 18.646**

      The Welch–Satterthwaite approximation gives:

      .. math::

         \nu = \frac{\left(\dfrac{s_\text{CA}^2}{n_\text{CA}} +
         \dfrac{s_\text{non-CA}^2}{n_\text{non-CA}}\right)^2}
         {\dfrac{\left(s_\text{CA}^2/n_\text{CA}\right)^2}{n_\text{CA}-1} +
         \dfrac{\left(s_\text{non-CA}^2/n_\text{non-CA}\right)^2}{n_\text{non-CA}-1}}
         = 18.646

      - (A) 10 would be the degrees of freedom for one group alone (:math:`n - 1`).
      - (B) 20 would be the pooled degrees of freedom, which assumes equal variances.
      - (C) 18.646 is the Welch–Satterthwaite result — correct for unequal variances.

.. admonition:: Question 5e  (3 pts)
   :class: note

   Select the correct R code and output that provides the correct critical value for
   constructing a 97% confidence interval. Assume :math:`\nu` represents the correct
   degrees of freedom if appropriate.

   .. code-block:: r

      # (A) qnorm(p=0.03/2, lower.tail = FALSE)   -> 2.17009
      # (B) qt(p=0.03/2, df=nu, lower.tail = FALSE) -> 2.349237
      # (C) 2*qnorm(p=0.03, lower.tail = FALSE)   -> 3.761587
      # (D) 2*qt(p=0.03, df=nu, lower.tail = FALSE) -> 4.004843

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Answer: (B)** ``qt(p=0.03/2, df=nu, lower.tail = FALSE)`` → 2.349237

      Since :math:`\sigma` is unknown we use a t-distribution, not the normal. For a 97%
      confidence interval, the two-sided error is :math:`\alpha = 0.03`, so we need the
      upper :math:`\alpha/2 = 0.015` critical value:
      ``qt(p=0.03/2, df=nu, lower.tail=FALSE)`` with :math:`\nu = 18.646`.

      - (A) uses the z-distribution — incorrect since :math:`\sigma` is unknown.
      - (C) and (D) double the quantile, which is not how critical values for CIs are
        computed.

.. admonition:: Question 5f  (6 pts)
   :class: note

   Using the summary statistics provided and the critical value, calculate the 97%
   confidence interval for the difference in mean total monthly mileage (California minus
   non-California). Clearly show all necessary formulas and steps.

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Point estimate:**

      .. math::

         \bar{x}_\text{CA} - \bar{x}_\text{non-CA} = 752{,}962 - 824{,}387.6 = -71{,}425.6

      **Standard error** (from part c):

      .. math::

         \sqrt{\frac{s_\text{CA}^2}{n_\text{CA}} + \frac{s_\text{non-CA}^2}{n_\text{non-CA}}} = 347{,}738.3

      **97% CI:**

      .. math::

         \left(\bar{x}_\text{CA} - \bar{x}_\text{non-CA}\right) \pm t_{0.03/2,\,\nu}
         \cdot \sqrt{\frac{s_\text{CA}^2}{n_\text{CA}} + \frac{s_\text{non-CA}^2}{n_\text{non-CA}}}

      .. math::

         -71{,}425.6 \pm 2.349237 \times 347{,}738.3

      .. math::

         \boxed{(-888{,}345.3,\; 745{,}494.1)}

.. admonition:: Question 5g  (3 pts)
   :class: note

   Provide an accurate interpretation of the confidence interval in context, explaining
   what it indicates about the difference in average total monthly mileage between
   California and non-California cities.

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      We are **97% confident** that the true difference in average total monthly mileage
      (California minus non-California) is captured by the interval
      :math:`(-888{,}345.3,\; 745{,}494.1)`.

      Since this interval includes both negative and positive values (including zero), it
      indicates that there is **no clear evidence of a significant difference** in the
      average monthly mileage between California and non-California cities at the
      :math:`\alpha = 0.03` significance level.
