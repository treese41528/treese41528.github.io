.. admonition:: Question 1.1  (2 pts)
   :class: note

   Let :math:`X` be a Poisson random variable with :math:`E[X] = \mu` (average per-hour rate). Suppose we define two Poisson random variables, :math:`Y` and :math:`Z`, defined on two three-hour periods, sharing the rate of :math:`X`. Then :math:`Y` and :math:`Z` must be independent and identically distributed, :math:`\text{Poisson}(3\mu)`.

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Answer: FALSE**

      Conditionally on :math:`X = \lambda`, both :math:`Y \mid X = \lambda` and :math:`Z \mid X = \lambda` are :math:`\text{Poisson}(3\lambda)` and independent. However, they **share the same random rate** :math:`X`, which induces a positive marginal correlation. By the **law of total covariance** (and conditional independence of :math:`Y` and :math:`Z` given :math:`X`):

      .. math::

         \text{Cov}(Y, Z) = \underbrace{E[\text{Cov}(Y,Z \mid X)]}_{=\,0} + \text{Cov}(E[Y \mid X],\, E[Z \mid X]) = \text{Cov}(3X,\, 3X) = 9\,\text{Var}(X) = 9\mu > 0

      Because :math:`\text{Cov}(Y, Z) > 0`, :math:`Y` and :math:`Z` are **not** marginally independent. The i.i.d. claim fails — the statement is FALSE.

.. admonition:: Question 1.2  (2 pts)
   :class: note

   Let :math:`A_1, A_2, \ldots, A_n` and :math:`B` be events from a sample space :math:`\Omega` where :math:`A_1, A_2, \ldots, A_n` form a partition of :math:`\Omega` and :math:`P(B) > 0`. Then it must follow that :math:`\sum_{i=1}^{n} P(A_i \mid B) = 1`.

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Answer: TRUE**

      Since the :math:`A_i` partition :math:`\Omega`, the events :math:`A_1 \cap B, A_2 \cap B, \ldots, A_n \cap B` partition :math:`B`. Therefore:

      .. math::

         \sum_{i=1}^{n} P(A_i \mid B) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \frac{P(A_i \cap B)}{P(B)} = \frac{P(B)}{P(B)} = 1

.. admonition:: Question 1.3  (2 pts)
   :class: note

   A special deck of cards contains eight cards: for each number 1, 2, 3, and 4 there is exactly one red card and one black card (so the cards are 1R, 1B, 2R, 2B, 3R, 3B, 4R, 4B). Two cards are drawn at random without replacement, in order. Let :math:`C` denote the event that the first card drawn is red, and let :math:`D` denote the event that the second card drawn is either a 1 or a 2. Events :math:`C` and :math:`D` are independent.

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Answer: TRUE**

      :math:`P(C) = 4/8 = 1/2`. To find :math:`P(D \mid C)`, condition on each red first card:

      - First is **1R**: remaining cards with number 1 or 2: {1B, 2R, 2B} — 3 of 7.
      - First is **2R**: remaining with number 1 or 2: {1R, 1B, 2B} — 3 of 7.
      - First is **3R** or **4R**: remaining with number 1 or 2: {1R, 1B, 2R, 2B} — 4 of 7.

      .. math::

         P(D \mid C) = \frac{1}{4}\cdot\frac{3}{7} + \frac{1}{4}\cdot\frac{3}{7} + \frac{1}{4}\cdot\frac{4}{7} + \frac{1}{4}\cdot\frac{4}{7} = \frac{14}{28} = \frac{1}{2}

      By symmetry, :math:`P(D \mid C') = 1/2` as well (verified by the same case analysis for black first cards). Therefore :math:`P(D) = 1/2 = P(D \mid C)`, confirming that :math:`C` and :math:`D` are **independent**.

.. admonition:: Question 1.4  (2 pts)
   :class: note

   On each day, a factory may be in a high-stress state (:math:`Y = 1`, probability :math:`1/4`) or normal operating mode (:math:`Y = 0`, probability :math:`3/4`). Let :math:`X` be the number of machine breakdowns, where :math:`X \mid Y = 1 \sim \text{Poisson}(10)` and :math:`X \mid Y = 0 \sim \text{Poisson}(6)`. Define :math:`V = X \cdot (1 - Y)`. Since :math:`V = X \cdot (1 - Y)`, it follows that :math:`E[V] = E[X] \cdot (1 - E[Y]) = 21/4`.

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Answer: FALSE**

      The factoring :math:`E[X \cdot (1-Y)] = E[X] \cdot E[1-Y]` would be valid **only if :math:`X` and :math:`Y` were independent**. They are not — :math:`X` and :math:`Y` are dependent by construction (:math:`X`'s distribution changes with :math:`Y`).

      The correct calculation conditions on :math:`Y`:

      .. math::

         E[V] = E[X \cdot (1 - Y)] = E[X(1-Y) \mid Y = 1] \cdot P(Y=1) + E[X(1-Y) \mid Y = 0] \cdot P(Y=0)

      .. math::

         = E[X \cdot 0 \mid Y = 1] \cdot \tfrac{1}{4} + E[X \cdot 1 \mid Y = 0] \cdot \tfrac{3}{4} = 0 + 6 \cdot \tfrac{3}{4} = \tfrac{18}{4} = 4.5

      The claim that :math:`E[V] = E[X](1 - E[Y]) = 7 \cdot (3/4) = 21/4 = 5.25` is incorrect.

.. admonition:: Question 1.5  (2 pts)
   :class: note

   :math:`X \sim N(\mu_X = 120, \sigma_X = 15)` and :math:`Y \sim N(\mu_Y = 160, \sigma_Y = 20)` are independent. The planning index is :math:`I = 0.3X + 0.7Y + 50`. The variance of :math:`I` satisfies :math:`\text{Var}(I) = 0.3 \cdot \text{Var}(X) + 0.7 \cdot \text{Var}(Y) = 347.5`.

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Answer: FALSE**

      For a linear combination of independent random variables, the variance rule requires **squaring the coefficients**:

      .. math::

         \text{Var}(I) = (0.3)^2 \text{Var}(X) + (0.7)^2 \text{Var}(Y) = 0.09 \times 225 + 0.49 \times 400 = 20.25 + 196 = 216.25

      The statement uses :math:`0.3 \cdot \text{Var}(X) + 0.7 \cdot \text{Var}(Y) = 0.3(225) + 0.7(400) = 67.5 + 280 = 347.5`, which omits the squaring of the coefficients.

.. admonition:: Question 1.6  (2 pts)
   :class: note

   In a one-way ANOVA, a Tukey HSD output reports results for 105 unique pairwise comparisons among the group means. The single factor variable in the ANOVA model must have exactly 15 levels.

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Answer: TRUE**

      The number of unique pairwise comparisons among :math:`k` groups is :math:`\binom{k}{2} = k(k-1)/2`. Setting this equal to 105:

      .. math::

         \frac{k(k-1)}{2} = 105 \implies k^2 - k - 210 = 0 \implies k = \frac{1 + \sqrt{841}}{2} = \frac{1 + 29}{2} = 15

      The factor must have exactly **15 levels**.

.. admonition:: Question 1.7  (2 pts)
   :class: note

   A one-way ANOVA is conducted, all assumptions are found reasonable, the F-test statistic is approximately 1 with a large p-value, and the null hypothesis of equal treatment means is not rejected. In this situation, the appropriate next step is to carry out a Tukey multiple comparison procedure to determine which specific treatment means differ.

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Answer: FALSE**

      The Tukey HSD post-hoc procedure is carried out **only after rejecting** the ANOVA null hypothesis, to identify which specific pairs of means differ. When :math:`H_0` is not rejected (F ≈ 1, large p-value), there is no evidence that any means differ, and performing Tukey HSD would be both statistically inappropriate and uninterpretable. The appropriate conclusion is simply to fail to reject :math:`H_0` and report no evidence of differences among treatment means.

.. admonition:: Question 1.8  (2 pts)
   :class: note

   In simple linear regression :math:`y_i = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_i + \varepsilon_i` where the :math:`\varepsilon_i` are i.i.d. normal with variance :math:`\sigma^2`. Under :math:`H_0\colon \beta_1 = 0`, if :math:`\sigma^2` is known, the test statistic :math:`\hat{\beta}_1 \div \sqrt{\sigma^2 / S_{XX}}` follows a standard normal distribution.

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Answer: TRUE**

      Since the :math:`\varepsilon_i` are i.i.d. :math:`N(0, \sigma^2)`, the least-squares estimator satisfies :math:`\hat{\beta}_1 \sim N(\beta_1,\, \sigma^2 / S_{XX})`. Under :math:`H_0\colon \beta_1 = 0`:

      .. math::

         \frac{\hat{\beta}_1}{\sqrt{\sigma^2 / S_{XX}}} \sim N(0, 1)

      When :math:`\sigma^2` is unknown and replaced by :math:`s^2 = \text{MSE}`, the statistic follows a :math:`t`-distribution with :math:`n - 2` degrees of freedom. With :math:`\sigma^2` **known**, the standard normal applies.

.. admonition:: Question 1.9  (2 pts)
   :class: note

   In a simple linear regression setting, a residual plot shows points randomly scattered around the horizontal line at 0 with roughly the same vertical spread for all values of :math:`x`, and no clear curvature or funnel shape. This residual pattern provides reasonable support for both the linearity assumption and the constant variance (homoscedasticity) assumption.

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Answer: TRUE**

      A residual plot where points are (1) randomly scattered around zero with no curvature and (2) maintain roughly constant vertical spread across all :math:`x`-values is precisely what we expect when both the linearity assumption and the homoscedasticity assumption hold. Curvature in the residual plot would indicate a violation of linearity; a fan or funnel shape would indicate heteroscedasticity. Neither is present, so both assumptions receive support.

.. admonition:: Question 1.10  (2 pts)
   :class: note

   In a simple linear regression analysis, the sample coefficient of determination :math:`R^2` is found to be very close to 1. From this, we can conclude that a large proportion of the variability in the response variable is explained by its linear relationship with the explanatory variable, and that the relationship between them is in fact linear.

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Answer: FALSE**

      :math:`R^2 \approx 1` does confirm that the fitted linear model explains a large proportion of the variability in :math:`Y`. However, it **does not prove** that the true underlying relationship is linear. A nonlinear relationship can still yield a high :math:`R^2` over a restricted range of :math:`x` values, and the linear model could be a good approximation without being the true mechanism. Assessing linearity requires examining diagnostic plots (scatter plot, residual plot), not just :math:`R^2`.

.. admonition:: Question 2.1  (3 pts)
   :class: note

   Three elevators in the Math building malfunction during the day (8 am–7 pm) but tend to work normally at night (7 pm–8 am). Let :math:`X \in \{\text{day}, \text{night}\}` and :math:`Y` = number of working elevators. The conditional distributions of :math:`Y` given :math:`X` are displayed in the bar graphs below.

   .. figure:: https://yjjpfnblgtrogqvcjaon.supabase.co/storage/v1/object/public/stat-350-assets/images/Exams/FinalExam/FALL2025/image1.png
      :alt: Two side-by-side bar graphs. Day graph: P(Y=0)=0.01, P(Y=1)=0.05, P(Y=2)=0.34, P(Y=3)=0.60. Night graph: P(Y=0)=0.01, P(Y=1)=0.05, P(Y=2)=0.14, P(Y=3)=0.80.
      :align: center
      :width: 80%

   Which of the following probabilities is computed correctly?

   - (A) :math:`P(Y \leq 1) = 0.12`
   - (B) :math:`P(\{X = \text{day}\} \cap \{Y = 3\}) = 0.6`
   - (C) :math:`P(X = \text{day}) = P(X = \text{night}) = 1`
   - (D) :math:`P(Y > 1 \mid X = \text{night}) = 0.94`
   - (E) All of the above

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Answer: (D)**

      Reading directly from the Night bar chart:

      .. math::

         P(Y > 1 \mid X = \text{night}) = P(Y = 2 \mid X = \text{night}) + P(Y = 3 \mid X = \text{night}) = 0.14 + 0.80 = 0.94 \checkmark

      - **(A) FALSE.** :math:`P(Y \leq 1)` is a marginal probability. The bar charts show only the conditional distributions :math:`P(Y \mid X)`, and :math:`P(X = \text{day})` and :math:`P(X = \text{night})` are not given, so this marginal cannot be computed from the chart alone.
      - **(B) FALSE.** :math:`P(\{X = \text{day}\} \cap \{Y = 3\}) = P(Y = 3 \mid X = \text{day}) \cdot P(X = \text{day}) = 0.6 \cdot P(X = \text{day})`. Since :math:`P(X = \text{day})` is unknown, this joint probability cannot be determined.
      - **(C) FALSE.** :math:`P(X = \text{day})` and :math:`P(X = \text{night})` are probabilities of a single categorical variable; they must sum to 1, so each must be strictly between 0 and 1.

.. admonition:: Question 2.2  (3 pts)
   :class: note

   Let :math:`X` be a discrete random variable with support :math:`\{0, 1, 2, 3\}`. Find the constant :math:`k` that makes the following a valid pmf:

   .. math::

      f_X(x) = \begin{cases} 0.45, & x = 0 \\ k/x, & x = 1, 2, 3 \\ 0, & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}

   - (A) :math:`k = 0.45`
   - (B) :math:`k = 0.30`
   - (C) :math:`k = 0.5455`
   - (D) :math:`k = 0.9124`
   - (E) :math:`k = 0.5006`
   - (F) :math:`k = \infty`

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Answer: (B)**

      For a valid pmf, probabilities must sum to 1:

      .. math::

         0.45 + \frac{k}{1} + \frac{k}{2} + \frac{k}{3} = 1

      .. math::

         k\!\left(1 + \frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{3}\right) = 0.55 \implies k \cdot \frac{11}{6} = 0.55 \implies k = 0.55 \times \frac{6}{11} = \frac{3.3}{11} = \boxed{0.30}

.. admonition:: Question 2.3  (3 pts)
   :class: note

   A simple linear regression is fit to relate product price (:math:`x`) to weekly sales (:math:`Y`). All standard assumptions are satisfied. A 95% confidence interval for :math:`\beta_1` is :math:`(-12.4,\,-4.1)`. At :math:`\alpha = 0.05`, what is the correct conclusion about the population linear association?

   - (A) There is no evidence of a linear association because 0 is not in the interval.
   - (B) There is evidence of a linear association because 0 is not in the interval.
   - (C) There is evidence that changing price will cause weekly sales to decrease, because 0 is not in the interval.
   - (D) We can only conclude that there is a linear association in this particular sample; a CI cannot draw conclusions about the population.
   - (E) We cannot draw any conclusion about linear association without also knowing the p-value.

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Answer: (B)**

      By the duality between confidence intervals and hypothesis tests, a 95% CI for :math:`\beta_1` that excludes 0 is equivalent to rejecting :math:`H_0\colon \beta_1 = 0` at :math:`\alpha = 0.05`. Since :math:`(-12.4, -4.1)` does not contain 0, we have statistically significant evidence of a linear association between price and sales **in the population**.

      - **(A) FALSE.** The fact that 0 is *not* in the interval is precisely why we *do* have evidence of association — not the reverse.
      - **(C) FALSE.** The existence of a linear association does not establish causation. A regression model alone cannot prove that changing price *causes* a change in sales.
      - **(D) FALSE.** Confidence intervals *do* generalize to population-level conclusions — that is their purpose.
      - **(E) FALSE.** The CI and the two-sided t-test for :math:`\beta_1` are exactly dual; the CI provides the same information as the p-value for this purpose.

.. admonition:: Question 2.4  (3 pts)
   :class: note

   A researcher fits a simple linear regression model. Which plot is most appropriate for checking the normality assumption of the errors?

   - (A) Scatterplot of :math:`y` versus :math:`x`.
   - (B) Plot of residuals versus :math:`x` values.
   - (C) Normal probability plot (QQ-plot) of the residuals.
   - (D) Normal probability plot (QQ-plot) of the response.
   - (E) Histogram of the response.
   - (F) Histogram of the explanatory variable.

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Answer: (C)**

      The normality assumption in SLR states that the **error terms** :math:`\varepsilon_i` are normally distributed. Since errors are unobservable, we assess this using the **residuals** as estimates. A normal QQ-plot of the residuals is the standard diagnostic: if the residuals are approximately normal, the points will fall close to the 45° reference line.

      - **(A), (B)** assess linearity and homoscedasticity, not normality.
      - **(D), (E)** plot the raw response :math:`Y`, whose distribution depends on both :math:`x` and the error; it is not the same as the distribution of the errors.
      - **(F)** is irrelevant to normality of errors.

.. admonition:: Question 2.5  (3 pts)
   :class: note

   The ANOVA F-statistic always falls within which range?

   - (A) The positive real numbers :math:`(0, \infty)`.
   - (B) The real numbers :math:`(-\infty, \infty)`.
   - (C) The negative real numbers :math:`(-\infty, 0)`.
   - (D) The real numbers between 0 and 1.
   - (E) None of the above.

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Answer: (A)**

      The F-statistic is :math:`F_{\text{TS}} = \text{MSA}/\text{MSE}`. Both MSA and MSE are ratios of non-negative sums of squares to positive degrees of freedom, so each is non-negative. The F-statistic is therefore non-negative. In practice, :math:`F_{\text{TS}} > 0` whenever there is any variability between groups, which is virtually always the case with real data. The key accepts (A) as the correct answer.

      .. admonition:: Note
         :class: tip

         Technically, :math:`F_{\text{TS}} = 0` is possible if all group means are identical (SSA = 0). Strictly speaking the range is :math:`[0, \infty)`, but (A) is the best available answer since none of the other options are correct.

.. admonition:: Question 2.6  (3 pts)
   :class: note

   A researcher compares four insomnia drugs using one-way ANOVA by randomly assigning seniors to one of the four treatments. Which statements correctly describe the relationship between between-drug variation, within-drug variation, and the F-statistic?

   - (A) The ANOVA F-statistic is the ratio of between-group variation to within-group variation.
   - (B) A large F occurs when differences among the four drug sample means are large relative to the typical person-to-person variability within each drug group.
   - (C) The within-drug variation sets the noise level for judging whether observed differences among drug sample means look unusually large.
   - (D) An F-statistic near 1 indicates that between-drug variation is about the same size as within-drug variation, so the drug means do not stand out beyond background variability.
   - (E) All of the above.
   - (F) None of the above.

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Answer: (E)**

      All four statements accurately describe how the F-statistic works in one-way ANOVA. Each is a correct and complementary description: (A) gives the mathematical definition; (B) describes when F is large; (C) explains the role of MSE as a baseline; and (D) explains the null-hypothesis-consistent case of F ≈ 1.

Problem 3 — Venn Diagrams and Set Theory (18 points)
------------------------------------------------------

.. admonition:: Question 3a  (8 pts)
   :class: note

   Match each Venn diagram (A, B, C, or D) with the probability statement that correctly represents its colored region.

   .. figure:: https://yjjpfnblgtrogqvcjaon.supabase.co/storage/v1/object/public/stat-350-assets/images/Exams/FinalExam/FALL2025/image2.png
      :alt: Four Venn diagrams labeled A, B, C, D, each showing three overlapping circles E, F, G. Diagram A shades the region where E and G overlap. Diagram B shades the region of F only (the part of F outside both E and G). Diagram C shades the part of F and G that falls outside E. Diagram D shades the overlapping regions of F with E and with G.
      :align: center
      :width: 85%

   .. flat-table:: Venn Diagram Matching
      :header-rows: 1
      :widths: 10 45 45

      * - Row
        - Notation
        - Venn Diagram Letter
      * - i
        - :math:`P(E' \cap F \cap G')`
        - B
      * - ii
        - :math:`P(E \cap G)`
        - A
      * - iii
        - :math:`P\!\bigl(F \cap \bigl((E \cap F) \cup G\bigr)\bigr)`
        - D
      * - iv
        - :math:`P\!\bigl(E' \cap (F \cup G)\bigr)`
        - C

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Row i — B:** :math:`E' \cap F \cap G'` is the part of :math:`F` that lies outside both :math:`E` and :math:`G`. In a three-circle diagram this is the region of :math:`F` that does not overlap with :math:`E` or :math:`G` — the center-left "F-only" region. Diagram B shows exactly this.

      **Row ii — A:** :math:`E \cap G` is the overlap of :math:`E` and :math:`G`, which includes both the :math:`E \cap G \cap F'` region and the :math:`E \cap G \cap F` region (:math:`E \cap F \cap G`). Diagram A shades all overlapping area between :math:`E` and :math:`G`.

      **Row iii — D:** :math:`F \cap \bigl((E \cap F) \cup G\bigr) = (E \cap F) \cup (F \cap G)` — this is the region of :math:`F` that overlaps with :math:`E` or :math:`G` (or both). Diagram D shades the two overlap zones within :math:`F`.

      **Row iv — C:** :math:`E' \cap (F \cup G)` is the part of :math:`F \cup G` that falls outside :math:`E`. This excludes :math:`E` but includes everything in :math:`F` or :math:`G` that :math:`E` doesn't cover. Diagram C shows this region shaded.

.. admonition:: Question 3b  (10 pts)
   :class: note

   Using the Venn diagram probabilities, define events :math:`A`, :math:`B`, :math:`C`, and :math:`D` as the colored regions from parts i–iv respectively.

   (i) **(4 pts)** Compute :math:`P(A \cap B \cap C \cap D)`.

   (ii) **(6 pts)** Compute :math:`P(A \cup B \cup C \cup D)`.

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **(i)** From the part (a) matching:

      - :math:`A = E \cap G`. From the Venn diagram probabilities, the region :math:`E \cap G \cap F'` (E and G without F) has probability 0; so effectively :math:`A = E \cap F \cap G` with :math:`P(A) = 0.32`.
      - :math:`B = E' \cap F \cap G'` (F-only region). The diagram shows this region also has probability 0, so :math:`P(B) = 0`.

      Since :math:`A \subseteq E` and :math:`B \subseteq E'`, we have :math:`A \cap B \subseteq E \cap E' = \emptyset`. Therefore:

      .. math::

         P(A \cap B \cap C \cap D) \leq P(A \cap B) = 0 \implies \boxed{P(A \cap B \cap C \cap D) = 0}

      **(ii)** Identifying :math:`A \cup B \cup C \cup D`:

      - :math:`A = E \cap G` (regions within both :math:`E` and :math:`G`)
      - :math:`B = E' \cap F \cap G'` (F-only)
      - :math:`C = E' \cap (F \cup G)` (part of :math:`F \cup G` outside :math:`E`)
      - :math:`D = (E \cap F) \cup (F \cap G)` (overlaps within :math:`F`)

      Together, :math:`A \cup B \cup C \cup D = F \cup G` — they cover every region touching :math:`F` or :math:`G`.

      Reading probabilities from the Venn diagram for regions in :math:`F \cup G`:

      .. math::

         P(A \cup B \cup C \cup D) = P(F \cup G) = 0.20 + 0.32 + 0.05 + 0.10 = \boxed{0.67}

      (The region :math:`E' \cap F \cap G'` = F-only contributes 0 per the diagram, and :math:`E`-only (0.15) and outside (0.18) are outside :math:`F \cup G`.)

Problem 4 — Uniform and Custom Distributions (31 points)
----------------------------------------------------------

.. admonition:: Question 4a  (4 pts)
   :class: note

   Determine the distribution of :math:`Z` and its parameter(s).

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      Zhenghao walks at a **constant pace** around the 100 m path, and the observation time is chosen uniformly over the entire lap. A uniform observation time on a constant-speed path produces a uniform position on the path:

      .. math::

         Z \sim \text{Uniform}(a = 0,\; b = 100)

.. admonition:: Question 4b  (4 pts)
   :class: note

   What is the probability that Zhenghao is walking up the path at the observation time?

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      "Walking up" corresponds to the left-side segment, which spans distances 30 m to 50 m along the path.

      .. math::

         P(30 < Z < 50) = \frac{50 - 30}{100 - 0} = \frac{20}{100} = \boxed{0.2}

.. admonition:: Question 4c  (10 pts)
   :class: note

   Based on Haoyu's speed, the pdf of :math:`H` is:

   .. math::

      f_H(x) = \begin{cases} k, & 0 < x \leq 30 \\ 2k, & 30 < x \leq 50 \\ k, & 50 < x \leq 80 \\ k/2, & 80 < x \leq 100 \\ 0, & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}

   Determine the value of :math:`k` that makes :math:`f_H(x)` a valid pdf.

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      The pdf must integrate to 1. Since each piece is constant, each integral equals (interval length) × (density):

      .. math::

         30k + 20(2k) + 30k + 20\!\left(\frac{k}{2}\right) = 1

      .. math::

         30k + 40k + 30k + 10k = 110k = 1

      .. math::

         k = \frac{1}{110} \approx \boxed{0.0091}

      **Intuition:** The density is highest on [30, 50] (the up-slope) because Haoyu moves slowly there — a uniformly chosen observation time is more likely to catch him in segments where he spends more time.

.. admonition:: Question 4d  (6 pts)
   :class: note

   What is the probability that Haoyu has traveled between 40 m and 70 m from Dr. Reese?

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      The interval [40, 70] spans two pieces of the pdf:

      .. math::

         P(40 < H < 70) = \int_{40}^{50} 2k\,dx + \int_{50}^{70} k\,dx = 2k(10) + k(20) = \frac{20}{110} + \frac{20}{110} = \frac{40}{110} = \boxed{0.3636}

.. admonition:: Question 4e  (4 pts)
   :class: note

   Compare the average travel distance of Zhenghao and Haoyu. Who travels farther on average?

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Zhenghao:** :math:`Z \sim \text{Uniform}(0, 100)`, so :math:`E[Z] = (0 + 100)/2 = 50` m.

      **Haoyu:**

      .. math::

         E[H] = \int_0^{100} x\,f_H(x)\,dx = k\int_0^{30} x\,dx + 2k\int_{30}^{50} x\,dx + k\int_{50}^{80} x\,dx + \frac{k}{2}\int_{80}^{100} x\,dx

      .. math::

         = \frac{1}{110}\cdot\frac{30^2}{2} + \frac{2}{110}\cdot\frac{50^2 - 30^2}{2} + \frac{1}{110}\cdot\frac{80^2-50^2}{2} + \frac{1}{220}\cdot\frac{100^2-80^2}{2}

      Using the weighted-midpoint shortcut (weight = probability in each segment):

      .. math::

         E[H] = 15 \cdot \frac{30}{110} + 40 \cdot \frac{40}{110} + 65 \cdot \frac{30}{110} + 90 \cdot \frac{10}{110} = \frac{450 + 1600 + 1950 + 900}{110} = \frac{4900}{110} = \boxed{44.5455}\ \text{m}

      Since :math:`E[Z] = 50 > 44.5455 = E[H]`, **Zhenghao travels farther on average**.

      **Intuition:** The observation time is uniform, but Haoyu moves slowly on the uphill segment [30, 50], so more observation times catch him there (closer to the start). This pulls his average observed position below 50 m.

      .. admonition:: Note on solution key ⚠️
         :class: danger

         The solution key's "Easier Calculation" section states :math:`E[H] = 4900/110 = 45.5455`. This is a transcription error — :math:`4900/110 = 44.5455`, not 45.5455. Both the exact integral and the midpoint method confirm :math:`E[H] = 44.5455`.

.. admonition:: Question 4f  (3 pts)
   :class: note

   Suppose Zhenghao and Haoyu now walk **counterclockwise** instead of clockwise. Which of the following quantities will change?

   - (A) The expected value of :math:`Z`
   - (B) The expected value of :math:`H`
   - (C) The variance of :math:`Z`
   - (D) The variance of :math:`H`
   - (E) All of the above

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Answer: (B)**

      - **:math:`Z`** walks at constant speed on the same 100 m path. A uniform observation time still produces :math:`Z \sim \text{Uniform}(0, 100)`. Both :math:`E[Z]` and :math:`\text{Var}(Z)` are unchanged.
      - **:math:`H`**: Reversing direction swaps which segments are "up" and "down." In the counterclockwise direction, the downhill segment (fast, low density) would now come **earlier** in the path, while the uphill segment (slow, high density) would come **later**. This shifts the high-density region toward larger distances, increasing :math:`E[H]`. The shape of the pdf changes, so both :math:`E[H]` and :math:`\text{Var}(H)` change.

      Since the question asks which quantities **will** change, and both :math:`E[H]` and :math:`\text{Var}(H)` change when the direction reverses, a complete answer is (B) and (D). The solution key marks **(B)** only, emphasizing the expected value shift.

--------------------------------------

.. admonition:: Question 5a  (6 pts)
   :class: note

   Determine the value of :math:`m` (replications per technique).

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      From the ANOVA table: :math:`\text{df}_T = n - 1 = 87`, so :math:`n = 88`. With :math:`k = 4` techniques:

      .. math::

         n = 4m \implies 88 = 4m \implies \boxed{m = 22}

.. admonition:: Question 5b  (2 pts)
   :class: note

   Check whether the constant variance assumption is satisfied using the summary statistics.

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Answer: Satisfied.**

      Apply the rule of thumb — the ratio of the largest to smallest sample standard deviation should not exceed 2:

      .. math::

         \frac{s_{\max}}{s_{\min}} = \frac{6.3}{3.8} = 1.6579 \leq 2

      The homogeneity of variance assumption holds.

.. admonition:: Question 5c  (5 pts)
   :class: note

   Using the ANOVA output and assuming all four techniques share the same error variance, compute the pooled estimate of the error **standard deviation**.

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      The pooled estimate of :math:`\sigma` is :math:`\hat{\sigma} = \sqrt{\text{MSE}}`:

      .. math::

         \hat{\sigma} = \sqrt{\text{MSE}} = \sqrt{24.7575} = \boxed{4.9757}

.. admonition:: Question 5d  (5 pts)
   :class: note

   Now ignore technique labels and treat all :math:`n = 88` measurements as one combined sample. Compute the standard deviation of this combined sample.

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      The combined-sample standard deviation uses :math:`\text{SST}` and :math:`n - 1`:

      .. math::

         \hat{\sigma}^* = \sqrt{\frac{\text{SST}}{n - 1}} = \sqrt{\frac{3011.09}{87}} \approx \sqrt{34.6103} \approx \boxed{5.8830}

.. admonition:: Question 5e  (3 pts)
   :class: note

   Which statement best describes the difference between the standard deviations in parts (c) and (d)?

   - (A) Both measure exactly the same quantity, just computed differently.
   - (B) Part (c) measures variability between technique means; part (d) measures variability within each technique.
   - (C) Part (c) measures how much all observations vary around a single overall mean; part (d) measures how much observations vary around their own technique's mean.
   - (D) Part (c) measures how much observations vary around their own technique's mean (assuming common error variance); part (d) measures how much all observations vary around a single overall mean when technique labels are ignored.

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Answer: (D)**

      - :math:`\hat{\sigma} = \sqrt{\text{MSE}}` in part (c) pools within-group variability — it estimates how much individual observations deviate from **their own technique's mean**, assuming that variance is common across techniques.
      - :math:`\hat{\sigma}^* = \sqrt{\text{SST}/(n-1)}` in part (d) is the ordinary sample standard deviation of all 88 measurements pooled, measuring how much they deviate from the **single grand mean** when technique labels are disregarded.

      :math:`\hat{\sigma}^*` will always be at least as large as :math:`\hat{\sigma}` (here 5.8830 > 4.9757) because it includes both within-group and between-group variability, while :math:`\hat{\sigma}` captures only within-group variability.

.. admonition:: Question 5f  (2 pts)
   :class: note

   Provide the first two steps of the four-step hypothesis testing procedure.

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Step 1 — Parameters of interest:**

      Let :math:`\mu_{\text{Reg}}`, :math:`\mu_{\text{ANOVA}}`, :math:`\mu_{\text{Taguchi}}`, :math:`\mu_{\text{SEM}}` denote the true mean effectiveness scores for each of the four statistical techniques at capturing hidden correlations between depression indices and human health indicators relevant to women's health in Indiana.

      **Step 2 — Hypotheses:**

      .. math::

         H_0\colon \mu_{\text{Reg}} = \mu_{\text{ANOVA}} = \mu_{\text{Taguchi}} = \mu_{\text{SEM}}

      .. math::

         H_a\colon \mu_i \neq \mu_j \text{ for some } i \neq j

      In words: :math:`H_0` states that all four techniques have the same true mean effectiveness. :math:`H_a` states that at least two techniques have different true mean effectiveness scores.

.. admonition:: Question 5g  (5 pts)
   :class: note

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      The data give support (:math:`p`-value :math:`= 7.465 \times 10^{-7}`) to the claim that the true mean effectiveness at capturing hidden correlations between depression indices and human health indicators differs across at least two of the four statistical techniques.

Problem 6 — Simple Linear Regression (38 points)
--------------------------------------------------

.. admonition:: Question 6a  (10 pts)
   :class: note

   Compute the least-squares regression line for predicting weekly sales from weekly advertising spending.

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      :math:`\bar{x} = 72/8 = 9`, :math:`\bar{y} = 77/8 = 9.625`.

      .. math::

         S_{xy} = \sum x_i y_i - n\bar{x}\bar{y} = 768 - 8(9)(9.625) = 768 - 693 = 75

      .. math::

         S_{xx} = \sum x_i^2 - n\bar{x}^2 = 816 - 8(9)^2 = 816 - 648 = 168

      **Slope:**

      .. math::

         b_1 = \frac{S_{xy}}{S_{xx}} = \frac{75}{168} = \boxed{0.4464}

      **Intercept:**

      .. math::

         b_0 = \bar{y} - b_1\bar{x} = 9.625 - 0.4464(9) = 9.625 - 4.0179 = \boxed{5.6071}

      **Fitted regression equation:**

      .. math::

         \hat{y} = 5.6071 + 0.4464\,x_{\text{Ad Spending}}

      **Interpretation of slope:** For each additional $100 in weekly advertising spending, the estimated mean weekly sales **increase** by approximately **$446.40** (i.e., 0.4464 thousand dollars).

.. admonition:: Question 6g  (5 pts)

   Is there a significant linear association between advertising spending and sales at :math:`\alpha = 0.01`? State the hypotheses and provide a formal conclusion.

      Let :math:`\beta_1` be the true slope of the linear relationship between weekly advertising spending and weekly sales.

      **Step 2 — Hypotheses:**

      .. math::

         H_0\colon \beta_1 = 0 \quad \text{(no linear association between ad spending and sales)}

      .. math::

         H_a\colon \beta_1 \neq 0 \quad \text{(there is a linear association)}
