Indicate the correct answer by completely filling in the appropriate circle. If you indicate

.. admonition:: Question 1.1  (2 pts)
   :class: note

   The boxplot below visually displays the summary information for a dataset.

   .. figure:: https://yjjpfnblgtrogqvcjaon.supabase.co/storage/v1/object/public/stat-350-assets/images/Exams/Exam1/SPRING2025/image1.png
      :alt: A horizontal modified boxplot. Three explicit points (outliers) appear to the left of the lower whisker, indicating lower-end outliers. The lower whisker extends right from the outliers to the left edge of the box. The box (IQR) sits in the centre with a vertical median line. The upper whisker extends right from the box to a capped endpoint. No outliers on the upper (right) side.
      :align: center
      :width: 60%

   **T or F:** According to the boxplot above, a lower inner fence must be located between
   the lower whisker and the largest outlier.

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Answer: TRUE**

      Recall how the modified boxplot is constructed:

      - The **lower inner fence** is defined as :math:`Q_1 - 1.5 \times IQR`.
      - The **lower whisker** extends left from :math:`Q_1` to the smallest observed value
        that is still at or above the lower inner fence.
      - Any data point below the lower inner fence is plotted as an explicit point (outlier).

      The left-to-right arrangement is therefore:

      .. math::

         \underbrace{\text{most extreme outlier}}_{\text{farthest left}}
         \;\cdots\;
         \underbrace{\text{largest outlier}}_{\text{closest to fence}}
         \;<\;
         \underbrace{\text{lower inner fence}}_{}
         \;\leq\;
         \underbrace{\text{lower whisker endpoint}}_{}
         \;\leq\; Q_1.

      The lower inner fence lies between the largest outlier (to its left) and the lower
      whisker endpoint (to its right). The statement is **TRUE**.

.. admonition:: Question 1.2  (2 pts)
   :class: note

   The Binomial distribution is symmetric when

   **T or F:** the probability of success :math:`p` is close to 0 or 1.

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Answer: FALSE**

      A :math:`\text{Binomial}(n, p)` distribution is symmetric when :math:`p = 0.5`.
      When :math:`p = 0.5`, every outcome :math:`x` and its mirror :math:`n - x` are equally
      probable, making the PMF perfectly symmetric about :math:`n/2`.

      When :math:`p` is close to 0, the distribution is strongly right-skewed. When :math:`p`
      is close to 1, the distribution is strongly left-skewed. The statement is **FALSE**.

.. admonition:: Question 1.3  (2 pts)
   :class: note

   Suppose :math:`Y` is the outcome of a single roll of a 6-sided die with an unknown
   probability mass function having nonzero variance. The outcome for a random variable
   :math:`X` is obtained by throwing this die once then multiplying the resulting number by 3,
   i.e., :math:`X = 3Y`. The outcome for another random variable :math:`Z` is obtained by
   throwing the same die three times, then adding the results together, i.e.,
   :math:`Z = Y_1 + Y_2 + Y_3` where :math:`Y_1, Y_2, Y_3` are independent copies of
   :math:`Y`. Then,

   **T or F:** it follows that :math:`\text{Var}(X) = \text{Var}(Z)`.

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Answer: FALSE**

      **Variance of X:**

      .. math::

         \text{Var}(X) = \text{Var}(3Y) = 3^2\,\text{Var}(Y) = 9\,\text{Var}(Y).

      **Variance of Z:**

      Since :math:`Y_1, Y_2, Y_3` are independent:

      .. math::

         \text{Var}(Z) = \text{Var}(Y_1) + \text{Var}(Y_2) + \text{Var}(Y_3) = 3\,\text{Var}(Y).

      Since :math:`\text{Var}(Y) > 0`:

      .. math::

         \text{Var}(X) = 9\,\text{Var}(Y) \neq 3\,\text{Var}(Y) = \text{Var}(Z).

      The statement is **FALSE**.

      .. admonition:: Intuition
         :class: tip

         Multiplying a single roll by 3 amplifies spread by :math:`3^2 = 9`. Adding three
         independent rolls grows variance only linearly (factor of 3). Despite having the
         same expected value, :math:`X` is much more variable than :math:`Z`.

.. admonition:: Question 1.4  (2 pts)
   :class: note

   Let :math:`X` denote a normal random variable, then regardless of the value of
   :math:`E[X]` and :math:`\text{Var}(X)`,

   **T or F:**
   :math:`P\!\left(E[X] - 2\sqrt{\text{Var}(X)} < X < E[X] + 2\sqrt{\text{Var}(X)}\right) \approx 0.95`
   is always true.

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Answer: TRUE**

      Let :math:`\mu = E[X]` and :math:`\sigma = \sqrt{\text{Var}(X)}`. Standardize:

      .. math::

         P(\mu - 2\sigma < X < \mu + 2\sigma)
         = P\!\left(-2 < \frac{X - \mu}{\sigma} < 2\right)
         = P(-2 < Z < 2),

      where :math:`Z \sim N(0,1)`. From the standard normal table:

      .. math::

         P(-2 < Z < 2) = \Phi(2) - \Phi(-2) = 0.9772 - 0.0228 = 0.9544 \approx 0.95.

      This holds for any normal random variable regardless of :math:`\mu` or :math:`\sigma`.
      The statement is **TRUE**.

.. admonition:: Question 1.5  (2 pts)
   :class: note

   Let :math:`X` be an exponential random variable. Then,

   **T or F:** the distribution of :math:`X` models the probability associated with the
   total number of events occurring during a fixed interval of time.

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Answer: FALSE**

      The Exponential distribution models the **waiting time** between successive events in a
      Poisson process — it is continuous, taking values in :math:`[0, \infty)`.

      The distribution that models the **number of events** in a fixed time interval is the
      **Poisson** distribution, which is discrete.

      .. list-table:: Poisson vs. Exponential
         :header-rows: 1
         :widths: 30 35 35

         * - Feature
           - Poisson(:math:`\lambda`)
           - Exponential(:math:`\lambda`)
         * - Models
           - Number of events in fixed time
           - Waiting time until next event
         * - Type
           - Discrete
           - Continuous
         * - Mean
           - :math:`\lambda`
           - :math:`1/\lambda`

      The statement is **FALSE**.

.. admonition:: Question 1.6  (2 pts)
   :class: note

   Let :math:`X` be a continuous random variable with finite expected value :math:`\mu` and
   variance :math:`\sigma^2`. Define a new random variable :math:`Y = aX + b` where
   :math:`a, b` are real numbers with :math:`a \neq 0`.

   **T or F:** It follows that
   :math:`E[Y^2] = a^2(\sigma^2 + \mu^2) + 2ab\mu + b^2`
   always holds, regardless of the distribution of :math:`X`.

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Answer: TRUE**

      Expand :math:`Y^2 = (aX+b)^2 = a^2X^2 + 2abX + b^2` and apply linearity of expectation:

      .. math::

         E[Y^2] = a^2\,E[X^2] + 2ab\,E[X] + b^2.

      Substitute :math:`E[X] = \mu` and :math:`E[X^2] = \text{Var}(X) + (E[X])^2 = \sigma^2 + \mu^2`:

      .. math::

         E[Y^2] = a^2(\sigma^2 + \mu^2) + 2ab\mu + b^2.

      This follows from the definitions of expectation and variance alone — no assumptions
      about the distribution of :math:`X` are required. The statement is **TRUE**.

.. admonition:: Question 2.1  (3 pts)
   :class: note

   Which of the following provides the best measures of center and spread respectively,
   based on the boxplots below?

   .. figure:: https://yjjpfnblgtrogqvcjaon.supabase.co/storage/v1/object/public/stat-350-assets/images/Exams/Exam1/SPRING2025/image2.png
      :alt: Two side-by-side modified boxplots. Boxplot 1 (left): data range roughly 5 to 16, two explicit points to the right of the upper whisker indicating upper outliers, box shifted left indicating right skew. Boxplot 2 (right): data range roughly negative 5 to 0, three explicit points to the left of the lower whisker plus one additional outlier, box shifted right indicating left skew.
      :align: center
      :width: 85%

   - **(A)** Boxplot 1: sample mean, interquartile range (IQR)
   - **(B)** Boxplot 1: sample median, range
   - **(C)** Boxplot 1: sample mean, sample standard deviation
   - **(D)** Boxplot 2: sample median, interquartile range (IQR)
   - **(E)** Boxplot 2: sample median, range
   - **(F)** Boxplot 2: sample mean, sample standard deviation

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Answer: (D)**

      Both distributions are skewed and contain outliers. The sample mean and standard
      deviation are non-resistant and are pulled toward extreme values. The median and IQR
      are resistant summaries better suited to skewed or outlier-prone data.

      Only **(D)** correctly pairs a resistant measure of center (median) with a resistant
      measure of spread (IQR) for a distribution that is skewed and contains outliers.

      **Why the Boxplot 1 options fail:**

      - **(A)** uses the sample **mean** on Boxplot 1, which is pulled upward by the two
        upper outliers. The median would be the appropriate measure of center here.
      - **(B)** pairs the sample median (good choice) with the **range** (poor choice). The
        range is entirely determined by the two most extreme values and is the least resistant
        measure of spread possible.
      - **(C)** uses both the sample **mean** and **standard deviation**, both of which are
        non-resistant to the upper outliers in Boxplot 1.

      **Why the remaining Boxplot 2 options fail:**

      - **(E)** correctly identifies the sample median as the measure of center, but pairs it
        with the **range**, which is dominated by the four lower outliers in Boxplot 2.
      - **(F)** uses the sample **mean** and **standard deviation**, both of which are heavily
        distorted by the four lower outliers visible in Boxplot 2.

.. admonition:: Question 2.2  (3 pts)
   :class: note

   Assume :math:`X \sim N(\mu, \sigma)`, :math:`Y \sim \text{Exp}(\lambda)`, and
   :math:`Z \sim \text{Bin}(n, p)`. Which of the following statements is **TRUE**?

   - **(A)** For the parameters of :math:`X` the ratio :math:`\mu/\sigma` must be greater than 1.
   - **(B)** For any :math:`y` in the support of :math:`Y` there exists an :math:`x` in the
     support of :math:`X` such that :math:`x = y`.
   - **(C)** The variance of :math:`Y` is the same as the mean of :math:`Y`.
   - **(D)** If :math:`p < 0.5`, then :math:`Z` can never take any values greater than
     :math:`n/2`. In other words, :math:`Z` is supported only on integers strictly less
     than :math:`n/2`.
   - **(E)** The parameter :math:`\lambda` must be a positive integer.

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Answer: (B)**

      **(A) FALSE.** The only constraint on normal parameters is :math:`\sigma > 0`. The mean
      :math:`\mu` may be any real number, including negative values. For example,
      :math:`X \sim N(\mu = -5,\; \sigma = 10)` is perfectly valid, giving
      :math:`\mu/\sigma = -0.5`, which is not greater than 1.

      **(B) TRUE.** The support of :math:`Y \sim \text{Exp}(\lambda)` is :math:`[0, \infty)`.
      The support of :math:`X \sim N(\mu, \sigma)` is all of :math:`(-\infty, \infty)`. Since
      :math:`(-\infty, \infty) \supset [0, \infty)`, every non-negative value in the support
      of :math:`Y` is also in the support of :math:`X`. The statement is **TRUE**.

      **(C) FALSE.** For :math:`Y \sim \text{Exp}(\lambda)`:

      .. math::

         E[Y] = \frac{1}{\lambda}, \qquad \text{Var}(Y) = \frac{1}{\lambda^2}.

      The claim is that :math:`\text{Var}(Y) = E[Y]`, i.e., :math:`1/\lambda^2 = 1/\lambda`.
      This holds only when :math:`\lambda = 1`. For any other value of :math:`\lambda`,
      :math:`\text{Var}(Y) \neq E[Y]`.

      **(D) FALSE.** The support of :math:`Z \sim \text{Bin}(n,p)` is :math:`\{0, 1, \ldots, n\}`
      for any :math:`p \in (0,1)`, regardless of whether :math:`p < 0.5`. When :math:`p < 0.5`
      the distribution is right-skewed, but values near :math:`n` still have positive
      probability — for example, :math:`P(Z = n) = p^n > 0`.

      **(E) FALSE.** The Exponential rate parameter :math:`\lambda` must only be **positive**
      (:math:`\lambda > 0`); it need not be an integer. For example,
      :math:`\lambda = 0.3` or :math:`\lambda = 2.7` are both valid.

.. admonition:: Question 2.3  (3 pts)
   :class: note

   Harry works at Hogwarts mail center. The number of owls that he receives in an hour at
   the center (:math:`X`) follows Poisson distribution with an average hourly rate of 1. In
   other words, :math:`X \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda = 1)`. Which of the following is
   **not** true?

   - **(A)** The probability that Harry receives :math:`x` owls in an hour is
     :math:`\dfrac{1^x e^{-1}}{x!}`.
   - **(B)** The probability that Harry receives 1 owl in an hour is approximately 0.3679.
   - **(C)** The probability that Harry receives 2 owls in an hour is approximately 0.1839.
   - **(D)** The probability that Harry receives more than 2 owls in an hour is approximately
     0.2642.
   - **(E)** The probability that Harry receives 1 owl in the first hour of his shift then
     zero in the second hour is approximately 0.1353.

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Answer: (D)**

      With :math:`\lambda = 1`, the PMF is :math:`P(X = x) = e^{-1}/x!`.

      **(A) TRUE.** Substituting :math:`\lambda = 1` into the Poisson PMF formula
      :math:`P(X=x) = e^{-\lambda}\lambda^x / x!` gives
      :math:`P(X=x) = e^{-1} \cdot 1^x / x! = 1^x e^{-1} / x!`. ✓

      **(B) TRUE.** :math:`P(X=1) = e^{-1}/1! = e^{-1} \approx 0.3679`.

      **(C) TRUE.** :math:`P(X=2) = e^{-1}/2! = e^{-1}/2 \approx 0.1839`.

      **(D) NOT TRUE.** Compute :math:`P(X > 2) = 1 - P(X \leq 2)`:

      .. math::

         P(X \leq 2) = e^{-1}\!\left(1 + 1 + \tfrac{1}{2}\right) = 2.5\,e^{-1} \approx 0.9197.

      .. math::

         P(X > 2) = 1 - 0.9197 = 0.0803 \neq 0.2642.

      **(E) TRUE.** By independence of the two hours:
      :math:`P(X_1=1)\cdot P(X_2=0) = e^{-1} \cdot e^{-1} = e^{-2} \approx 0.1353`.

.. admonition:: Question 2.4  (3 pts)
   :class: note

   If :math:`X` is a Poisson random variable that satisfies :math:`P(X=5) = P(X=7)`, then
   :math:`P(X=0) = {?}`

   - **(A)** :math:`P(X=0) = e^{0}`
   - **(B)** :math:`P(X=0) = e^{-5}`
   - **(C)** :math:`P(X=0) = e^{-7}`
   - **(D)** :math:`P(X=0) = e^{-\sqrt{35}}`
   - **(E)** :math:`P(X=0) = e^{-\sqrt{42}}`

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Answer: (E)**

      Set the two Poisson probabilities equal and solve for :math:`\lambda`:

      .. math::

         \frac{e^{-\lambda}\lambda^5}{5!} = \frac{e^{-\lambda}\lambda^7}{7!}.

      Cancel :math:`e^{-\lambda}` and :math:`\lambda^5` (both positive):

      .. math::

         \frac{1}{5!} = \frac{\lambda^2}{7!}
         \implies \lambda^2 = \frac{7!}{5!} = 7 \times 6 = 42
         \implies \lambda = \sqrt{42}.

      Therefore :math:`P(X=0) = e^{-\lambda} = e^{-\sqrt{42}}`. The answer is **(E)**.

.. admonition:: Question 2.5  (3 pts)
   :class: note

   In the standard Normal distribution, for any :math:`z > 0`, how does the probability
   compare between the two regions :math:`-z < Z < 0` and :math:`0 < Z < z`? Determine the
   correct symbol connecting the two probability statements below (fill in the blank).

   .. math::

      P(-z < Z < 0) \quad \underline{\hspace{1cm}} \quad P(0 < Z < z)

   - **(A)** :math:`<`
   - **(B)** :math:`>`
   - **(C)** :math:`=`
   - **(D)** :math:`\neq`
   - **(E)** :math:`\subset`

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Answer: (C)**

      The standard normal PDF is symmetric about zero: :math:`f(-t) = f(t)` for all :math:`t`.
      Therefore:

      .. math::

         P(-z < Z < 0) = \Phi(0) - \Phi(-z) = 0.5 - (1 - \Phi(z)) = \Phi(z) - 0.5,

      .. math::

         P(0 < Z < z) = \Phi(z) - \Phi(0) = \Phi(z) - 0.5.

      Both expressions equal :math:`\Phi(z) - 0.5`, so the correct symbol is :math:`=`.

.. admonition:: Question 3a  (6 pts)
   :class: note

   Given that STAT HS will hold at least one soccer game this month, what is the probability
   that it holds exactly three?

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      We want :math:`P(N_G = 3 \mid N_G \geq 1,\;\text{STAT HS})`. Writing this out fully
      using the definition of conditional probability:

      .. math::

         P(N_G = 3 \mid N_G \geq 1,\;\text{STAT HS})
         &= \frac{P(\{N_G = 3\} \cap \{N_G \geq 1\} \cap \{\text{STAT HS}\})}{P(\{N_G \geq 1\} \cap \{\text{STAT HS}\})} \\[6pt]
         &= \frac{P(N_G = 3 \mid \text{STAT HS})\,P(\text{STAT HS})}{P(N_G \geq 1 \mid \text{STAT HS})\,P(\text{STAT HS})}.

      The factor :math:`P(\text{STAT HS}) = 0.74` appears in both numerator and denominator
      and cancels, leaving:

      .. math::

         P(N_G = 3 \mid N_G \geq 1,\;\text{STAT HS})
         = \frac{P(N_G = 3 \mid \text{STAT HS})}{P(N_G \geq 1 \mid \text{STAT HS})}.

      **Numerator:**

      .. math::

         P(N_G = 3 \mid \text{STAT HS}) = 0.30.

      **Denominator:**

      .. math::

         P(N_G \geq 1 \mid \text{STAT HS}) = 1 - P(N_G = 0 \mid \text{STAT HS}) = 1 - 0.05 = 0.95.

      **Result:**

      .. math::

         P(N_G = 3 \mid N_G \geq 1,\;\text{STAT HS}) = \frac{0.30}{0.95} = \boxed{0.3158}.

----

.. admonition:: Question 3b  (6 pts)
   :class: note

   On any given month, what is the probability that at least one game is played?

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      Apply the **Law of Total Probability**, partitioning on the reservation holder:

      .. math::

         P(N_G \geq 1)
         = P(N_G \geq 1 \mid \text{STAT HS})\,P(\text{STAT HS})
         + P(N_G \geq 1 \mid \text{STAT MS})\,P(\text{STAT MS}).

      .. math::

         P(N_G \geq 1 \mid \text{STAT HS}) = 1 - 0.05 = 0.95, \qquad
         P(N_G \geq 1 \mid \text{STAT MS}) = 1 - 0.40 = 0.60.

      .. math::

         P(N_G \geq 1) = 0.95 \times 0.74 + 0.60 \times 0.26
                       = 0.7030 + 0.1560 = \boxed{0.8590}.

----

.. admonition:: Question 3c  (8 pts)
   :class: note

   Knowing that at least one game is held next month, what is the probability that the
   reservation is held by STAT MS?

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      Apply **Bayes' Theorem**:

      .. math::

         P(\text{STAT MS} \mid N_G \geq 1)
         = \frac{P(N_G \geq 1 \mid \text{STAT MS})\,P(\text{STAT MS})}{P(N_G \geq 1)}.

      **Numerator:**

      .. math::

         0.60 \times 0.26 = 0.1560.

      **Denominator** (from 3b):

      .. math::

         P(N_G \geq 1) = 0.8590.

      **Result:**

      .. math::

         P(\text{STAT MS} \mid N_G \geq 1) = \frac{0.1560}{0.8590} = \boxed{0.1816}.

----

.. admonition:: Question 3d  (6 pts)
   :class: note

   Are the reservation holder and the number of games played in a month independent?
   Justify your conclusion mathematically.

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **No — they are not independent.**

      From Question 3c:

      .. math::

         P(\text{STAT MS} \mid N_G \geq 1) = 0.1816 \neq 0.26 = P(\text{STAT MS}).

      Since knowing that at least one game was played changes the probability of STAT MS
      holding the reservation, the two are **not independent**.

      **Alternative (more direct) argument:** From the PMF, :math:`P(N_G = 3 \mid \text{STAT MS}) = 0.00`,
      so if STAT MS holds the reservation, we know for a fact that 3 games will not be played.
      Therefore the number of games played is not independent of who holds the reservation.

Problem 4  (26 points)
------------------------------------------------------------------

.. admonition:: Problem 4 Setup
   :class: important

   A zombie enthusiast is studying the walking speeds of classic zombies. Based on extensive
   observations, the enthusiast concludes that the speed of a classic zombie follows a normal
   distribution with:

   - Mean :math:`\mu = 2` miles per hour (mph)
   - Standard Deviation :math:`\sigma = 0.19` mph

   Use this information to address the following questions.

----

.. admonition:: Question 4a  (4 pts)
   :class: note

   What is the probability that a randomly chosen classic zombie walks faster than 2.3 mph?

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      Let :math:`F \sim N(\mu = 2,\;\sigma = 0.19)`. Standardize:

      .. math::

         P(F > 2.3) = P\!\left(Z > \frac{2.3 - 2}{0.19}\right) = P(Z > 1.58) = 1 - \Phi(1.58) = 1 - 0.9429 = \boxed{0.0571}.

----

.. admonition:: Question 4b  (6 pts)
   :class: note

   Given that a randomly chosen classic zombie walks faster than 2 mph, what is the
   probability that it also walks faster than 2.3 mph?

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      Since :math:`\{F > 2.3\} \subset \{F > 2\}`:

      .. math::

         P(F > 2.3 \mid F > 2) = \frac{P(F > 2.3)}{P(F > 2)}.

      **Numerator** (from 4a): :math:`P(F > 2.3) = 0.0571`.

      **Denominator:** Because :math:`\mu = 2`, :math:`P(F > 2) = P(Z > 0) = 0.5000`.

      .. math::

         P(F > 2.3 \mid F > 2) = \frac{0.0571}{0.5000} = \boxed{0.1142}.

----

.. admonition:: Question 4c  (6 pts)
   :class: note

   If the enthusiast randomly selects 10 classic zombies, what is the probability that at
   least one has a speed greater than 2.3 mph?

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      Let :math:`F_i \overset{\text{iid}}{\sim} N(2,\;0.19)` for :math:`i = 1, \ldots, 10`.
      Use the complement rule — it is much easier to compute the probability that
      *none* of the 10 zombies exceed 2.3 mph:

      .. math::

         P\!\left(\bigcup_{i=1}^{10}\{F_i > 2.3\}\right)
         &= 1 - P\!\left(\bigcap_{i=1}^{10}\{F_i \leq 2.3\}\right) \\
         &= 1 - \prod_{i=1}^{10} P(F_i \leq 2.3) \\
         &= 1 - [P(F \leq 2.3)]^{10} \\
         &= 1 - (1 - 0.0571)^{10} \\
         &= 1 - (0.9429)^{10} \\
         &= 1 - 0.5555 = \boxed{0.4445}.

      .. admonition:: Complement is key
         :class: tip

         Computing "at least one" directly would require summing :math:`P(\text{exactly } k
         \text{ exceed } 2.3)` for :math:`k = 1, 2, \ldots, 10` — ten separate binomial
         terms. The complement collapses this to a single calculation.

----

.. admonition:: Question 4d  (4 pts)
   :class: note

   Suppose two classic zombies both begin traveling at the same time. After 3 hours, what
   is the expected total distance they will have covered combined?

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      Since both classic zombies follow the same distribution with expected walking speed
      :math:`E[F] = \mu = 2` mph, and they both walk for 3 hours, each zombie will have
      covered an expected distance of:

      .. math::

         E[\text{distance per zombie}] = E[F] \times 3\;\text{hours} = 2 \times 3 = 6\;\text{miles}.

      By linearity of expectation, the expected **combined** distance is:

      .. math::

         E[D_1 + D_2] = E[D_1] + E[D_2] = 6 + 6 = \boxed{12\;\text{miles}}.

      Note that linearity of expectation holds regardless of whether :math:`D_1` and
      :math:`D_2` are independent — it is a universal property of expectation.

.. admonition:: Question 4e  (6 pts)
   :class: note

   A classic zombie is considered Elite if its speed is in the top 3%. Determine the minimum
   speed at which a classic zombie is classified as Elite.

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      "Top 3%" means :math:`P(F \leq f^*) = 0.97`.

      **Step 1:** From the z-table, :math:`z_{0.97} \approx 1.88`
      (since :math:`\Phi(1.88) = 0.9699 \approx 0.97`).

      **Step 2:** Transform back:

      .. math::

         f^* = \mu + z_{0.97} \cdot \sigma = 2 + 1.88 \times 0.19 = 2 + 0.3572 = \boxed{2.3572 \text{ mph}}.

Problem 5  (26 points)
------------------------------------------------------------------

.. admonition:: Problem 5 Setup
   :class: important

   At a busy taco truck, customers wait different amounts of time depending on order
   complexity and queue length. Let :math:`X` be the total time, in minutes, from joining
   the line to receiving food.

   No one is served in less than five minutes. The likelihood of finishing follows a parabolic
   pattern between five and seven and a half minutes, increasing until it peaks at seven and a
   half. After that, the rate of completion remains constant until twelve and a half minutes,
   when all orders are fulfilled.

   The proposed probability density function has the form

   .. math::

      f_X(x) = \begin{cases}
         k\,\bigl(6.25 - (x - 7.5)^2\bigr) & 5 \leq x < 7.5 \\[4pt]
         \dfrac{25}{4}\,k                   & 7.5 \leq x < 12.5 \\[4pt]
         0                                  & \text{otherwise}
      \end{cases}

   where :math:`k` must be determined so that :math:`f_X(x)` is a valid probability density
   function.

----

.. admonition:: Question 5a  (18 pts)
   :class: note

   Determine the value of :math:`k` such that the function :math:`f_X(x)` is a valid PDF.

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      A valid PDF must integrate to 1:

      .. math::

         \int_5^{7.5} k\,(6.25-(x-7.5)^2)\,dx
         + \int_{7.5}^{12.5} \frac{25}{4}\,k\,dx = 1.

      **Region 2** (constant piece):

      .. math::

         \int_{7.5}^{12.5} \frac{25}{4}\,k\,dx = \frac{25}{4}\,k \cdot 5 = \frac{125}{4}\,k.

      **Region 1** — substitute :math:`u = x - 7.5`:

      .. math::

         k\int_{-2.5}^{0}(6.25 - u^2)\,du
         = k\!\left[6.25u - \frac{u^3}{3}\right]_{-2.5}^{0}
         = k\!\left[0 - \left(-15.625 + \frac{15.625}{3}\right)\right]
         = \frac{125}{12}\,k.

      **Setting the total equal to 1:**

      .. math::

         \frac{125}{12}\,k + \frac{125}{4}\,k
         = \frac{125 + 375}{12}\,k
         = \frac{500}{12}\,k = 1
         \implies k = \frac{12}{500} = \frac{3}{125} = \boxed{0.0240}.

      **Verification:** Region 1 area :math:`= \tfrac{125}{12} \cdot \tfrac{3}{125} = 0.25`;
      Region 2 area :math:`= \tfrac{125}{4} \cdot \tfrac{3}{125} = 0.75`. Total = 1. ✓

----

The cumulative distribution function for the total time, in minutes, from joining the line
to receiving food is given by:

.. math::

   F_X(x) = \begin{cases}
      0                                                             & x < 5 \\[4pt]
      -\dfrac{1}{125}(x-7.5)^3 + \dfrac{3}{20}\,x - \dfrac{7}{8} & 5 \leq x < 7.5 \\[4pt]
      \dfrac{3}{20}\,x - \dfrac{7}{8}                              & 7.5 \leq x < 12.5 \\[4pt]
      1                                                             & x \geq 12.5
   \end{cases}

----

.. admonition:: Question 5b  (8 pts)
   :class: note

   The taco truck owner wants to know how long a typical customer waits before receiving
   their order. Instead of looking at the average, they are interested in the median wait
   time, the time by which half of all customers have received their food.

   Determine the median wait time :math:`\tilde{\mu}`, where
   :math:`P(X \leq \tilde{\mu}) = 0.5`.

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Step 1: Determine which piece of the CDF contains the median.**

      From the work in part (a), Region 1 (:math:`5 \leq x < 7.5`) accumulates a total area of

      .. math::

         \frac{125}{12}\,k = \frac{125}{12} \times \frac{3}{125} = \frac{1}{4} = 0.25.

      We can confirm this by evaluating :math:`F_X(7.5)`:

      .. math::

         F_X(7.5) = -\frac{1}{125}(0)^3 + \frac{3}{20}(7.5) - \frac{7}{8}
                  = 0 + 1.125 - 0.875 = 0.25.

      Since :math:`F_X(7.5) = 0.25 < 0.50`, the 50th percentile (median) must occur in
      Region 2 (:math:`7.5 \leq x < 12.5`), where :math:`F_X(x) = \dfrac{3}{20}\,x - \dfrac{7}{8}`.

      **Step 2: Solve** :math:`F_X(\tilde{\mu}) = 0.50`:

      .. math::

         \frac{3}{20}\,\tilde{\mu} - \frac{7}{8} = \frac{1}{2}
         \implies \frac{3}{20}\,\tilde{\mu} = \frac{11}{8}
         \implies \tilde{\mu} = \frac{11}{8} \times \frac{20}{3} = \frac{55}{6} \approx \boxed{9.1667 \text{ minutes}}.

      **Sanity check:** The constant density in Region 2 is :math:`0.15`. Area from 7.5 to
      9.1667 under this density: :math:`0.15 \times 1.6\overline{6} = 0.25`. Adding the 0.25
      from Region 1 gives a total of 0.50. ✓
