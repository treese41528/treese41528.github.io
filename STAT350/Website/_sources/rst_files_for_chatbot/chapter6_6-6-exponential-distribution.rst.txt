Exponential Distribution
=======================================

We now encounter the exponential distribution, which captures the essence of 
**decay and waiting times**.

.. admonition:: Road Map üß≠
   :class: important

   ‚Ä¢ Understand the **exponential distribution** as a model for waiting times between events.
   ‚Ä¢ Distinguish between **Poisson** (counting events) and **exponential** (measuring length of time between events) distributions.
   ‚Ä¢ Master the exponential **PDF and CDF**.
   ‚Ä¢ Explore the **memoryless property** of exponential random variables.
   ‚Ä¢ Learn about the **two ways of parameterizing** an exponential distribution: using the rate parameter :math:`\lambda` vs. the mean parameter :math:`\mu`.

From Event Counting to Waiting Times
---------------------------------------

Consider a hospital emergency room where patients arrive according to a Poisson process 
with an average rate of 3 patients per hour. The Poisson distribution tells us the probabilities 
of seeing 0, 1, 2, or more patients in any given hour. 
But suppose we ask instead: ‚ÄúGiven that a patient has just arrived, how long will it be until the next one arrives?‚Äù

This waiting time follows an exponential distribution. The connection is profound: 
if events occur according to a Poisson process with rate :math:`\lambda`, then 
the **time between consecutive events follows an exponential distribution** with the same rate parameter :math:`\lambda`.

Why "Exponential"?
~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~

The distribution gets its name from the patterns in its probability density function. 
It assigns the highest probability density to small wait times and exponentially decreasing density to higher wait times. 
This reflects an intuitive property of many real-world processes‚Äîshort wait times are much more 
likely than long wait times, but extremely long wait times, while rare, remain possible.

Mathematical Definition and Properties
-----------------------------------------

The Exponential PDF
~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~

A continuous random variable :math:`X` follows an exponential distribution if its probability density function is:

.. math::

   f_X(x) = \begin{cases}
   \lambda e^{-\lambda x} & \text{for } x \geq 0 \\
   0 & \text{for } x < 0
   \end{cases}

We write :math:`X \sim \text{Exp}(\lambda)` or :math:`X \sim \text{Exponential}(\lambda)`.

**Understanding the Components**

- **Rate parameter** :math:`\lambda`:
  Just like in Poisson distributions, :math:`\lambda` represents the average number of events per unit time
  and is therefore always positive. Higher values of :math:`\lambda` indicate more frequent events which lead to 
  shorter expected waiting times.

- **Exponential decay** :math:`e^{-\lambda x}` creates the characteristic decreasing curve.
- :math:`\text{supp}(X) =[0, \infty)` because waiting times cannot be negative. 
- The PDF starts at its maximum value :math:`\lambda` when :math:`x = 0` and **decreases exponentially**, 
  approaching but **never reaching zero** as :math:`x \to \infty`.

   :alt: Exponential PDF showing decay pattern
   :align: center
   :figwidth: 40%

The Expoenential CDF
~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~

The cumulative distribution function requires integrating the PDF. For any :math:`x \geq 0`,

.. math::

   F_X(x) &= P(X \leq x) = \int_0^x \lambda e^{-\lambda s} \, ds 
   = \lambda \left[ \frac{e^{-\lambda s}}{-\lambda} \right]_0^x\\ 
   &= \lambda \left[ \frac{e^{-\lambda x} - e^0}{-\lambda} \right]
   = \lambda \left[ \frac{e^{-\lambda x} - 1}{-\lambda} \right] = 1 - e^{-\lambda x}

Therefore, the exponential CDF is:

.. math::

   F_X(x) = \begin{cases}
   1 - e^{-\lambda x} & \text{for } x \geq 0 \\
   0 & \text{for } x < 0
   \end{cases}

.. figure:: https://yjjpfnblgtrogqvcjaon.supabase.co/storage/v1/object/public/stat-350-assets/images/chapter6/exponential-cdf.png
   :alt: Exponential CDF
   :align: center
   :figwidth: 40%

   An exponential CDF

**A Notable Property**

The exponential CDF approaches 1 as :math:`x \to \infty`, but technically only equals 1 in the limit.
This occurs because the PDF never actually reaches zero on the right tail. 

Expected Value and Variance
------------------------------

Expected Value
~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~

To find :math:`E[X]`, we compute:

.. math::

   E[X] = \int_0^{\infty} x \cdot \lambda e^{-\lambda x} \, dx

This requires integration by parts with :math:`u = x` and :math:`dv = \lambda e^{-\lambda x} dx`:

- :math:`du = dx`
- :math:`v = -e^{-\lambda x}`

.. math::

   E[X] = \left[ x (-e^{-\lambda x}) \right]_0^{\infty} - \int_0^{\infty} (-e^{-\lambda x}) \, dx

The first term equals zero since :math:`xe^{-\lambda x} \to 0` as :math:`x \to \infty` and :math:`x(-e^{-\lambda x})=0` at :math:`x = 0`, leaving:

.. math::

   E[X] = \int_0^{\infty} e^{-\lambda x} \, dx = \left[ \frac{e^{-\lambda x}}{-\lambda} \right]_0^{\infty} = \frac{1}{\lambda}.

Variance
~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~

For variance, we use :math:`\text{Var}(X) = E[X^2] - (E[X])^2`. Finding :math:`E[X^2]` requires another integration by parts,
which we leave as an independent exercise. 

.. math::

   E[X^2] = \int_0^{\infty} x^2 \cdot \lambda e^{-\lambda x} \, dx = \frac{2}{\lambda^2}

Therefore,

.. math::

   \text{Var}(X) = E[X^2] - (E[X])^2 = \frac{2}{\lambda^2} - \left(\frac{1}{\lambda}\right)^2 = \frac{2}{\lambda^2} - \frac{1}{\lambda^2} = \frac{1}{\lambda^2}.

Summary
~~~~~~~~~~

For :math:`X \sim \text{Exp}(\lambda)`,

.. math::

   E[X] = \frac{1}{\lambda} \quad \text{and} \quad \text{Var}(X) = \frac{1}{\lambda^2}.

The standard deviation is :math:`\sigma_X = \frac{1}{\lambda}`.

Interpretation
~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~

If events occur at the rate :math:`\lambda` per unit time, we expect to wait :math:`\frac{1}{\lambda}` 
time units on average until the next event. Higher rates mean shorter waiting times, and the variance 
decreases as the rate increases, indicating more predictable waiting times.

Two Parameterizations: Rate vs. Mean
-------------------------------------

The exponential distribution can be parameterized in two equivalent ways, each emphasizing 
different aspects of the underlying process.

Rate Parameterization
~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~

The rate parameterization uses :math:`\lambda > 0` as we've seen:

.. math::

   f_X(x) = \lambda e^{-\lambda x}, \quad x \geq 0

Here, :math:`\lambda` represents **the average number of events per unit time**. 
This parameterization is natural when we think about process rates: phone calls per minute, 
component failures per year, or customer arrivals per hour.

Mean Parameterization
~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~

The mean parameterization uses :math:`\mu = \frac{1}{\lambda}` as the parameter:

.. math::

   f_X(x) = \frac{1}{\mu} e^{-x/\mu}, \quad x \geq 0

Here, :math:`\mu` represents the **average waiting time between events**. 
This parameterization is natural when we think about typical waiting times: 
average time between phone calls, mean component lifetime, 
or expected time between customer arrivals.

Converting Between Parameterizations
~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~

The relationship :math:`\mu = \frac{1}{\lambda}` allows easy conversion:

- If events occur at rate :math:`\lambda = 3` per hour, the mean waiting time 
  is :math:`\mu = \frac{1}{3}` hour (20 minutes).
- If the mean waiting time is :math:`\mu = 2` minutes, the rate is 
  :math:`\lambda = \frac{1}{2}` events per minute (30 events per hour).

Choosing the Right Parameterization
~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~

Use the parameterization that matches your problem's natural description.

- Use **rate parameterization** when the problem gives or asks for rates ("failures per year", "arrivals per hour").
- Use **mean parameterization** when the problem gives or asks for typical waiting times ("mean time to failure", "average service time").

The Memoryless Property
----------------------------------

For any exponential random variable :math:`X` and any positive values :math:`s` and :math:`t`:

.. math::

   P(X > s + t \mid X > s) = P(X > t)

The memoryless property implies that if we've already waited time :math:`s` without an event occurring, 
the probability of waiting an additional time :math:`t` is **the same as if we were starting fresh**. 
The process **"forgets" how long we've already waited**‚Äîpast waiting time provides no information 
about future waiting time.

Proving the Memoryless Property
~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~

Using the definition of conditional probability,

.. math::

   P(X > s + t \mid X > s) = \frac{P(\{X > s + t\} \cap \{X > s\})}{P(X > s)} = \frac{P(X > s + t)}{P(X > s)}

From the exponential CDF :math:`F_X(x) = 1 - e^{-\lambda x}`, we get :math:`P(X > x) = e^{-\lambda x}`. Using this,

.. math::

   P(X > s + t \mid X > s) = \frac{e^{-\lambda(s+t)}}{e^{-\lambda s}} 
   = \frac{e^{-\lambda s} \cdot e^{-\lambda t}}{e^{-\lambda s}} = e^{-\lambda t} = P(X > t)‚úî

.. admonition:: Exampleüí°: Exponential Distribution
   :class: note 

   Customers arrive at a service desk with exponentially distributed inter-arrival times averaging 4 minutes.
   
   a) What's the probability that no customer arrives for the next 2 minutes?
      
      Let :math:`Y` denote the inter-arrival wait time. :math:`Y \sim \text{Exp}(\mu=4)`. The parameter
      is provided as an average time, so we use :math:`\mu`.

      .. math:: P(Y > 2) = 1 - F_Y(y) = 1 - (1 - e^{-2/4}) = e^{-2/4} = 0.6065

   b) If no customer has arrived in the last 6 minutes, what's the probability one arrives in the next minute?

      Begin by setting up the probability statement. We are looking for :math:`P(Y < 6 + 1 | Y > 6)`.
      Using memoryless property of exponential :math:`Y`, this is equal to :math:`P(Y < 1)`.

      .. math:: P(Y < 1) = 1 - e^{-1/4} = 0.2212

   c) Find the time :math:`t` such that 90% of inter-arrival times are less than :math:`t`.

      We need to find :math:`t` such that :math:`F_Y(t) = P(Y \leq t) = 0.9`. Replacing the left-hand side with
      the CDF of :math:`Y` and solving for :math:`t`,

      .. math:: 1 - e^{-t/4} = 0.9 \implies t = -4\cdot\text{ln}(0.1)=9.21.

      90% of the wait times at this service desk fall on or below 9.21 minutes.

Summary: Properties of Exponential Distribution
---------------------------------------------------

* **Notation**: :math:`X \sim \text{Exp}(\lambda)` or :math:`X \sim \text{Exponential}(\lambda)`

* **Parameter**: :math:`\lambda > 0` (rate parameter) or :math:`\mu = \frac{1}{\lambda} > 0` (mean parameter)

* **Support**: :math:`[0, \infty)`

* **PDF**: :math:`f_X(x) = \begin{cases} \lambda e^{-\lambda x} & \text{for } x \geq 0 \\ 0 & \text{for } x < 0 \end{cases}`

* **CDF**: :math:`F_X(x) = \begin{cases} 1 - e^{-\lambda x} & \text{for } x \geq 0 \\ 0 & \text{for } x < 0 \end{cases}`

* **Expected Value**: :math:`E[X] = \frac{1}{\lambda}`

* **Variance**: :math:`\text{Var}(X) = \frac{1}{\lambda^2}`

* **Standard Deviation**: :math:`\sigma_X = \frac{1}{\lambda}`

* **Memoryless Property**: :math:`P(X > s + t \mid X > s) = P(X > t)` for all :math:`s, t > 0`

Bringing It All Together
--------------------------

.. admonition:: Key Takeaways üìù
   :class: important

   1. The **exponential distribution** models waiting times between events in a Poisson process. 
      If events occur according to a Poisson process with rate :math:`\lambda`, then their inter-arrival 
      times follow :math:`\text{Exp}(\lambda)`.
   
   2. The **PDF** :math:`f_X(x) = \lambda e^{-\lambda x}, x \geq 0` creates an exponential decay pattern.
   
   3. The **CDF** is :math:`F_X(x) = 1 - e^{-\lambda x} , x \geq 0`.
   
   4. **Two parameterizations** exist: one using the rate :math:`\lambda` (events per time) and 
      the other using the mean :math:`\mu = \frac{1}{\lambda}` (average waiting time).
   
   5. The **memoryless property** :math:`P(X > s + t \mid X > s) = P(X > t)` uniquely 
      characterizes exponential distributions among continuous distributions.


..
   Working with Exponential Distributions in R
   -----------------------------------------------

   R provides comprehensive functions for working with exponential distributions, making calculations straightforward for both parameterizations.

   **The Four Essential R Functions**

   - **rexp()**: Generates random samples from an exponential distribution
   - **dexp()**: Calculates the probability density function (PDF values)
   - **pexp()**: Calculates the cumulative distribution function (CDF values)
   - **qexp()**: Finds quantiles (percentiles)

   **Basic Function Usage**

   .. code-block:: r

      # Generate random samples from Exp(rate = 2)
      set.seed(123)
      random_times <- rexp(n = 10, rate = 2)
      print(round(random_times, 3))
      
      # Calculate density at x = 1.5 for Exp(rate = 2)
      density_at_1.5 <- dexp(x = 1.5, rate = 2)
      print(paste("Density at x = 1.5:", round(density_at_1.5, 4)))
      
      # Calculate P(X ‚â§ 2) for Exp(rate = 2)
      prob_less_than_2 <- pexp(q = 2, rate = 2)
      print(paste("P(X ‚â§ 2) =", round(prob_less_than_2, 4)))
      
      # Find the median (50th percentile)
      median_value <- qexp(p = 0.5, rate = 2)
      print(paste("Median:", round(median_value, 3)))

   **Probability Calculations**

   .. code-block:: r

      # For X ~ Exp(rate = 0.5), calculate various probabilities
      
      # P(X > 3) using lower.tail = FALSE
      prob_greater_3 <- pexp(3, rate = 0.5, lower.tail = FALSE)
      print(paste("P(X > 3) =", round(prob_greater_3, 4)))
      
      # P(1 ‚â§ X ‚â§ 4) = P(X ‚â§ 4) - P(X ‚â§ 1)
      prob_between <- pexp(4, rate = 0.5) - pexp(1, rate = 0.5)
      print(paste("P(1 ‚â§ X ‚â§ 4) =", round(prob_between, 4)))
      
      # Demonstrate memoryless property
      # P(X > 5 | X > 2) should equal P(X > 3)
      conditional_prob <- pexp(5, rate = 0.5, lower.tail = FALSE) / 
                        pexp(2, rate = 0.5, lower.tail = FALSE)
      direct_prob <- pexp(3, rate = 0.5, lower.tail = FALSE)
      print(paste("P(X > 5 | X > 2) =", round(conditional_prob, 6)))
      print(paste("P(X > 3) =", round(direct_prob, 6)))

   **Visualizing Exponential Distributions**

   .. code-block:: r

      library(ggplot2)
      
      # Compare different rate parameters
      x_vals <- seq(0, 5, by = 0.1)
      
      # Create data for plotting
      plot_data <- data.frame(
      x = rep(x_vals, 3),
      density = c(dexp(x_vals, rate = 0.5),
                  dexp(x_vals, rate = 1),
                  dexp(x_vals, rate = 2)),
      rate = rep(c("Œª = 0.5", "Œª = 1", "Œª = 2"), each = length(x_vals))
      )
      
      # Plot PDFs
      ggplot(plot_data, aes(x = x, y = density, color = rate)) +
      geom_line(size = 1.2) +
      labs(title = "Exponential PDFs with Different Rates",
            x = "x", y = "Density", color = "Rate Parameter") +
      theme_minimal()
      
      # Plot CDFs
      cdf_data <- data.frame(
      x = rep(x_vals, 3),
      cdf = c(pexp(x_vals, rate = 0.5),
               pexp(x_vals, rate = 1),
               pexp(x_vals, rate = 2)),
      rate = rep(c("Œª = 0.5", "Œª = 1", "Œª = 2"), each = length(x_vals))
      )
      
      ggplot(cdf_data, aes(x = x, y = cdf, color = rate)) +
      geom_line(size = 1.2) +
      labs(title = "Exponential CDFs with Different Rates",
            x = "x", y = "Cumulative Probability", color = "Rate Parameter") +
      theme_minimal()

   **Applied Example: Equipment Failure Analysis**

   .. code-block:: r

      # Machine breakdowns follow Exp(rate = 1/90) per day
      # Mean time between failures = 90 days
      
      failure_rate <- 1/90  # failures per day
      
      # Calculate key probabilities
      prob_60_days <- pexp(60, rate = failure_rate, lower.tail = FALSE)
      print(paste("P(machine runs ‚â• 60 days) =", round(prob_60_days, 4)))
      
      # Memoryless property: already run 30 days, what's P(run 20 more)?
      prob_additional_20 <- pexp(20, rate = failure_rate, lower.tail = FALSE)
      print(paste("P(run additional 20 days | already 30 days) =", round(prob_additional_20, 4)))
      
      # Find 90th percentile (10% of machines fail after this time)
      percentile_90 <- qexp(0.9, rate = failure_rate)
      print(paste("90th percentile:", round(percentile_90, 1), "days"))
      
      # Simulate maintenance schedules
      set.seed(456)
      failure_times <- rexp(n = 100, rate = failure_rate)
      
      # Analyze simulation results
      mean_failure_time <- mean(failure_times)
      theoretical_mean <- 1/failure_rate
      
      print(paste("Simulated mean failure time:", round(mean_failure_time, 1), "days"))
      print(paste("Theoretical mean:", theoretical_mean, "days"))
      
      # Visualize failure time distribution
      failure_data <- data.frame(days = failure_times)
      
      ggplot(failure_data, aes(x = days)) +
      geom_histogram(bins = 20, fill = "lightblue", alpha = 0.7, aes(y = ..density..)) +
      geom_line(data = data.frame(x = seq(0, max(failure_times), length = 100)),
                  aes(x = x, y = dexp(x, rate = failure_rate)), 
                  color = "red", size = 1.2) +
      labs(title = "Machine Failure Times vs Theoretical Distribution",
            x = "Days Until Failure", y = "Density") +
      theme_minimal()

.. admonition:: Key Formulas
   :class: tip

   For :math:`X \sim \text{Exp}(\lambda)` where :math:`\lambda` is the **rate parameter**:

   - **PDF**: :math:`f_X(x) = \lambda e^{-\lambda x}` for :math:`x \geq 0`
   - **CDF**: :math:`F_X(x) = 1 - e^{-\lambda x}` for :math:`x \geq 0`
   - **Survival Function**: :math:`P(X > x) = e^{-\lambda x}`
   - **Expected Value**: :math:`E[X] = \frac{1}{\lambda}` (mean waiting time)
   - **Variance**: :math:`\text{Var}(X) = \frac{1}{\lambda^2}`
   - **Standard Deviation**: :math:`\sigma_X = \frac{1}{\lambda}` (uniquely for exponential distributions, SD = mean)
   - **Memoryless Property**: :math:`P(X > s + t \mid X > s) = P(X > t)`

.. admonition:: Parameterization Note
   :class: info

   The exponential distribution can be parameterized by rate :math:`\lambda` (events per unit time) or by mean :math:`\mu = \frac{1}{\lambda}` (average waiting time). With mean parameterization: :math:`f_X(x) = \frac{1}{\mu} e^{-x/\mu}` and :math:`F_X(x) = 1 - e^{-x/\mu}`.

.. admonition:: Exercise 1: Basic Exponential Calculations
   :class: note

   Server response times at a data center follow an exponential distribution with rate :math:`\lambda = 0.5` responses per second.

   a. Write out the PDF :math:`f_X(x)` and CDF :math:`F_X(x)`.

   b. Find :math:`P(X \leq 2)` ‚Äî the probability a response takes at most 2 seconds.

   c. Find :math:`P(X > 3)` ‚Äî the probability a response takes more than 3 seconds.

   d. Find :math:`P(1 < X < 4)` ‚Äî the probability a response takes between 1 and 4 seconds.

   e. Sketch the PDF and indicate the region corresponding to part (d).

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      Let :math:`X` = server response time (seconds), where :math:`X \sim \text{Exp}(\lambda = 0.5)`.

      **Part (a): PDF and CDF**

      .. math::
         f_X(x) = \begin{cases}
         0.5 e^{-0.5x} & \text{for } x \geq 0 \\
         0 & \text{for } x < 0
         \end{cases}

      .. math::
         F_X(x) = \begin{cases}
         1 - e^{-0.5x} & \text{for } x \geq 0 \\
         0 & \text{for } x < 0
         \end{cases}

      **Part (b): P(X ‚â§ 2)**

      .. math::
         P(X \leq 2) = F_X(2) = 1 - e^{-0.5(2)} = 1 - e^{-1} = 1 - 0.3679 = 0.6321

      **Part (c): P(X > 3)**

      Using the survival function:

      .. math::
         P(X > 3) = e^{-0.5(3)} = e^{-1.5} = 0.2231

      Or equivalently: :math:`P(X > 3) = 1 - F_X(3) = 1 - (1 - e^{-1.5}) = e^{-1.5} = 0.2231`.

      **Part (d): P(1 < X < 4)**

      .. math::
         P(1 < X < 4) = F_X(4) - F_X(1) = (1 - e^{-2}) - (1 - e^{-0.5}) = e^{-0.5} - e^{-2}

      .. math::
         = 0.6065 - 0.1353 = 0.4712

      **Part (e): PDF sketch**

      .. figure:: https://yjjpfnblgtrogqvcjaon.supabase.co/storage/v1/object/public/stat-350-assets/Exercises/ch6-6/fig1_basic_exponential.png
         :alt: Exponential PDF with shaded region
         :align: center
         :width: 60%

         The exponential PDF with P(1 < X < 4) = 0.4712 shaded.

.. admonition:: Exercise 2: Expected Value and Variance
   :class: note

   The time between arrivals at an emergency room follows an exponential distribution with rate :math:`\lambda = 4` patients per hour.

   a. Find the expected time between arrivals (in minutes).

   b. Find the variance and standard deviation of the time between arrivals.

   c. What is notable about the relationship between the mean and standard deviation for exponential distributions?

   d. If the arrival rate doubled to 8 patients per hour, how would the expected waiting time change?

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      Let :math:`X` = time between arrivals (hours), where :math:`X \sim \text{Exp}(\lambda = 4)`.

      **Part (a): Expected time between arrivals**

      .. math::
         E[X] = \frac{1}{\lambda} = \frac{1}{4} \text{ hour} = 15 \text{ minutes}

      **Part (b): Variance and standard deviation**

      .. math::
         \text{Var}(X) = \frac{1}{\lambda^2} = \frac{1}{16} \text{ hours}^2

      .. math::
         \sigma_X = \frac{1}{\lambda} = \frac{1}{4} \text{ hour} = 15 \text{ minutes}

      **Part (c): Mean equals standard deviation**

      For exponential distributions, :math:`E[X] = \sigma_X = \frac{1}{\lambda}`. This equality is a **unique property of the exponential distribution**‚Äîit does not hold for other continuous distributions. It reflects the high variability inherent in exponential waiting times: the coefficient of variation (CV = œÉ/Œº) is always exactly 1.

      **Part (d): Effect of doubling the rate**

      If :math:`\lambda = 8` patients per hour:

      .. math::
         E[X] = \frac{1}{8} \text{ hour} = 7.5 \text{ minutes}

      Doubling the arrival rate **halves** the expected waiting time. This inverse relationship is characteristic of exponential distributions.

.. admonition:: Exercise 3: Rate vs. Mean Parameterization
   :class: note

   Convert between parameterizations and express the PDF in both forms.

   a. A machine's time to failure has rate :math:`\lambda = 0.02` failures per day. Express this using the mean parameterization and find :math:`E[X]`.

   b. A call center has an average call duration of :math:`\mu = 6` minutes. Express this using the rate parameterization and write the PDF.

   c. Network packets arrive with a mean inter-arrival time of 50 milliseconds. Find the rate parameter and calculate :math:`P(X > 75)`.

   d. If :math:`\lambda = 1.5` events per hour, find :math:`P(X \leq 30 \text{ minutes})`.

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Part (a): Rate to mean parameterization**

      Given :math:`\lambda = 0.02` failures per day:

      .. math::
         \mu = \frac{1}{\lambda} = \frac{1}{0.02} = 50 \text{ days}

      Mean parameterization: :math:`X \sim \text{Exp}(\mu = 50)` where :math:`\mu` is mean time to failure.

      PDF: :math:`f_X(x) = \frac{1}{50} e^{-x/50}` for :math:`x \geq 0`.

      Expected value: :math:`E[X] = \mu = 50` days.

      **Part (b): Mean to rate parameterization**

      Given :math:`\mu = 6` minutes:

      .. math::
         \lambda = \frac{1}{\mu} = \frac{1}{6} \text{ calls per minute}

      Rate parameterization: :math:`X \sim \text{Exp}(\lambda = \frac{1}{6})`.

      PDF: :math:`f_X(x) = \frac{1}{6} e^{-x/6}` for :math:`x \geq 0`.

      **Part (c): Mean inter-arrival time to probability**

      Given :math:`\mu = 50` ms, so :math:`\lambda = \frac{1}{50} = 0.02` packets per ms.

      .. math::
         P(X > 75) = e^{-\lambda \cdot 75} = e^{-0.02 \times 75} = e^{-1.5} = 0.2231

      Alternatively, using mean parameterization directly:

      .. math::
         P(X > 75) = e^{-75/50} = e^{-1.5} = 0.2231

      **Part (d): Rate with unit conversion**

      Given :math:`\lambda = 1.5` events per hour. For 30 minutes = 0.5 hours:

      .. math::
         P(X \leq 0.5) = 1 - e^{-1.5 \times 0.5} = 1 - e^{-0.75} = 1 - 0.4724 = 0.5276

      Alternatively, convert rate to per-minute: :math:`\lambda = \frac{1.5}{60} = 0.025` per minute.

      .. math::
         P(X \leq 30) = 1 - e^{-0.025 \times 30} = 1 - e^{-0.75} = 0.5276

.. admonition:: Exercise 4: The Memoryless Property
   :class: note

   A light bulb's lifetime follows an exponential distribution with mean :math:`\mu = 1000` hours.

   a. Find the probability that a new bulb lasts more than 500 hours.

   b. Given that a bulb has already lasted 800 hours, find the probability it lasts at least 300 more hours.

   c. Verify that your answers to (a) and (b) demonstrate the memoryless property.

   d. Given that a bulb has lasted 1500 hours, what is the expected additional lifetime?

   e. Explain why the memoryless property might seem counterintuitive for light bulbs but is mathematically consistent.

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      Let :math:`X` = bulb lifetime (hours), where :math:`X \sim \text{Exp}(\mu = 1000)`, so :math:`\lambda = \frac{1}{1000} = 0.001`.

      **Part (a): P(X > 500) for a new bulb**

      .. math::
         P(X > 500) = e^{-500/1000} = e^{-0.5} = 0.6065

      **Part (b): P(X > 1100 | X > 800)** ‚Äî conditional probability

      We want :math:`P(X > 800 + 300 \mid X > 800)`.

      By the memoryless property: :math:`P(X > s + t \mid X > s) = P(X > t)`.

      .. math::
         P(X > 800 + 300 \mid X > 800) = P(X > 300) = e^{-300/1000} = e^{-0.3} = 0.7408

      **Part (c): Verify memoryless property**

      Let's compute :math:`P(X > 300)` directly:

      .. math::
         P(X > 300) = e^{-300/1000} = e^{-0.3} = 0.7408

      This matches part (b), confirming the memoryless property: the probability of surviving an additional 300 hours is the same whether the bulb is new or has already operated for 800 hours.

      **Part (d): Expected additional lifetime after 1500 hours**

      By the memoryless property, given that the bulb has survived to time :math:`s`, its remaining lifetime has the **same distribution** as a new bulb. Therefore:

      .. math::
         E[X - 1500 \mid X > 1500] = E[X] = \mu = 1000 \text{ hours}

      Even after 1500 hours of operation, the bulb "expects" to last another 1000 hours on average‚Äîexactly the same as a brand new bulb.

      **Part (e): Intuitive explanation**

      The memoryless property seems counterintuitive because physical light bulbs do wear out‚Äîtheir filaments degrade, making older bulbs more likely to fail. Real light bulb lifetimes are better modeled by Weibull or log-normal distributions.

      However, the memoryless property is mathematically consistent for idealized processes where failure occurs due to random shocks (not wear). It's appropriate for modeling radioactive decay, random equipment failures due to external factors, or certain types of electronic components.

      .. figure:: https://yjjpfnblgtrogqvcjaon.supabase.co/storage/v1/object/public/stat-350-assets/Exercises/ch6-6/fig4_memoryless.png
         :alt: Memoryless property visualization
         :align: center
         :width: 70%

         The memoryless property: conditional survival probabilities equal unconditional probabilities.

.. admonition:: Exercise 5: Finding Percentiles
   :class: note

   Download times for a software update follow an exponential distribution with rate :math:`\lambda = 0.1` per minute.

   a. Find the median download time.

   b. Find the 90th percentile of download times.

   c. Find the time by which 25% of downloads are complete.

   d. What proportion of downloads take longer than twice the mean?

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      Let :math:`X` = download time (minutes), where :math:`X \sim \text{Exp}(\lambda = 0.1)`.

      First note: :math:`E[X] = \frac{1}{0.1} = 10` minutes.

      **Part (a): Median (50th percentile)**

      Find :math:`x_{0.50}` such that :math:`F_X(x_{0.50}) = 0.50`:

      .. math::
         1 - e^{-0.1 x_{0.50}} = 0.50

      .. math::
         e^{-0.1 x_{0.50}} = 0.50

      .. math::
         -0.1 x_{0.50} = \ln(0.50) = -0.6931

      .. math::
         x_{0.50} = \frac{0.6931}{0.1} = 6.931 \text{ minutes}

      Note: The median (6.93 min) is less than the mean (10 min) because exponential distributions are right-skewed.

      **Part (b): 90th percentile**

      Find :math:`x_{0.90}` such that :math:`F_X(x_{0.90}) = 0.90`:

      .. math::
         1 - e^{-0.1 x_{0.90}} = 0.90

      .. math::
         e^{-0.1 x_{0.90}} = 0.10

      .. math::
         x_{0.90} = \frac{-\ln(0.10)}{0.1} = \frac{2.303}{0.1} = 23.03 \text{ minutes}

      90% of downloads complete within 23.03 minutes.

      **Part (c): 25th percentile**

      .. math::
         e^{-0.1 x_{0.25}} = 0.75

      .. math::
         x_{0.25} = \frac{-\ln(0.75)}{0.1} = \frac{0.2877}{0.1} = 2.877 \text{ minutes}

      25% of downloads complete within about 2.88 minutes.

      **Part (d): P(X > 2Œº) = P(X > 20)**

      .. math::
         P(X > 20) = e^{-0.1 \times 20} = e^{-2} = 0.1353

      About **13.5%** of downloads take longer than twice the mean. This is a fixed property of all exponential distributions: :math:`P(X > 2\mu) = e^{-2} \approx 0.135`.

.. admonition:: Exercise 6: Connection to Poisson Process
   :class: note

   Customers arrive at a coffee shop according to a Poisson process with rate :math:`\lambda = 12` customers per hour.

   a. What is the distribution of the time between consecutive customer arrivals?

   b. Find the probability that the time between two consecutive arrivals exceeds 10 minutes.

   c. Find the expected time between arrivals.

   d. If 5 minutes have passed since the last customer, what is the probability that the next customer arrives within the next 3 minutes?

   e. In a given hour, what is the probability that exactly 10 customers arrive? (This uses the Poisson distribution.)

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Part (a): Distribution of inter-arrival times**

      If arrivals follow a Poisson process with rate :math:`\lambda`, then the time between arrivals follows an **exponential distribution** with the same rate parameter.

      :math:`X \sim \text{Exp}(\lambda = 12)` where X is measured in hours.

      Or equivalently: :math:`X \sim \text{Exp}(\lambda = 0.2)` per minute (converting 12/hour to 12/60 = 0.2/minute).

      **Part (b): P(X > 10 minutes)**

      Using :math:`\lambda = 0.2` per minute:

      .. math::
         P(X > 10) = e^{-0.2 \times 10} = e^{-2} = 0.1353

      Or using :math:`\lambda = 12` per hour with 10 min = 1/6 hour:

      .. math::
         P(X > 1/6) = e^{-12 \times (1/6)} = e^{-2} = 0.1353

      **Part (c): Expected time between arrivals**

      .. math::
         E[X] = \frac{1}{\lambda} = \frac{1}{12} \text{ hour} = 5 \text{ minutes}

      **Part (d): Conditional probability (memoryless property)**

      We want :math:`P(X \leq 8 \mid X > 5)` where X is in minutes.

      By the memoryless property:

      .. math::
         P(X \leq 5 + 3 \mid X > 5) = P(X \leq 3)

      .. math::
         P(X \leq 3) = 1 - e^{-0.2 \times 3} = 1 - e^{-0.6} = 1 - 0.5488 = 0.4512

      **Part (e): Poisson probability for counts**

      The number of arrivals in one hour follows :math:`N \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda = 12)`.

      .. math::
         P(N = 10) = \frac{e^{-12} \cdot 12^{10}}{10!} = \frac{e^{-12} \cdot 61917364224}{3628800} = 0.1048

      .. figure:: https://yjjpfnblgtrogqvcjaon.supabase.co/storage/v1/object/public/stat-350-assets/Exercises/ch6-6/fig6_poisson_exponential.png
         :alt: Poisson-Exponential relationship
         :align: center
         :width: 70%

         The Poisson process: counts follow Poisson, inter-arrival times follow Exponential.

.. admonition:: Exercise 7: Comparing Exponential Distributions
   :class: note

   Two servers process requests. Server A has response times following :math:`\text{Exp}(\lambda_A = 2)` per second, while Server B has response times following :math:`\text{Exp}(\lambda_B = 0.5)` per second.

   a. Which server has faster average response times? By how much?

   b. For each server, find the probability that a response takes less than 1 second.

   c. For each server, find the 95th percentile of response times.

   d. Which server has more consistent (less variable) response times? Justify your answer.

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      Server A: :math:`X_A \sim \text{Exp}(\lambda = 2)` per second.

      Server B: :math:`X_B \sim \text{Exp}(\lambda = 0.5)` per second.

      **Part (a): Expected response times**

      .. math::
         E[X_A] = \frac{1}{2} = 0.5 \text{ seconds}

      .. math::
         E[X_B] = \frac{1}{0.5} = 2 \text{ seconds}

      **Server A is faster** with average response time of 0.5 seconds compared to Server B's 2 seconds. Server A is **4 times faster** on average.

      **Part (b): P(X < 1 second)**

      *Server A*:

      .. math::
         P(X_A < 1) = 1 - e^{-2 \times 1} = 1 - e^{-2} = 1 - 0.1353 = 0.8647

      *Server B*:

      .. math::
         P(X_B < 1) = 1 - e^{-0.5 \times 1} = 1 - e^{-0.5} = 1 - 0.6065 = 0.3935

      Server A has 86.5% of responses under 1 second; Server B has only 39.4%.

      **Part (c): 95th percentile**

      For :math:`x_{0.95}`: :math:`e^{-\lambda x_{0.95}} = 0.05`, so :math:`x_{0.95} = \frac{-\ln(0.05)}{\lambda} = \frac{2.996}{\lambda}`.

      *Server A*:

      .. math::
         x_{0.95} = \frac{2.996}{2} = 1.498 \text{ seconds}

      *Server B*:

      .. math::
         x_{0.95} = \frac{2.996}{0.5} = 5.991 \text{ seconds}

      95% of Server A's responses complete within 1.5 seconds; for Server B, it's 6 seconds.

      **Part (d): Variability comparison**

      .. math::
         \sigma_A = \frac{1}{\lambda_A} = 0.5 \text{ seconds}

      .. math::
         \sigma_B = \frac{1}{\lambda_B} = 2 \text{ seconds}

      **Server A has more consistent response times** (œÉ = 0.5 sec vs. œÉ = 2 sec).

      Note: For exponential distributions, faster servers (higher Œª) are always more consistent because both mean and standard deviation equal :math:`\frac{1}{\lambda}`.

.. admonition:: Exercise 8: PDF and CDF Verification
   :class: note

   Let :math:`X \sim \text{Exp}(\lambda)`.

   a. Verify that :math:`f_X(x) = \lambda e^{-\lambda x}` integrates to 1 over the support.

   b. Verify that :math:`F_X(x) = 1 - e^{-\lambda x}` is the antiderivative of :math:`f_X(x)`.

   c. Find :math:`f_X(0)` (the PDF at x = 0). What does this tell you about small waiting times?

   d. Show that :math:`\lim_{x \to \infty} f_X(x) = 0`.

   e. At what value of :math:`x` does the PDF equal half its maximum value?

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      **Part (a): Verify PDF integrates to 1**

      .. math::
         \int_0^{\infty} \lambda e^{-\lambda x} \, dx = \lambda \left[ \frac{e^{-\lambda x}}{-\lambda} \right]_0^{\infty} = -\left[ e^{-\lambda x} \right]_0^{\infty}

      .. math::
         = -(e^{-\infty} - e^0) = -(0 - 1) = 1 \quad \checkmark

      **Part (b): Verify CDF is antiderivative of PDF**

      .. math::
         \frac{d}{dx} F_X(x) = \frac{d}{dx} (1 - e^{-\lambda x}) = 0 - (-\lambda) e^{-\lambda x} = \lambda e^{-\lambda x} = f_X(x) \quad \checkmark

      **Part (c): PDF at x = 0**

      .. math::
         f_X(0) = \lambda e^{-\lambda \cdot 0} = \lambda e^0 = \lambda

      The PDF achieves its **maximum value** at x = 0. This means the highest probability density is assigned to very small waiting times‚Äîshort waits are most likely. The larger Œª is, the higher the density at 0 and the more concentrated probability is near small values.

      **Part (d): Limit as x ‚Üí ‚àû**

      .. math::
         \lim_{x \to \infty} f_X(x) = \lim_{x \to \infty} \lambda e^{-\lambda x} = \lambda \cdot 0 = 0

      The PDF approaches 0 asymptotically, meaning very long waiting times are increasingly rare but never impossible.

      **Part (e): Half-maximum point**

      The maximum is :math:`f_X(0) = \lambda`. We want :math:`f_X(x) = \frac{\lambda}{2}`:

      .. math::
         \lambda e^{-\lambda x} = \frac{\lambda}{2}

      .. math::
         e^{-\lambda x} = \frac{1}{2}

      .. math::
         -\lambda x = \ln(0.5) = -\ln(2)

      .. math::
         x = \frac{\ln(2)}{\lambda} = \frac{0.693}{\lambda}

      This is exactly the **median** of the distribution. This coincidence‚Äîthat the PDF drops to half its maximum at the median‚Äîis a special property of the exponential distribution and does not hold for other distributions.

.. admonition:: Exercise 9: Application ‚Äî System Reliability
   :class: note

   A critical sensor has a lifetime that follows an exponential distribution with mean 2 years. The sensor must be replaced when it fails.

   a. Find the probability that a new sensor lasts at least 3 years.

   b. Find the probability that a sensor fails within the first 6 months.

   c. The manufacturer offers a 1-year warranty. What proportion of sensors will fail under warranty?

   d. A sensor has been operating for 18 months without failure. What is the probability it lasts at least another year?

   e. If 100 identical sensors are deployed, how many are expected to fail within the first year?

   f. Find the time by which 80% of sensors are expected to have failed.

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: sd-border-success

      Let :math:`X` = sensor lifetime (years), where :math:`X \sim \text{Exp}(\mu = 2)`, so :math:`\lambda = \frac{1}{2} = 0.5` per year.

      **Part (a): P(X ‚â• 3)**

      .. math::
         P(X \geq 3) = e^{-0.5 \times 3} = e^{-1.5} = 0.2231

      About **22.3%** of sensors last at least 3 years.

      **Part (b): P(X ‚â§ 0.5)** ‚Äî failure within 6 months

      .. math::
         P(X \leq 0.5) = 1 - e^{-0.5 \times 0.5} = 1 - e^{-0.25} = 1 - 0.7788 = 0.2212

      About **22.1%** fail within the first 6 months.

      **Part (c): P(X ‚â§ 1)** ‚Äî warranty claims

      .. math::
         P(X \leq 1) = 1 - e^{-0.5 \times 1} = 1 - e^{-0.5} = 1 - 0.6065 = 0.3935

      About **39.4%** of sensors will fail under the 1-year warranty.

      **Part (d): Conditional probability (memoryless)**

      After 18 months (1.5 years), what is P(X > 2.5 | X > 1.5)?

      By the memoryless property:

      .. math::
         P(X > 1.5 + 1 \mid X > 1.5) = P(X > 1) = e^{-0.5 \times 1} = e^{-0.5} = 0.6065

      About **60.7%** chance of lasting another year, regardless of current age.

      **Part (e): Expected failures in first year out of 100**

      From part (c), P(failure in year 1) = 0.3935.

      Expected failures = :math:`100 \times 0.3935 = 39.35 \approx 39` sensors.

      **Part (f): 80th percentile (time by which 80% fail)**

      Find :math:`x_{0.80}` such that :math:`F_X(x_{0.80}) = 0.80`:

      .. math::
         e^{-0.5 x_{0.80}} = 0.20

      .. math::

      80% of sensors fail within about **3.2 years**.
