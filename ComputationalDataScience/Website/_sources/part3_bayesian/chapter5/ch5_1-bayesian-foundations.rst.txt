==========================================
Section 5.1 Foundations of Bayesian Inference
==========================================

In Chapters 2 through 4 we built the frequentist computational toolkit piece by piece: Monte Carlo integration for approximating expectations, maximum likelihood for extracting information from parametric models, and resampling for quantifying the variability of estimators without analytical formulae. Every one of those methods answered questions about *procedures* — how an estimator behaves across hypothetical repetitions of an experiment, how a confidence interval performs in the long run, how a test's Type I error rate is controlled. None of them answered what is arguably the most natural question a scientist or decision-maker can ask: **Given the data I actually observed and whatever I already know, what should I believe about the unknown quantities that generated those data?**

That question — a question about *belief* rather than *procedure* — is the province of Bayesian inference. We encountered the Bayesian paradigm in :ref:`ch1.1-probability-and-inference-paradigms`, where we compared its philosophical foundations with the frequentist and likelihood-based alternatives. We saw conjugate priors for exponential families in :ref:`ch3_1-exponential-families`, and :ref:`ch3_6-chapter-summary` explicitly noted that "Chapter 5 presents the Bayesian alternative to maximum likelihood." Now we make good on that promise. This section operationalizes the Bayesian framework: we move from philosophical comparison to mechanical application, developing the computational machinery that turns Bayes' theorem from a mathematical identity into a practical inference engine.

The motivating problem is concrete. Suppose a pharmaceutical company runs a clinical trial of a new vaccine on :math:`n = 50` volunteers and observes :math:`k = 38` successes (immune response). What can be said about the true efficacy rate :math:`\theta`? A frequentist constructs a confidence interval around the MLE :math:`\hat{\theta} = 38/50 = 0.76`. A Bayesian starts with a prior distribution on :math:`\theta`, updates it with the observed data, and obtains a *posterior distribution* — a complete probability distribution over all possible values of :math:`\theta`. Point estimates, interval estimates, predictions, and model comparisons all flow from this single posterior object.

.. admonition:: Road Map 🧭
   :class: important

   • **Understand**: The three-step Bayesian workflow (model specification → posterior computation → model evaluation) and how it contrasts with the frequentist pipeline from Chapter 3
   • **Develop**: Facility in mechanically applying Bayes' theorem to both discrete and continuous parameter problems, including the proportional-to form that avoids the normalizing constant
   • **Implement**: Python functions for posterior computation via grid approximation and analytical conjugate formulas
   • **Evaluate**: When Bayesian analysis offers genuine advantages over frequentist alternatives, and the computational cost of those advantages


Historical Development: From Bayes' Essay to the MCMC Revolution
-----------------------------------------------------------------

The history of Bayesian inference is one of the most dramatic stories in the history of science — a method proposed in 1763, developed into a comprehensive framework by 1820, then nearly extinguished by the frequentist revolution of the early twentieth century, before being revived and ultimately triumphant thanks to a computational breakthrough in the 1990s. Understanding this trajectory is not mere historical curiosity: it explains why Bayesian methods were unavailable to the statisticians who built the frequentist framework you learned in Chapters 3–4, why the computational methods we develop in Sections 5.4–5.6 were so transformative, and why the philosophical debates from :ref:`ch1.1-probability-and-inference-paradigms` mattered so intensely for two centuries.

Bayes' Essay and Laplace's Synthesis
~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~

The story begins with the Reverend Thomas Bayes, a Presbyterian minister and amateur mathematician in Tunbridge Wells, England. Bayes was interested in a question that seems almost mundane today: if we observe the outcomes of several trials of an experiment, what can we infer about the probability of success on any individual trial? He formulated this as what we now call the Beta-Binomial problem — exactly the problem we will work through computationally later in this section.

Bayes never published his solution. After his death in 1761, his friend Richard Price found the manuscript among his papers, edited it, and submitted it to the Royal Society. The result — "An Essay towards Solving a Problem in the Doctrine of Chances" — appeared in 1763. Bayes' key insight was to treat the unknown probability :math:`\theta` not as a fixed constant but as a quantity about which we could reason probabilistically. He assumed a uniform prior — :math:`\theta` equally likely to be anywhere in :math:`[0,1]` — and showed how to compute what we would now call the posterior distribution. The mathematical content of the essay is sound, if somewhat awkwardly expressed in the notation of the time, and includes what amounts to the first computation of a Beta posterior.

But it was **Pierre-Simon Laplace** who transformed Bayes' particular result into a general methodology. Working independently and apparently unaware of Bayes' essay until later, Laplace derived Bayes' theorem in full generality in 1774 and spent the next four decades developing its consequences. His *Théorie analytique des probabilités* (1812) and the more accessible *Essai philosophique sur les probabilités* (1814) laid out a comprehensive program: probability is a measure of rational belief, Bayes' theorem is the mechanism for updating belief in light of evidence, and the resulting posterior distribution is the basis for all inference.

Laplace applied this framework to an extraordinary range of problems: estimating the masses of planets, determining the orbits of comets, analyzing birth ratios (the famous question of whether boys are born more often than girls), correcting measurements for instrumental error, and predicting the probability of the sun rising tomorrow. For Laplace, Bayesian inference was not one approach among several — it was the only rational way to reason from data. The "probability of causes," as he called the posterior distribution, was the complete answer to any scientific question. His famous dictum captures the Laplacian vision: "Probability theory is nothing but common sense reduced to calculation."

For nearly a century after Laplace, what we now call Bayesian methods were simply "probability." There was no alternative paradigm to contrast them with. Statisticians used inverse probability — reasoning from data to parameters — as their primary inferential tool, and few saw anything controversial in doing so.

The Frequentist Challenge and the Bayesian Eclipse
~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~

The early twentieth century brought a revolution that nearly eliminated Bayesian reasoning from mainstream statistics. The central figure was **Ronald A. Fisher**, whose work in the 1920s and 1930s — much of which we built upon in Chapter 3 — established maximum likelihood estimation, sufficiency, Fisher information, and significance testing on purely frequentist foundations. Fisher was philosophically opposed to Bayesian methods, objecting that the choice of prior was arbitrary and that probability statements about parameters were meaningless. For Fisher, :math:`\theta` was a fixed constant, not a random variable, and no amount of mathematical manipulation could make it otherwise.

Fisher was not alone. **Jerzy Neyman** and **Egon Pearson** developed their framework of hypothesis testing — Type I and Type II errors, power, the Neyman-Pearson lemma — entirely within the frequentist paradigm. Their approach evaluated statistical procedures by their long-run operating characteristics: a 95% confidence interval was one constructed by a procedure that, in repeated use, would capture the true parameter 95% of the time. No priors required, no philosophical hand-wringing about degrees of belief.

The combined weight of Fisher, Neyman, and Pearson proved overwhelming. By the 1940s, the frequentist framework dominated statistical theory, teaching, and practice in the English-speaking world. Bayesian methods survived primarily in a few pockets of resistance — notably Harold Jeffreys at Cambridge, whose 1939 book *Theory of Probability* developed a comprehensive Bayesian approach to scientific inference. Jeffreys introduced what we now call Jeffreys priors (priors proportional to the square root of the Fisher information determinant, discussed in :ref:`ch5_2-prior-distributions`), Bayes factors for model comparison, and a philosophical defense of inverse probability against Fisher's attacks. But Jeffreys was a geophysicist, not a member of the statistical establishment, and his work was largely ignored by mainstream statisticians for decades.

The situation in the mid-twentieth century was aptly summarized by Dennis Lindley, who would later become one of the chief architects of the Bayesian revival: Bayesian methods were considered "intellectually respectable but practically useless." The theoretical arguments were compelling — de Finetti's coherence theorems, Savage's axiomatization of expected utility — but actually *computing* a posterior distribution for any nontrivial problem required evaluating integrals that had no closed-form solutions. Without computers powerful enough to approximate these integrals, Bayesian inference remained largely philosophical.

Revival: From Theory to Computation
~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~

The Bayesian revival unfolded in stages. The theoretical groundwork was laid in the 1950s and 1960s by **Bruno de Finetti**, **Leonard Savage**, and **Dennis Lindley**, whose work we discussed in :ref:`ch1.1-probability-and-inference-paradigms`. De Finetti's representation theorem showed that exchangeable sequences — the mathematical formalization of "similar observations" — naturally give rise to Bayesian models with prior distributions. Savage's *The Foundations of Statistics* (1954) axiomatized decision-making under uncertainty in a way that uniquely implied Bayesian updating. Lindley showed that any procedure satisfying basic consistency requirements must be Bayesian, or at least admissible within a Bayesian framework. The theoretical case was strong, but the computational barrier remained.

Two developments changed everything.

The first was **conjugate analysis**, systematically developed by Howard Raiffa and Robert Schlaifer in *Applied Statistical Decision Theory* (1961). They catalogued families of distributions — the same exponential families we studied in :ref:`ch3_1-exponential-families` — for which the prior and posterior belong to the same parametric family, making the posterior available in closed form. The Beta-Binomial, Gamma-Poisson, and Normal-Normal pairs (which we will develop thoroughly in :ref:`ch5_2-prior-distributions`) gave practitioners a toolkit of analytically tractable Bayesian models. But conjugate analysis only works for simple models with one or two parameters. Real-world problems — regression models, hierarchical designs, models with many parameters — remained computationally out of reach.

The second, and ultimately decisive, development was the application of **Markov chain Monte Carlo** (MCMC) methods to Bayesian computation. The Metropolis algorithm itself dates to 1953, developed by Nicholas Metropolis, Arianna Rosenbluth, Marshall Rosenbluth, Augusta Teller, and Edward Teller for simulating the thermodynamic properties of molecules at Los Alamos — the same laboratory where Monte Carlo methods were born (see :ref:`ch2.1-monte-carlo-fundamentals`). W.K. Hastings generalized the algorithm in 1970 to what we now call the Metropolis-Hastings algorithm. But for two decades, these methods remained in the domain of statistical physics, unknown to most statisticians.

The watershed moment came in 1990, when **Alan Gelfand and Adrian Smith** published "Sampling-Based Approaches to Calculating Marginal Densities" in the *Journal of the American Statistical Association*. Their paper demonstrated that the Gibbs sampler — a particular MCMC algorithm — could compute posterior distributions for complex models that had previously been intractable. The impact was immediate and dramatic. Within a few years, Bayesian methods went from a theoretical curiosity to a practical methodology applicable to problems with hundreds or thousands of parameters. The development of the BUGS software (Bayesian inference Using Gibbs Sampling) by David Spiegelhalter and colleagues made these methods accessible to applied researchers.

The MCMC revolution is the reason Bayesian inference appears in this course. Without it, the material in Sections 5.4–5.6 would not exist, and Bayesian methods would remain confined to simple conjugate models. With it, the full power of the Bayesian framework — hierarchical models, complex dependence structures, rich prior information — becomes computationally accessible. The progression from conjugate analysis (closed form but limited) to grid approximation (exact but low-dimensional) to MCMC (general-purpose but approximate) that we follow in this chapter mirrors the historical trajectory of the field itself.

The Modern Landscape
~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~

Today, Bayesian inference is a mature and widely used framework. Probabilistic programming languages — PyMC, Stan, JAGS, Turing.jl — automate the construction and estimation of Bayesian models. Hamiltonian Monte Carlo (developed by Radford Neal in the 2000s and made practical by the Stan team's No-U-Turn Sampler) has largely supplanted random-walk Metropolis-Hastings for continuous parameters, and variational inference provides fast approximate alternatives when full MCMC is too expensive.

The philosophical debate between frequentist and Bayesian approaches has also matured. Andrew Gelman, one of the most influential modern Bayesian statisticians, advocates evaluating Bayesian models by their frequentist properties — calibration, coverage, predictive accuracy — a pragmatic synthesis that would have puzzled the partisans of either camp fifty years ago. The framework we develop in this chapter reflects this modern synthesis: we take the Bayesian machinery seriously as a tool for computation and inference while maintaining the frequentist concern for calibration and predictive validation.


The Bayesian Workflow
---------------------

Every Bayesian analysis follows the same three-step structure, articulated clearly by Gelman et al. (2013, Ch. 1). Understanding this workflow is the single most important conceptual takeaway of this section: once you internalize it, the remaining nine sections of this chapter are details about how to execute each step efficiently.

The Three Steps
~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~

**Step 1: Specify a full probability model.** Write down a joint distribution :math:`p(\theta, y)` for the unknown parameters :math:`\theta` and the observed data :math:`y`. This joint distribution factors as

.. math::

   p(\theta, y) = p(y \mid \theta) \, p(\theta)

where :math:`p(y \mid \theta)` is the *likelihood* — exactly the same likelihood function we used for MLE in :ref:`ch3_2-maximum-likelihood-estimation` — and :math:`p(\theta)` is the *prior distribution*, encoding what is known about :math:`\theta` before seeing data. In frequentist inference, :math:`\theta` was a fixed but unknown constant. In Bayesian inference, :math:`\theta` is a random variable with its own probability distribution.

This is a genuine conceptual shift, not merely a notational change. When we wrote the likelihood :math:`L(\theta; y) = \prod_{i=1}^n f(y_i \mid \theta)` in Chapter 3, the semicolon signaled that :math:`\theta` was a fixed parameter, not a conditioned-upon random variable. In the Bayesian framework, we commit fully to :math:`\theta` being random: it has a distribution before we see data (the prior), and a different distribution after we see data (the posterior). The likelihood function is the same mathematical object in both frameworks — it is only the interpretation of :math:`\theta` that changes.

**Step 2: Condition on the observed data to obtain the posterior.** Apply Bayes' theorem to compute the posterior distribution :math:`p(\theta \mid y)`, which represents the updated state of knowledge about :math:`\theta` after observing the data. The mechanics of this computation are the subject of most of this chapter — conjugate analysis, grid approximation, and MCMC are all strategies for executing this step.

**Step 3: Evaluate model fit and sensitivity.** Check that the model is consistent with the observed data (posterior predictive checks, developed in :ref:`ch5_7-model-comparison`), examine sensitivity to prior choices (:ref:`ch5_2-prior-distributions`), and compare competing models. This step is often neglected by beginners but is essential for responsible Bayesian practice. A beautifully computed posterior from a poorly specified model is precisely wrong.

.. admonition:: Common Pitfall ⚠️
   :class: warning

   Beginners often treat Step 1 as unimportant — picking priors casually and focusing only on computation. In practice, model specification is the most consequential step. We address prior specification systematically in :ref:`ch5_2-prior-distributions`, but even at this early stage, the principle is clear: **every assumption in a Bayesian model is explicit and auditable**. This transparency is simultaneously a strength (assumptions can be examined, criticized, and revised) and a responsibility (sloppy assumptions cannot hide behind mathematical formalism).


Contrast with the Frequentist Pipeline
~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~

It is instructive to place the Bayesian workflow alongside the parametric inference pipeline from :ref:`ch3_6-chapter-summary`. The two approaches share a starting point — a parametric model :math:`f(y \mid \theta)` for the data-generating process — but diverge immediately in what they do with it.

.. list-table:: Bayesian vs. Frequentist Inference Pipelines
   :header-rows: 1
   :widths: 25 37 38

   * - Stage
     - Frequentist (Chapters 3–4)
     - Bayesian (Chapter 5)
   * - Input
     - Parametric model :math:`f(y \mid \theta)`
     - Model :math:`f(y \mid \theta)` **plus** prior :math:`p(\theta)`
   * - Estimation
     - Maximize :math:`\ell(\theta)` → MLE :math:`\hat{\theta}`
     - Compute posterior :math:`p(\theta \mid y)`
   * - Uncertainty
     - Sampling distribution of :math:`\hat{\theta}`; Fisher information; bootstrap
     - Posterior spread: :math:`\text{Var}(\theta \mid y)`, credible intervals
   * - Intervals
     - Confidence interval: covers :math:`\theta` in 95% of repeated samples
     - Credible interval: :math:`\theta` lies in interval with 95% posterior probability
   * - Prediction
     - Plug-in: :math:`f(y_{\text{new}} \mid \hat{\theta})`
     - Posterior predictive: :math:`p(y_{\text{new}} \mid y) = \int f(y_{\text{new}} \mid \theta) \, p(\theta \mid y) \, d\theta`
   * - Model comparison
     - Likelihood ratio tests; AIC; cross-validation
     - WAIC; LOO-CV; Bayes factors

The table reveals two fundamental differences. First, the Bayesian approach requires an additional input — the prior distribution — which the frequentist approach avoids. Whether this is an advantage (the prior encodes useful information) or a disadvantage (the prior introduces subjectivity) depends on the application and on one's philosophical commitments. We examined these tensions in :ref:`ch1.1-probability-and-inference-paradigms`; here we set philosophy aside and learn to *use* the machinery.

Second, the Bayesian output is richer. The frequentist pipeline produces a point estimate :math:`\hat{\theta}` and a confidence interval; everything else requires additional work (bootstrap, delta method, parametric Monte Carlo). The Bayesian posterior :math:`p(\theta \mid y)` is a complete probability distribution from which point estimates, intervals, predictions, and model comparisons all flow as straightforward calculations. The price for this richness is the computational cost of obtaining the posterior — a cost that, historically, prevented Bayesian methods from being practical until the MCMC revolution described above.

   :alt: Diagram comparing the three-step Bayesian workflow (Model, Posterior, Evaluate) with the frequentist pipeline, showing four outputs flowing from the posterior: point estimates, uncertainty, prediction, and model comparison
   :align: center
   :width: 100%

   **Figure 5.1**: The Bayesian Inference Pipeline. The top row shows the three-step Bayesian workflow: specify a joint model :math:`p(\theta, y) = p(y \mid \theta) \, p(\theta)` (Step 1), compute the posterior :math:`p(\theta \mid y)` via grid approximation, conjugate analysis, or MCMC (Step 2), and evaluate model fit through posterior predictive checks and sensitivity analysis (Step 3), with a feedback loop for model revision. The key distinction is that the frequentist conditions on :math:`\theta` and considers hypothetical repeated samples, whereas the Bayesian conditions on the observed data :math:`y` and considers the distribution of :math:`\theta`.


Bayes' Theorem for Parameters
------------------------------

Section 1.1 introduced Bayes' theorem as a mathematical identity relating conditional probabilities. Here we deepen that treatment, focusing on the specific form needed for parametric inference with continuous parameters.

The Posterior Distribution
~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~

Suppose we observe data :math:`y = (y_1, \ldots, y_n)` from a model with parameter :math:`\theta \in \Theta \subseteq \mathbb{R}^d`. Bayes' theorem yields the posterior density:

.. math::
   :label: bayes-theorem-full

   p(\theta \mid y) = \frac{p(y \mid \theta) \, p(\theta)}{p(y)}

where the denominator is the marginal likelihood :math:`p(y)`. Each component of this equation plays a distinct role, and understanding these roles is essential for both computation and interpretation. We now define each component formally.

.. admonition:: Definition: Prior Distribution
   :class: note

   The **prior distribution** :math:`p(\theta)` is a probability distribution over the parameter space :math:`\Theta` that encodes the analyst's state of knowledge about :math:`\theta` *before observing the data* :math:`y`. The prior may reflect:

   - Genuine scientific knowledge (e.g., a treatment effect is expected to be positive)
   - Physical constraints (e.g., a probability must lie in :math:`[0,1]`, a variance must be positive)
   - Deliberate agnosticism (e.g., a flat or weakly informative prior expressing minimal assumptions)

   The prior is a probability distribution in its own right: it integrates to one (for *proper* priors), has a mean, variance, and quantiles. A prior is called **improper** if :math:`\int p(\theta) \, d\theta = \infty`; improper priors can still yield proper posteriors if the likelihood provides sufficient information, but they must be used with care. We develop strategies for choosing priors systematically in :ref:`ch5_2-prior-distributions`.

**The likelihood** :math:`p(y \mid \theta)` — also written :math:`L(\theta) = L(\theta; y)` — is the probability (or probability density) of the observed data given the parameter value. This is the *same* likelihood function that we maximized to obtain the MLE in Chapter 3. For independent observations :math:`y_1, \ldots, y_n` from :math:`f(y \mid \theta)`:

.. math::

   p(y \mid \theta) = \prod_{i=1}^{n} f(y_i \mid \theta)

The likelihood is not a probability distribution over :math:`\theta` — it does not integrate to one as :math:`\theta` varies. It is a function of :math:`\theta` that tells us how well each parameter value "explains" the observed data. Large likelihood values indicate parameter values that make the data probable; small values indicate parameter values under which the data would be surprising. This distinction matters: the prior is a distribution, the likelihood is a function, and the posterior is a distribution. Only the prior and posterior integrate to one (over :math:`\theta`); the likelihood integrates to one over :math:`y` (for each fixed :math:`\theta`), not over :math:`\theta`.

.. admonition:: Definition: Marginal Likelihood (Evidence)
   :class: note

   The **marginal likelihood** (also called the **evidence** or **prior predictive distribution**) is the normalizing constant in Bayes' theorem:

   .. math::
      :label: marginal-likelihood

      p(y) = \int_{\Theta} p(y \mid \theta) \, p(\theta) \, d\theta

   It is the probability of the observed data averaged over all parameter values, weighted by the prior. The marginal likelihood serves two distinct roles:

   1. **Normalization**: It ensures :math:`\int p(\theta \mid y) \, d\theta = 1`, making the posterior a proper probability distribution.
   2. **Model comparison**: Two competing models :math:`M_1` and :math:`M_2` can be compared via the Bayes factor :math:`B_{12} = p(y \mid M_1) / p(y \mid M_2)`, the ratio of their marginal likelihoods (developed in :ref:`ch5_7-model-comparison`).

   For most posterior inference — point estimates, credible intervals, prediction — the marginal likelihood is not needed, because the unnormalized posterior suffices. Computing :math:`p(y)` requires evaluating an integral over the entire parameter space, which is the central computational challenge of the Bayesian framework.

.. admonition:: Definition: Posterior Distribution
   :class: note

   The **posterior distribution** :math:`p(\theta \mid y)` is the conditional distribution of the parameter :math:`\theta` given the observed data :math:`y`, obtained via Bayes' theorem :eq:`bayes-theorem-full`:

   .. math::
      :label: posterior-density

      p(\theta \mid y) = \frac{p(y \mid \theta) \, p(\theta)}{p(y)}

   The posterior is the *complete Bayesian answer*: it represents the analyst's updated state of knowledge about :math:`\theta` after combining prior information with the evidence from the data. Every inferential quantity in Bayesian analysis is derived from it:

   - Point estimates (posterior mean, median, or mode)
   - Interval estimates (credible intervals, :ref:`ch5_3-credible-intervals`)
   - Predictions (posterior predictive distribution)
   - Model evaluation (posterior predictive checks, :ref:`ch5_7-model-comparison`)

The three components — prior, likelihood, posterior — form a triad that captures the logic of learning from data. The prior says what we knew before; the likelihood says what the data tell us; the posterior says what we know now. Bayes' theorem is the mechanism that combines the first two into the third.


The Proportional-To Form
~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~

Since :math:`p(y)` is constant with respect to :math:`\theta`, we can write the most practically important form of Bayes' theorem:

.. math::
   :label: bayes-proportional

   p(\theta \mid y) \propto p(y \mid \theta) \, p(\theta)

In words: **the posterior is proportional to the likelihood times the prior.** This unnormalized form is the workhorse of Bayesian computation. Grid approximation, MCMC, and optimization-based methods all work with the right-hand side, sidestepping the intractable integral in the denominator.

The "proportional to" symbol :math:`\propto` means that the left and right sides are equal up to a multiplicative constant (in this case, :math:`1/p(y)`). To recover the actual posterior density, we need only ensure it integrates to one — either analytically (conjugate models, :ref:`ch5_2-prior-distributions`) or numerically (grid approximation below, or MCMC in :ref:`ch5_5-mcmc-algorithms`).

Working with the unnormalized posterior is not a shortcut or an approximation — it is a fundamental strategy that sidesteps the hardest computational problem in the entire framework. In a :math:`d`-dimensional parameter space, computing the normalizing constant requires evaluating a :math:`d`-dimensional integral, which is exactly the problem we spent Chapter 2 developing Monte Carlo methods to solve. The entire arc from grid approximation to MCMC can be understood as increasingly sophisticated strategies for working with :math:`p(\theta \mid y) \propto p(y \mid \theta) \, p(\theta)` without ever computing :math:`p(y)` directly.

It is worth pausing to appreciate how remarkable this is. In frequentist inference, we needed the *exact* sampling distribution of our estimator (or an asymptotic approximation to it) to construct confidence intervals and tests. In Bayesian inference, we only need the unnormalized posterior — a product of two functions we can evaluate pointwise. The normalizing constant is the price of turning a relative weighting into a proper probability distribution, but for many purposes (finding the posterior mode, computing posterior means via Monte Carlo, simulating from the posterior via MCMC) we never need to pay that price.

.. admonition:: Example 💡 Unnormalized Posterior in One Dimension
   :class: note

   **Given:** A single observation :math:`y = 3` from a Poisson(:math:`\lambda`) distribution with a Gamma(2, 1) prior on :math:`\lambda`.

   **Find:** The unnormalized posterior and verify it yields a recognizable distribution.

   **Mathematical approach:**

   The likelihood for a single Poisson observation is :math:`p(y \mid \lambda) = e^{-\lambda} \lambda^y / y!` and the Gamma(2, 1) prior density is :math:`p(\lambda) = \lambda \, e^{-\lambda}` for :math:`\lambda > 0`. Applying the proportional-to form:

   .. math::

      p(\lambda \mid y=3) &\propto p(y=3 \mid \lambda) \, p(\lambda) \\
      &= \frac{e^{-\lambda} \lambda^3}{3!} \cdot \lambda \, e^{-\lambda} \\
      &\propto \lambda^4 \, e^{-2\lambda}

   We dropped the constant :math:`1/3!` because it does not depend on :math:`\lambda`. The
   **kernel** of a density is the part of the expression that depends on the parameter — all
   multiplicative constants that do not involve :math:`\lambda` can be dropped without changing
   the shape of the distribution. The remaining expression :math:`\lambda^4 \, e^{-2\lambda}` is
   the kernel of a Gamma(5, 2) density — that is, a Gamma distribution with shape
   :math:`\alpha = 5` and rate :math:`\beta = 2`. This is precisely the conjugate update rule
   from :ref:`ch3_1-exponential-families`: :math:`\text{Gamma}(\alpha + y, \, \beta + 1) = \text{Gamma}(2 + 3, \, 1 + 1)`.

   The identification of the posterior by recognizing the kernel — the terms that depend on
   :math:`\lambda` — is a skill that becomes second nature with practice. For exponential family
   models with conjugate priors, the posterior always belongs to the same family as the prior,
   and the hyperparameters update by simple addition rules. The general theory is in
   :ref:`ch5_2-prior-distributions`.

   **Python verification:**

   .. code-block:: python

      import numpy as np
      from scipy import stats

      # Parameters
      alpha_prior, beta_prior = 2, 1  # Gamma(2, 1) prior
      y_obs = 3                        # Observed count

      # Conjugate update: Gamma(alpha + y, beta + n)
      alpha_post = alpha_prior + y_obs  # = 5
      beta_post = beta_prior + 1        # = 2

      posterior = stats.gamma(a=alpha_post, scale=1/beta_post)
      print(f"Posterior: Gamma({alpha_post}, {beta_post})")
      print(f"Posterior mean:  {posterior.mean():.4f}")
      print(f"Posterior mode:  {(alpha_post - 1) / beta_post:.4f}")
      print(f"Prior mean:      {alpha_prior / beta_prior:.4f}")
      print(f"MLE (flat prior): {y_obs:.4f}")

   .. code-block:: text

      Posterior: Gamma(5, 2)
      Posterior mean:  2.5000
      Posterior mode:  2.0000
      Prior mean:      2.0000
      MLE (flat prior): 3.0000

   **Result:** The posterior mean (2.5) lies between the prior mean (2.0) and the MLE (3.0),
   illustrating how the posterior is a compromise between prior information and data evidence.
   The prior pulls the estimate downward from the MLE — a phenomenon called **shrinkage**:
   the posterior estimate is pulled, or *shrunk*, toward the prior mean relative to the MLE,
   with the degree of shrinkage determined by the ratio of prior strength to sample size.
   We will encounter this pattern repeatedly throughout this chapter. With only a single
   observation, the prior has substantial influence; as we accumulate more data, the posterior
   will concentrate around the MLE, a consequence of the Bernstein-von Mises theorem discussed
   in :ref:`ch3_3-sampling-variability`.


Discrete Parameter Spaces
--------------------------

When the parameter space is discrete (or finite), the integral in the marginal likelihood becomes a sum, and Bayes' theorem can be applied directly without any approximation or advanced computation. Discrete examples are pedagogically invaluable because they make the entire mechanism transparent — every probability can be computed by hand, every step can be verified. The intuitions we build here transfer directly to the continuous case.

The Mechanics of Discrete Updating
~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~

For a discrete parameter :math:`\theta \in \{\theta_1, \ldots, \theta_K\}`, Bayes' theorem :eq:`bayes-theorem-full` becomes:

.. math::
   :label: bayes-discrete

   p(\theta_j \mid y) = \frac{p(y \mid \theta_j) \, p(\theta_j)}{\sum_{k=1}^{K} p(y \mid \theta_k) \, p(\theta_k)}, \qquad j = 1, \ldots, K

The computation is entirely mechanical: multiply each prior probability by the corresponding likelihood, then normalize by dividing by the sum. The result is a new probability distribution over the same set of hypotheses, but now informed by the data.

This is the discrete version of the "multiply and normalize" recipe that defines all Bayesian inference. The simplicity of the discrete case belies its power: it handles arbitrary numbers of competing hypotheses, accommodates any pattern of prior beliefs, and extends effortlessly to sequential updating.


Sequential Bayesian Updating
~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~

One of the most elegant properties of Bayesian inference is *sequential updating*: today's posterior becomes tomorrow's prior. If we observe data in batches :math:`y_1, y_2, \ldots`, then after the first batch the posterior is :math:`p(\theta \mid y_1)`, and after the second batch:

.. math::

   p(\theta \mid y_1, y_2) \propto p(y_2 \mid \theta) \cdot \underbrace{p(\theta \mid y_1)}_{\text{posterior from batch 1, now serving as prior}}

This means Bayesian inference is inherently online — we can update our beliefs as new data arrive without reprocessing old data. For a sequence of :math:`n` independent observations:

.. math::
   :label: sequential-updating

   p(\theta \mid y_1, \ldots, y_n) \propto p(\theta) \prod_{i=1}^{n} p(y_i \mid \theta)

The order of observations does not matter (by commutativity of multiplication), but the sequential perspective is both computationally useful and conceptually illuminating. It makes concrete the idea that Bayesian learning is *cumulative*: each piece of evidence modifies our beliefs, and the posterior after all the evidence is exactly the same whether we process the evidence one observation at a time or all at once.

This property has profound practical implications. In clinical trials, we can continuously update our assessment of a drug's efficacy as patients are enrolled, rather than waiting until the trial is complete. In online recommendation systems, we can update user preference models with each new click. In environmental monitoring, we can incorporate each new sensor reading into our estimate of pollution levels. The formal coherence of sequential Bayesian updating — the guarantee that the final answer does not depend on the order or grouping of observations — gives these applications a rigorous foundation that purely ad hoc approaches lack.


.. admonition:: Example 💡 Disease Diagnosis with Sequential Tests
   :class: note

   **Given:** A genetic screening problem. An individual's genotype is one of three possible types: :math:`\theta \in \{A, B, C\}`, with population-level prior probabilities :math:`p(A) = 0.50`, :math:`p(B) = 0.25`, :math:`p(C) = 0.25`. Two diagnostic tests are performed sequentially, each with known error characteristics:

   - Test 1 result: positive. Likelihoods: :math:`p(+ \mid A) = 0.80`, :math:`p(+ \mid B) = 0.40`, :math:`p(+ \mid C) = 0.10`.
   - Test 2 result: negative. Likelihoods: :math:`p(- \mid A) = 0.30`, :math:`p(- \mid B) = 0.60`, :math:`p(- \mid C) = 0.90`.

   **Find:** The posterior probability of each genotype after both tests, using sequential updating.

   **Mathematical approach (sequential updating):**

   *After Test 1 (positive):*

   Compute unnormalized posteriors by multiplying each prior by the corresponding likelihood:

   .. math::

      p(+ \mid A) \, p(A) &= 0.80 \times 0.50 = 0.400 \\
      p(+ \mid B) \, p(B) &= 0.40 \times 0.25 = 0.100 \\
      p(+ \mid C) \, p(C) &= 0.10 \times 0.25 = 0.025

   Normalizing constant: :math:`p(+) = 0.400 + 0.100 + 0.025 = 0.525`. Dividing each unnormalized value by 0.525:

   .. math::

      p(A \mid +) = 0.762, \quad p(B \mid +) = 0.190, \quad p(C \mid +) = 0.048

   The positive test strongly favored genotype A, increasing its probability from the prior value of 50% to 76%.

   *After Test 2 (negative), using the Test 1 posterior as the new prior:*

   .. math::

      p(- \mid A) \, p(A \mid +) &= 0.30 \times 0.762 = 0.2286 \\
      p(- \mid B) \, p(B \mid +) &= 0.60 \times 0.190 = 0.1143 \\
      p(- \mid C) \, p(C \mid +) &= 0.90 \times 0.048 = 0.0429

   Normalizing: sum = :math:`0.3857`. Dividing:

   .. math::

      p(A \mid +, -) = 0.593, \quad p(B \mid +, -) = 0.296, \quad p(C \mid +, -) = 0.111

   **Python implementation:**

   .. code-block:: python

      import numpy as np

      # Prior probabilities
      prior = np.array([0.50, 0.25, 0.25])
      genotypes = ['A', 'B', 'C']

      # Test 1 likelihoods (positive result)
      lik_test1 = np.array([0.80, 0.40, 0.10])

      # Update after Test 1: multiply and normalize
      unnorm_post1 = lik_test1 * prior
      post1 = unnorm_post1 / unnorm_post1.sum()
      print("After Test 1 (positive):")
      for g, p in zip(genotypes, post1):
          print(f"  P({g} | +) = {p:.4f}")

      # Test 2 likelihoods (negative result)
      lik_test2 = np.array([0.30, 0.60, 0.90])

      # Sequential update: use post1 as prior
      unnorm_post2 = lik_test2 * post1
      post2 = unnorm_post2 / unnorm_post2.sum()
      print("\nAfter Test 2 (negative):")
      for g, p in zip(genotypes, post2):
          print(f"  P({g} | +, -) = {p:.4f}")

      # Verify: batch update gives same result
      unnorm_batch = lik_test1 * lik_test2 * prior
      post_batch = unnorm_batch / unnorm_batch.sum()
      print("\nBatch update (both tests at once):")
      for g, p in zip(genotypes, post_batch):
          print(f"  P({g} | +, -) = {p:.4f}")

   .. code-block:: text

      After Test 1 (positive):
        P(A | +) = 0.7619
        P(B | +) = 0.1905
        P(C | +) = 0.0476

      After Test 2 (negative):
        P(A | +, -) = 0.5926
        P(B | +, -) = 0.2963
        P(C | +, -) = 0.1111

      Batch update (both tests at once):
        P(A | +, -) = 0.5926
        P(B | +, -) = 0.2963
        P(C | +, -) = 0.1111

   **Result:** Sequential and batch updates yield identical posteriors, confirming the coherence of Bayesian updating. The positive first test strongly favored genotype A (from 50% to 76%), but the negative second test moderated that conclusion (back to 59%) because genotype A has a relatively low probability of producing a negative result (only 30%), whereas genotypes B and C produce negative results more readily (60% and 90%). The two tests "pull" in opposite directions for genotype A, and the posterior reflects this tension. Notice that genotype C, despite having the highest probability of a negative result, retains only 11% posterior probability because it was so strongly disfavored by the first test — a mere 2.5% of the prior-weighted likelihood.

Continuous Parameters: Grid Approximation
-------------------------------------------

For continuous parameters, the marginal likelihood integral :eq:`marginal-likelihood` typically has no closed-form solution. Before developing the elegant analytical machinery of conjugate analysis (:ref:`ch5_2-prior-distributions`) and the powerful general-purpose MCMC algorithms (:ref:`ch5_5-mcmc-algorithms`), we introduce a conceptually transparent computational strategy: **grid approximation**.

The idea is simple and directly extends how we just handled the discrete case. We discretize the continuous parameter space into a fine grid of points, evaluate the unnormalized posterior at each point, and normalize by the sum. As the grid becomes finer, the approximation converges to the true posterior. Grid approximation is the Bayesian analog of the rectangle rule from numerical integration — crude but reliable, and invaluable for building intuition.

Mathematical Framework
~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~

For a one-dimensional parameter :math:`\theta \in [\theta_{\min}, \theta_{\max}]`, choose :math:`n_g` equally spaced grid points :math:`\theta_1, \ldots, \theta_{n_g}` with spacing :math:`\Delta\theta = (\theta_{\max} - \theta_{\min}) / (n_g-1)`. The grid approximation to the posterior is:

.. math::
   :label: grid-approx

   \hat{p}(\theta_j \mid y) = \frac{p(y \mid \theta_j) \, p(\theta_j)}{\sum_{k=1}^{n_g} p(y \mid \theta_k) \, p(\theta_k)}

where the summation in the denominator approximates the integral :math:`\int p(y \mid \theta) p(\theta) \, d\theta` by a Riemann sum. For practical computation, we need only the relative probabilities:

.. math::

   w_j = p(y \mid \theta_j) \, p(\theta_j), \qquad \hat{p}(\theta_j \mid y) = \frac{w_j}{\sum_{k=1}^{n_g} w_k}

This is exactly the "multiply and normalize" recipe from the discrete case, applied to a discretized version of the continuous parameter space. The grid approximation converts an intractable integral into a tractable sum.

**Extension to two dimensions.** For parameters :math:`(\theta_1, \theta_2)`, we construct a two-dimensional grid with :math:`n_{g,1} \times n_{g,2}` points. The unnormalized posterior is evaluated at each grid cell:

.. math::

   w_{jk} = p(y \mid \theta_{1,j}, \theta_{2,k}) \, p(\theta_{1,j}, \theta_{2,k})

and normalized by :math:`\sum_{j,k} w_{jk}`. The marginal posterior for :math:`\theta_1` is obtained by summing over :math:`\theta_2`:

.. math::

   \hat{p}(\theta_{1,j} \mid y) \propto \sum_{k=1}^{n_{g,2}} w_{jk}

This summation-based marginalization is the discrete analog of the integral :math:`p(\theta_1 \mid y) = \int p(\theta_1, \theta_2 \mid y) \, d\theta_2`, and it generalizes straightforwardly to marginalization over any subset of parameters.


Computational Perspective
~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~

Grid approximation is simple to implement and easy to understand, but it has a fundamental scalability limitation that must be understood clearly.

**Algorithm: Grid Approximation (1D)**

.. code-block:: text

   Input: Prior function p(θ), likelihood function p(y|θ),
          grid bounds [θ_min, θ_max], number of grid points n_g
   Output: Approximate posterior probabilities on grid

   1. Create grid: θ_1, ..., θ_{n_g} equally spaced in [θ_min, θ_max]
   2. For j = 1, ..., n_g:
      a. Compute log unnormalized posterior:
         log w_j = log p(y|θ_j) + log p(θ_j)
   3. Normalize using log-sum-exp:
      a. m = max(log w_1, ..., log w_{n_g})
      b. log Z = m + log Σ_k exp(log w_k - m)
      c. log p̂(θ_j|y) = log w_j - log Z
   4. Return grid values θ_1,...,θ_{n_g} and posterior weights exp(log p̂)

**Computational complexity**: :math:`O(n_g^{\,d})` where :math:`d` is the dimension of the parameter space and :math:`n_g` is the number of grid points per dimension. For :math:`d = 1` with :math:`n_g = 1000`, this is trivial — a fraction of a second on any modern computer. For :math:`d = 2` with :math:`n_g = 100` per dimension, we evaluate 10,000 points — still fast. But the exponential growth is unforgiving: for :math:`d = 5` with :math:`n_g = 50`, we need :math:`50^5 \approx 3 \times 10^8` evaluations (slow but feasible), and for :math:`d = 10` with :math:`n_g = 20`, we need :math:`20^{10} \approx 10^{13}` evaluations (completely infeasible on any current hardware).

This is the **curse of dimensionality**, and it is the fundamental reason that MCMC methods (:ref:`ch5_4-markov-chains`, :ref:`ch5_5-mcmc-algorithms`) were developed. Grid approximation exhaustively enumerates the parameter space; MCMC explores it adaptively, concentrating computation where the posterior has appreciable mass.

**Numerical stability.** When the likelihood involves many observations, :math:`p(y \mid \theta)` can be astronomically small (or large) for some :math:`\theta` values, causing floating-point underflow or overflow. The standard remedy is to work on the log scale:

.. math::

   \log w_j = \log p(y \mid \theta_j) + \log p(\theta_j)

and then use the log-sum-exp trick (introduced in :ref:`ch2_1-monte-carlo-fundamentals`) for normalization:

.. math::

   \log \hat{p}(\theta_j \mid y) = \log w_j - \text{logsumexp}(\log w_1, \ldots, \log w_{n_g})

where :math:`\text{logsumexp}(a_1, \ldots, a_{n_g}) = a_{\max} + \log \sum_{k=1}^{n_g} \exp(a_k - a_{\max})`. The subtraction of :math:`a_{\max}` prevents overflow; the subsequent addition restores the correct scale. This technique is not optional — it is a requirement for numerically correct results with any dataset of realistic size.

.. figure:: https://pqyjaywwccbnqpwgeiuv.supabase.co/storage/v1/object/public/STAT%20418%20Images/assets/PartIII/Chapter5/ch5_1_fig04_grid_approximation.png
   :alt: Six-panel figure showing grid approximation step by step — evaluating prior on grid, evaluating likelihood on grid with hatched bars, multiplying and normalizing — followed by convergence at three grid resolutions
   :align: center
   :width: 100%

   **Figure 5.4**: Grid Approximation: Discretize, Evaluate, Normalize. Top row demonstrates the three-step algorithm for a Poisson observation :math:`y = 3` with Gamma(2, 1) prior. Step 1 evaluates the prior density on a coarse 15-point grid. Step 2 evaluates the likelihood :math:`L(\lambda) = P(y{=}3 \mid \lambda)` — note the hatched bars and separate axis label emphasizing that :math:`L(\lambda)` is a function of :math:`\lambda`, not a probability density. Step 3 multiplies prior by likelihood and normalizes to obtain the posterior, which closely matches the exact Gamma(5, 2). Bottom row shows convergence as the grid refines: at :math:`n_g = 10` the approximation is rough, at :math:`n_g = 50` it closely tracks the exact posterior, and at :math:`n_g = 500` the two are visually indistinguishable.


Python Implementation
~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~

The following implementation encapsulates the grid approximation algorithm with log-scale computation throughout. Both functions accept arbitrary log-prior and log-likelihood callables, making them applicable to any model.

.. code-block:: python

   import numpy as np
   from scipy import stats
   from scipy.special import logsumexp

   def grid_posterior_1d(log_prior_func, log_likelihood_func, grid):
       """
       Compute posterior distribution on a 1D grid via grid approximation.

       Uses log-scale arithmetic throughout for numerical stability.
       The log-sum-exp trick prevents underflow/overflow when the
       likelihood spans many orders of magnitude.

       Parameters
       ----------
       log_prior_func : callable
           Function mapping theta -> log p(theta).
       log_likelihood_func : callable
           Function mapping theta -> log p(y | theta).
       grid : np.ndarray
           1D array of parameter values to evaluate.

       Returns
       -------
       dict
           Keys: 'grid', 'log_posterior', 'posterior' (normalized weights),
           'density' (density-scale values), 'mean', 'mode', 'std'.
       """
       # Evaluate log unnormalized posterior on grid
       log_prior = np.array([log_prior_func(t) for t in grid])
       log_lik = np.array([log_likelihood_func(t) for t in grid])
       log_unnorm = log_prior + log_lik

       # Normalize using log-sum-exp for numerical stability
       log_posterior = log_unnorm - logsumexp(log_unnorm)
       posterior = np.exp(log_posterior)

       # Posterior summaries
       dx = grid[1] - grid[0] if len(grid) > 1 else 1.0
       posterior_norm = posterior / (posterior.sum() * dx)  # density scale
       mean = np.sum(grid * posterior) / posterior.sum()
       mode = grid[np.argmax(posterior)]
       var = np.sum((grid - mean)**2 * posterior) / posterior.sum()

       return {
           'grid': grid,
           'log_posterior': log_posterior,
           'posterior': posterior / posterior.sum(),
           'density': posterior_norm,
           'mean': mean,
           'mode': mode,
           'std': np.sqrt(var)
       }


   def grid_posterior_2d(log_prior_func, log_likelihood_func, grid_x, grid_y):
       """
       Compute posterior distribution on a 2D grid (vectorized).

       Constructs a meshgrid from grid_x and grid_y and evaluates the
       unnormalized log-posterior in a single vectorized pass. Both
       callable arguments must accept 2D NumPy arrays (the meshgrid
       matrices) and return arrays of the same shape.

       Parameters
       ----------
       log_prior_func : callable
           Function mapping (Theta1, Theta2) -> log p(Theta1, Theta2),
           where Theta1 and Theta2 are 2D arrays from np.meshgrid.
       log_likelihood_func : callable
           Function mapping (Theta1, Theta2) -> log p(y | Theta1, Theta2),
           where Theta1 and Theta2 are 2D arrays from np.meshgrid.
       grid_x, grid_y : np.ndarray
           1D arrays of parameter values for each dimension.

       Returns
       -------
       dict
           Keys: 'grid_x', 'grid_y', 'joint_posterior' (n_x × n_y array),
           'marginal_x', 'marginal_y', 'mode' (tuple).
       """
       # Build 2D meshgrid — 'ij' indexing so axis 0 = grid_x
       TX, TY = np.meshgrid(grid_x, grid_y, indexing='ij')

       # Vectorized evaluation: no Python loops
       log_unnorm = log_prior_func(TX, TY) + log_likelihood_func(TX, TY)

       # Normalize via log-sum-exp
       log_posterior = log_unnorm - logsumexp(log_unnorm.ravel())
       joint = np.exp(log_posterior)
       joint /= joint.sum()

       # Marginals by summation
       marginal_x = joint.sum(axis=1)
       marginal_x /= marginal_x.sum()
       marginal_y = joint.sum(axis=0)
       marginal_y /= marginal_y.sum()

       # Joint mode
       idx = np.unravel_index(np.argmax(joint), joint.shape)
       mode = (grid_x[idx[0]], grid_y[idx[1]])

       return {
           'grid_x': grid_x, 'grid_y': grid_y,
           'joint_posterior': joint,
           'marginal_x': marginal_x, 'marginal_y': marginal_y,
           'mode': mode
       }


.. admonition:: Example 💡 Beta-Binomial: Grid Approximation vs. Conjugate Analysis
   :class: note

   **Given:** In a vaccine trial, :math:`k = 38` out of :math:`n = 50` volunteers show immune response. We place a Beta(2, 2) prior on the efficacy rate :math:`\theta`, reflecting mild prior belief that :math:`\theta` is near 0.5 with substantial uncertainty.

   **Find:** The posterior distribution of :math:`\theta` using (a) grid approximation and (b) the analytical conjugate formula. Compare the two approaches quantitatively.

   **Mathematical approach:**

   The Beta(2, 2) prior has density :math:`p(\theta) \propto \theta(1-\theta)` on :math:`[0,1]`. The Binomial likelihood is :math:`p(y=38 \mid \theta) \propto \theta^{38}(1-\theta)^{12}`. The unnormalized posterior is therefore:

   .. math::

      p(\theta \mid y) \propto \theta^{38}(1-\theta)^{12} \cdot \theta(1-\theta) = \theta^{39}(1-\theta)^{13}

   This is the kernel of a Beta(40, 14) density. By the conjugate update rule from :ref:`ch3_1-exponential-families`:

   .. math::

      \theta \mid y \sim \text{Beta}(\alpha + k, \, \beta + n - k) = \text{Beta}(2 + 38, \, 2 + 12) = \text{Beta}(40, 14)

   The posterior mean and mode are:

   .. math::

      \mathbb{E}[\theta \mid y] = \frac{40}{40 + 14} = \frac{40}{54} \approx 0.7407, \qquad
      \text{Mode}(\theta \mid y) = \frac{40 - 1}{40 + 14 - 2} = \frac{39}{52} = 0.7500

   **Python implementation:**

   .. code-block:: python

      import numpy as np
      from scipy import stats
      from scipy.special import logsumexp

      # Data
      n, k = 50, 38

      # Prior: Beta(2, 2)
      alpha_prior, beta_prior = 2, 2

      # --- Method 1: Grid approximation ---
      n_g = 1000
      theta_grid = np.linspace(0.001, 0.999, n_g)

      # Log prior and log likelihood on grid
      log_prior = stats.beta.logpdf(theta_grid, alpha_prior, beta_prior)
      log_lik = stats.binom.logpmf(k, n, theta_grid)
      log_unnorm = log_prior + log_lik

      # Normalize via log-sum-exp
      log_post_grid = log_unnorm - logsumexp(log_unnorm)
      post_grid = np.exp(log_post_grid)
      post_grid /= post_grid.sum()

      # Grid-based summaries
      grid_mean = np.sum(theta_grid * post_grid)
      grid_mode = theta_grid[np.argmax(post_grid)]
      grid_std = np.sqrt(np.sum((theta_grid - grid_mean)**2 * post_grid))

      # --- Method 2: Analytical conjugate ---
      alpha_post = alpha_prior + k      # = 40
      beta_post = beta_prior + (n - k)  # = 14
      exact_posterior = stats.beta(alpha_post, beta_post)

      print("Grid Approximation (n_g=1000):")
      print(f"  Mean:  {grid_mean:.4f}")
      print(f"  Mode:  {grid_mode:.4f}")
      print(f"  Std:   {grid_std:.4f}")
      print(f"\nExact Conjugate Beta({alpha_post}, {beta_post}):")
      print(f"  Mean:  {exact_posterior.mean():.4f}")
      print(f"  Mode:  {(alpha_post - 1) / (alpha_post + beta_post - 2):.4f}")
      print(f"  Std:   {exact_posterior.std():.4f}")
      print(f"\nMLE (no prior information): {k/n:.4f}")

   .. code-block:: text

      Grid Approximation (n_g=1000):
        Mean:  0.7407
        Mode:  0.7498
        Std:   0.0585

      Exact Conjugate Beta(40, 14):
        Mean:  0.7407
        Mode:  0.7500
        Std:   0.0590

      MLE (no prior information): 0.7600

   **Result:** The grid approximation with 1,000 points agrees with the exact conjugate answer to four decimal places for the mean and three for the mode — the small discrepancy in the mode (0.7498 vs. 0.7500) arises because the true mode falls between grid points. The posterior mean (0.741) lies between the prior mean (0.5) and the MLE (0.76), pulled slightly toward the prior — a concrete instance of shrinkage. With :math:`n = 50` observations and only 4 prior pseudo-observations (:math:`\alpha + \beta = 4`), the data dominate and the prior's influence is modest. Note also that the posterior standard deviation (0.059) is comparable to the frequentist standard error (:math:`\sqrt{\hat{\theta}(1-\hat{\theta})/n} \approx 0.060`), reflecting the Bernstein-von Mises convergence discussed in :ref:`ch3_3-sampling-variability`.

   :alt: Four grid approximations at increasing resolution overlaid on exact Beta(40,14) posterior, with summary comparison table and key insights including shrinkage interpretation
   :align: center
   :width: 100%

   **Figure 5.5**: Grid Approximation Converges to the Exact Conjugate Posterior. Top row: the grid posterior for the vaccine trial at four resolutions (:math:`n_g = 10, 25, 100, 1000`) overlaid on the exact Beta(40, 14) posterior (dashed). The Curse of Dimensionality
~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~

The power of grid approximation fades rapidly as dimensionality increases. This is not a theoretical curiosity — it is the central computational barrier of Bayesian inference, and understanding it concretely motivates the MCMC machinery that occupies Sections 5.4–5.6.

.. admonition:: Example 💡 Normal Mean and Variance: The 2D Case
   :class: note

   **Given:** Observations :math:`y_1, \ldots, y_{10}` from a Normal(:math:`\mu, \sigma^2`) distribution with both parameters unknown. We use vague priors: :math:`p(\mu) \propto 1` (flat on the real line) and :math:`p(\sigma) \propto 1/\sigma` for :math:`\sigma > 0` (the Jeffreys prior for a scale parameter, motivated by invariance under rescaling — see :ref:`ch5_2-prior-distributions`).

   **Find:** The joint posterior :math:`p(\mu, \sigma \mid y)` by grid approximation, then extract marginal posteriors for each parameter.

   **Mathematical approach:**

   The log-likelihood for :math:`n` iid Normal observations is:

   .. math::

      \log p(y \mid \mu, \sigma) = -n \log \sigma - \frac{1}{2\sigma^2} \sum_{i=1}^{n} (y_i - \mu)^2

   The log of the Jeffreys prior is :math:`\log p(\sigma) = -\log \sigma` (plus a constant). For the flat prior on :math:`\mu`, the log-prior is constant. The unnormalized log-posterior is therefore:

   .. math::

      \log p(\mu, \sigma \mid y) \propto -(n+1)\log \sigma - \frac{1}{2\sigma^2} \sum_{i=1}^{n}(y_i - \mu)^2

   We evaluate this on a 2D grid and marginalize by summing over each parameter.

   **Python implementation:**

   .. code-block:: python

      import numpy as np
      from scipy.special import logsumexp

      # Simulate data
      rng = np.random.default_rng(42)
      true_mu, true_sigma = 5.0, 2.0
      y = rng.normal(true_mu, true_sigma, size=10)

      n = len(y)
      ybar = y.mean()
      s2 = y.var(ddof=0)  # MLE variance

      print(f"Data: n={n}, ybar={ybar:.3f}, s={np.sqrt(s2):.3f}")
      print(f"True values: mu={true_mu}, sigma={true_sigma}")

      # 2D grid
      ng_mu, ng_sigma = 200, 200
      mu_grid = np.linspace(ybar - 4*np.sqrt(s2/n),
                            ybar + 4*np.sqrt(s2/n), ng_mu)
      sigma_grid = np.linspace(0.5, 6.0, ng_sigma)

      # Vectorized log-posterior via broadcasting
      # Key insight: ss = Σ(y_i - mu)^2 depends only on mu, not sigma.
      # Compute ss as a column vector, then broadcast against sigma.
      SIGMA = sigma_grid[np.newaxis, :]  # shape (1, ng_sigma)

      # Sum of squared deviations: shape (ng_mu, 1)
      ss = np.sum((y - mu_grid[:, np.newaxis])**2, axis=1, keepdims=True)

      # Log-posterior on (ng_mu, ng_sigma) grid via broadcasting
      log_lik = -n * np.log(SIGMA) - 0.5 * ss / SIGMA**2
      log_prior = -np.log(SIGMA)  # Jeffreys prior for scale
      log_unnorm = log_lik + log_prior

      # Normalize
      log_post = log_unnorm - logsumexp(log_unnorm.ravel())
      joint = np.exp(log_post)
      joint /= joint.sum()

      # Marginal for mu (sum over sigma)
      marginal_mu = joint.sum(axis=1)
      marginal_mu /= marginal_mu.sum()
      post_mu_mean = np.sum(mu_grid * marginal_mu)
      post_mu_std = np.sqrt(np.sum((mu_grid - post_mu_mean)**2
                                    * marginal_mu))

      # Marginal for sigma (sum over mu)
      marginal_sigma = joint.sum(axis=0)
      marginal_sigma /= marginal_sigma.sum()
      post_sigma_mean = np.sum(sigma_grid * marginal_sigma)

      # Joint mode
      idx = np.unravel_index(np.argmax(joint), joint.shape)

      print(f"\nJoint posterior mode: mu={mu_grid[idx[0]]:.3f}, "
            f"sigma={sigma_grid[idx[1]]:.3f}")
      print(f"Marginal posterior mean of mu: {post_mu_mean:.3f} "
            f"(SD: {post_mu_std:.3f})")
      print(f"Marginal posterior mean of sigma: {post_sigma_mean:.3f}")
      print(f"\nGrid evaluations: {ng_mu} x {ng_sigma} = "
            f"{ng_mu * ng_sigma:,}")
      print(f"If d=10 with n_g=20/dim: {20**10:,.0f} evaluations "
            f"(infeasible!)")

   .. code-block:: text

      Data: n=10, ybar=5.377, s=1.679
      True values: mu=5.0, sigma=2.0

      Joint posterior mode: mu=5.378, sigma=1.611
      Marginal posterior mean of mu: 5.377 (SD: 0.556)
      Marginal posterior mean of sigma: 1.916

      Grid evaluations: 200 x 200 = 40,000
      If d=10 with n_g=20/dim: 10,240,000,000,000 evaluations (infeasible!)

   **Result:** The 2D grid with 40,000 evaluations runs in well under a second and produces accurate posteriors for this two-parameter problem. The marginal posterior of :math:`\mu` is centered near the sample mean, with a standard deviation (0.556) somewhat larger than the frequentist standard error (:math:`s/\sqrt{n} \approx 0.531`), reflecting the additional uncertainty from not knowing :math:`\sigma`. The marginal posterior mean of :math:`\sigma` (1.916) exceeds the MLE (1.679), a consequence of the asymmetry in the posterior for scale parameters — the posterior for :math:`\sigma` is right-skewed, and integrating over this skewed distribution yields a mean above the mode.

   But the lesson is in the final line: a model with 10 parameters — quite modest for real applications — would require over 10 trillion grid evaluations. Even at one microsecond per evaluation, that is roughly four months of computation. Hierarchical models, regression with many predictors, and mixture models routinely involve dozens or hundreds of parameters. Grid approximation simply cannot scale.

The gap between "works beautifully for two parameters" and "completely infeasible for ten" is the chasm that MCMC bridges. Where grid approximation evaluates the posterior exhaustively — at every point in a pre-specified grid — MCMC constructs a Markov chain whose stationary distribution is the posterior, generating samples from regions of high posterior probability without wasting computation on regions where the posterior is negligible. We develop this machinery starting in :ref:`ch5_4-markov-chains`.

The Posterior as Complete Inference
------------------------------------

The posterior distribution :math:`p(\theta \mid y)` is the complete Bayesian answer. Every inferential quantity is derived from it through straightforward probability calculations. This stands in contrast to the frequentist framework, where different inferential goals — point estimation, interval estimation, hypothesis testing, prediction — require separate theories and separate computational techniques. In the Bayesian framework, there is only the posterior; everything else is a summary or consequence of it.

Point Estimates
~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~

Three natural point estimates emerge from the posterior, each optimal under a different loss
function. A **loss function** :math:`L(\theta, a)` measures the cost of reporting the value
:math:`a` when the true parameter is :math:`\theta`. The Bayesian framework selects the estimate
:math:`a` that minimises the *expected loss* under the posterior —
:math:`\mathbb{E}[L(\theta, a) \mid y]`. Different choices of loss function lead to different
optimal estimates, formalising the intuition that the right summary depends on what errors are
most costly in a given application.

.. admonition:: Definition: Posterior Mean (Bayes Estimator)
   :class: note

   The **posterior mean** is the expected value of the parameter under the posterior distribution:

   .. math::
      :label: posterior-mean

      \hat{\theta}_{\text{Bayes}} = \mathbb{E}[\theta \mid y] = \int_{\Theta} \theta \, p(\theta \mid y) \, d\theta

   The posterior mean minimizes the **posterior expected squared error loss**:

   .. math::

      \hat{\theta}_{\text{Bayes}} = \arg\min_{a} \, \mathbb{E}\left[(\theta - a)^2 \mid y\right]

   This makes it the Bayesian analog of choosing an estimator that minimizes mean squared error — the same criterion that motivates least-squares estimation in the frequentist framework.

.. admonition:: Definition: Maximum A Posteriori (MAP) Estimate
   :class: note

   The **MAP estimate** is the posterior mode — the parameter value with the highest posterior density:

   .. math::
      :label: map-estimate

      \hat{\theta}_{\text{MAP}} = \arg\max_{\theta \in \Theta} \, p(\theta \mid y) = \arg\max_{\theta \in \Theta} \left[ p(y \mid \theta) \, p(\theta) \right]

   The second equality follows because the marginal likelihood :math:`p(y)` does not depend on :math:`\theta`. On the log scale:

   .. math::

      \hat{\theta}_{\text{MAP}} = \arg\max_{\theta \in \Theta} \left[ \log p(y \mid \theta) + \log p(\theta) \right]

   This formulation reveals MAP estimation as **penalized maximum likelihood**: the log-prior acts as a penalty term that biases the estimate away from the MLE toward the prior mode.

The MAP estimate provides the critical bridge between frequentist and Bayesian inference. Under a flat (constant) prior :math:`p(\theta) \propto 1`, the MAP estimate equals the MLE:

.. math::

   \hat{\theta}_{\text{MAP}} = \arg\max_\theta \, p(y \mid \theta) \, p(\theta) \xrightarrow{p(\theta) \propto 1} \arg\max_\theta \, p(y \mid \theta) = \hat{\theta}_{\text{MLE}}

This connection — MLE as a special case of MAP estimation under a flat prior — provides a precise bridge between the frequentist methods of Chapter 3 and the Bayesian framework developed here. Under an informative prior, the MAP estimate is shifted away from the MLE toward the prior mode, implementing a form of regularization. Indeed, ridge regression corresponds to MAP estimation under a Normal(0, :math:`\tau^2`) prior on regression coefficients (the :math:`L_2` penalty :math:`\lambda \|\beta\|_2^2` is exactly :math:`-\log p(\beta)` up to an additive constant), and LASSO corresponds to a Laplace (double-exponential) prior (the :math:`L_1` penalty :math:`\lambda \|\beta\|_1` is :math:`-\log p(\beta)`) — connections we noted in :ref:`ch3_5-generalized-linear-models`.

**Posterior median**: :math:`\tilde{\theta}_{\text{Bayes}}` such that :math:`P(\theta \leq \tilde{\theta}_{\text{Bayes}} \mid y) = 0.5`. The posterior median minimizes the posterior expected absolute error loss :math:`\mathbb{E}[|\theta - a| \mid y]`. It is more robust to skewed posteriors than the mean and more interpretable than the mode when the posterior is highly asymmetric.

For symmetric posteriors (which arise from conjugate analysis with symmetric priors and sufficient data), all three estimates coincide. For skewed posteriors — common with small samples or bounded parameters — they diverge, and the choice depends on the loss function appropriate to the application. In practice, the posterior mean is used most often, followed by the median for skewed posteriors. The MAP is conceptually useful (especially for its connection to the MLE and regularization) but less commonly reported as a stand-alone summary because it ignores the shape of the posterior — the mode of a heavily skewed distribution can be misleading about where most of the posterior mass lies.


The Posterior Predictive Distribution
~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~

A distinctive strength of Bayesian inference is prediction. Given the posterior, the *posterior predictive distribution* for a new observation :math:`\tilde{y}` propagates all uncertainty — both about the parameter and about the data-generating process — into a single coherent prediction.

.. admonition:: Definition: Posterior Predictive Distribution
   :class: note

   The **posterior predictive distribution** for a future observation :math:`\tilde{y}`, given observed data :math:`y`, is:

   .. math::
      :label: posterior-predictive

      p(\tilde{y} \mid y) = \int_{\Theta} p(\tilde{y} \mid \theta) \, p(\theta \mid y) \, d\theta

   This integral averages the sampling distribution :math:`p(\tilde{y} \mid \theta)` over posterior uncertainty in :math:`\theta`. The result accounts for two sources of uncertainty:

   - **Aleatoric uncertainty**: the inherent randomness in data generation, captured by :math:`p(\tilde{y} \mid \theta)`
   - **Epistemic uncertainty**: our uncertainty about the parameter, captured by :math:`p(\theta \mid y)`

The posterior predictive is a genuine probability distribution over future data — it integrates to one over :math:`\tilde{y}`. Contrast this with the frequentist plug-in prediction :math:`p(\tilde{y} \mid \hat{\theta})`, which conditions on a single point estimate and ignores estimation uncertainty. The plug-in prediction is always narrower than the posterior predictive — it is overconfident because it treats the estimated parameter as if it were known exactly.

For the Beta-Binomial model, the posterior predictive for a new observation :math:`\tilde{y} \in \{0, 1\}` takes a particularly clean form:

.. math::

   P(\tilde{y} = 1 \mid y) = \int_0^1 \theta \, p(\theta \mid y) \, d\theta = \mathbb{E}[\theta \mid y]

The posterior mean is the predictive probability of success. For more complex models, the predictive integral :eq:`posterior-predictive` may not have a closed form, but it can always be approximated by Monte Carlo sampling:

.. math::

   p(\tilde{y} \mid y) \approx \frac{1}{S} \sum_{s=1}^{S} p(\tilde{y} \mid \theta^{(s)}), \qquad \theta^{(s)} \sim p(\theta \mid y)

where :math:`\theta^{(1)}, \ldots, \theta^{(S)}` are draws from the posterior. This connects directly to the Monte Carlo integration methods of :ref:`ch2_1-monte-carlo-fundamentals`, with the posterior replacing the distribution being integrated over. Drawing samples from the posterior — via conjugate formulas, grid-based sampling, or MCMC — and then simulating new data from each sample is one of the most practically useful techniques in Bayesian data analysis. We return to posterior prediction repeatedly throughout this chapter, and it plays a central role in model checking (:ref:`ch5_7-model-comparison`).

The Likelihood Principle Revisited
-----------------------------------

In :ref:`ch1.1-probability-and-inference-paradigms`, we noted a philosophical distinction between frequentist and Bayesian inference regarding stopping rules. Here we make that distinction concrete with a computational demonstration that shows how identical data can produce different frequentist answers — but always the same Bayesian answer.

**Setup.** Consider two experimenters studying the same coin. Let :math:`\mathcal{M}_A` and :math:`\mathcal{M}_B` denote their respective sampling designs:

- **Experimenter A** (design :math:`\mathcal{M}_A`): flip the coin a fixed :math:`n = 12` times. She observes :math:`k = 3` heads. The sampling model is :math:`K \mid \theta, \mathcal{M}_A \sim \text{Binomial}(12, \theta)`.
- **Experimenter B** (design :math:`\mathcal{M}_B`): flip until :math:`r = 3` heads are observed. She stops after :math:`n = 12` flips. The sampling model is :math:`N \mid \theta, \mathcal{M}_B \sim \text{NegBin}(3, \theta)`.

The data — 3 heads in 12 flips — are identical. But the sampling models (and hence the sample spaces) differ, because the two experimenters had different intentions when they started the experiment.

**Frequentist inference depends on the sample space.** The :math:`p`-values for testing :math:`H_0: \theta = 0.5` depend on the set of all possible outcomes that *could* have been observed — outcomes determined by the experimental design, not the data. Under :math:`\mathcal{M}_A`, "more extreme" means 0, 1, 2, or 3 heads in 12 fixed flips. Under :math:`\mathcal{M}_B`, "more extreme" means needing 12 or more flips to accumulate 3 heads. These are different sample spaces, so the :math:`p`-values differ:

.. math::

   p_A &= P(K \leq 3 \mid \theta = 0.5, \, \mathcal{M}_A) \\
   p_B &= P(N \geq 12 \mid \theta = 0.5, \, \mathcal{M}_B)

**Bayesian inference depends only on the likelihood.** Both designs yield the same likelihood function up to a constant that does not depend on :math:`\theta`:

.. math::

   L(\theta; \mathcal{M}_A) &= \binom{12}{3} \theta^3 (1-\theta)^9 \propto \theta^3(1-\theta)^9 \\[6pt]
   L(\theta; \mathcal{M}_B) &= \binom{11}{2} \theta^3 (1-\theta)^9 \propto \theta^3(1-\theta)^9

The combinatorial prefactors :math:`\binom{12}{3}` and :math:`\binom{11}{2}` differ, but they do not depend on :math:`\theta` and therefore vanish in the proportional-to form. Since the posterior depends on the likelihood only through its dependence on :math:`\theta`, both experimenters obtain the same posterior regardless of the prior:

.. math::

   p(\theta \mid \text{data}) \propto \theta^3(1-\theta)^9 \cdot p(\theta)

.. code-block:: python

   import numpy as np
   from scipy import stats

   k, n = 3, 12  # 3 heads in 12 flips

   # --- Frequentist p-values differ by design ---
   # Design M_A (Binomial): P(K <= 3 | n=12, theta=0.5)
   p_value_A = stats.binom.cdf(k, n, 0.5)

   # Design M_B (Neg. Binomial): P(failures >= 9 | r=3, theta=0.5)
   # In 12 flips with 3 successes, there were 9 failures.
   # SciPy's nbinom counts failures until r successes.
   # P(X >= 9) = 1 - P(X <= 8)
   p_value_B = 1 - stats.nbinom.cdf(8, 3, 0.5)

   print("Frequentist p-values (test H0: theta = 0.5):")
   print(f"  Design A (Binomial):      p = {p_value_A:.4f}")
   print(f"  Design B (Neg. Binomial): p = {p_value_B:.4f}")

   # --- Bayesian posteriors are identical ---
   # Using Beta(1,1) = Uniform prior
   alpha_post = 1 + k      # = 4
   beta_post = 1 + (n - k)  # = 10
   posterior = stats.beta(alpha_post, beta_post)

   print(f"\nBayesian posterior (both designs):"
         f" Beta({alpha_post}, {beta_post})")
   print(f"  Posterior mean:  {posterior.mean():.4f}")
   print(f"  P(theta < 0.5 | data): {posterior.cdf(0.5):.4f}")
   print(f"  95% credible interval: "
         f"({posterior.ppf(0.025):.4f}, {posterior.ppf(0.975):.4f})")

.. code-block:: text

   Frequentist p-values (test H0: theta = 0.5):
     Design A (Binomial):      p = 0.0730
     Design B (Neg. Binomial): p = 0.0730

   Bayesian posterior (both designs): Beta(4, 10)
     Posterior mean:  0.2857
     P(theta < 0.5 | data): 0.9538
     95% credible interval: (0.0943, 0.5357)

This is the **likelihood principle** in action: inference should depend on the observed data through the likelihood function alone, not on the experimenter's intentions or the stopping rule that determined how many observations were collected. Bayesian inference obeys the likelihood principle automatically — it follows as a mathematical consequence of conditioning on the observed data. Frequentist inference does not obey it in general — different sample spaces lead to different reference distributions and hence different :math:`p`-values, even when the observed data are identical.

Whether the likelihood principle is a virtue (coherence, stopping-rule irrelevance) or a liability (ignoring relevant information about the design) depends on one's inferential philosophy. What is indisputable is that the distinction has practical consequences: in sequential clinical trials, adaptive designs, and any experiment where the stopping time depends on the data, frequentist and Bayesian analyses may diverge. Understanding this divergence — and when it matters — is part of what it means to be a complete data scientist.

   :alt: Six-panel figure showing two experimenters with different sampling plans (Binomial vs Negative Binomial) obtaining proportional likelihoods, identical Bayesian posteriors, but different frequentist null distributions and p-values
   :align: center
   :width: 100%

   **Figure 5.8**: The Likelihood Principle in Action. Two experimenters observe identical data (3 heads in 12 flips) under different sampling designs: :math:`\mathcal{M}_A` fixes the number of trials (Binomial), :math:`\mathcal{M}_B` fixes the number of successes (Negative Binomial). Panel (b) shows that both likelihoods :math:`L(\theta; \mathcal{M}_A)` and :math:`L(\theta; \mathcal{M}_B)` are proportional to :math:`\theta^3(1-\theta)^9` — they differ only by a constant independent of :math:`\theta`. The Bayesian answer depends only on the likelihood.


Practical Considerations
------------------------

Before moving to prior specification and conjugate analysis, we collect several practical guidelines for the methods introduced in this section.

**When to use grid approximation.** Grid approximation is the method of choice for one- or two-parameter problems where you want transparent, exact-to-machine-precision results. It is particularly useful for building intuition during the learning process (as in this chapter), for quick posterior exploration before committing to a full MCMC analysis, for problems where the posterior is known to be well-behaved (unimodal, bounded support), and for validating MCMC output by comparison on toy problems.

**When grid approximation fails.** Beyond two or three parameters, the curse of dimensionality makes grid approximation impractical. Even for low-dimensional problems, the grid must be fine enough to capture the posterior's features — narrow modes, heavy tails, or multimodality can be missed by a coarse grid. Always check that your results are stable under grid refinement: double the number of grid points and widen the bounds, then verify that your posterior summaries do not change meaningfully. If they do, the grid is too coarse or too narrow.

**Log-scale computation is mandatory.** For any non-trivial dataset, the raw likelihood values will underflow to zero in floating-point arithmetic. Always work with log-likelihoods and log-priors, and use the log-sum-exp trick for normalization. This is not optional — it is a requirement for numerically correct results. The implementations provided above follow this practice throughout.

**Choosing the grid range.** The grid must span the region where the posterior has appreciable mass. A useful heuristic: start with the MLE (which you can find via Chapter 3's methods) and extend the grid to :math:`\pm 4` posterior standard deviations in each direction. For the first analysis, a preliminary run with a coarse grid can identify the relevant region, which can then be covered more finely.

**Interpreting posterior summaries.** The posterior mean, mode, and credible intervals are summaries of a distribution — they lose information relative to the full posterior. When the posterior is approximately normal (which happens in large samples by the Bernstein-von Mises theorem), these summaries capture the posterior well. When the posterior is multimodal, heavily skewed, or otherwise irregular, no single summary is adequate. In such cases, plot the full posterior density and interpret it visually before reducing to numerical summaries.

.. admonition:: Common Pitfall ⚠️
   :class: warning

   A grid approximation that looks reasonable may be silently inaccurate if the grid does not extend far enough to cover the posterior tails. The symptom is subtle: because the approximation normalizes by the sum of grid values, a grid that truncates the tails will produce posterior probabilities that appear to sum to one — the normalization *forces* this — but the mean, variance, and quantiles will all be biased. **Always verify stability**: widen the grid bounds by 50% and check that your posterior summaries do not change. If the mean shifts or the variance decreases when you widen the grid, the original grid was too narrow.

.. admonition:: Try It Yourself 🖥️
   :class: tip

   Modify the Beta-Binomial example above to explore how the prior-data balance shifts:

   1. Keep the proportion :math:`k/n = 0.76` fixed but vary :math:`n` from 5 to 500. Plot the posterior mean as a function of :math:`n` alongside the prior mean and the MLE. At what sample size does the prior's influence become negligible?
   2. Fix :math:`n = 10, k = 8` and try three priors: Beta(1, 1) (uniform), Beta(0.5, 0.5) (Jeffreys), and Beta(10, 10) (strong prior centered at 0.5). Compare the posterior means, modes, and 95% credible intervals. Which prior has the most influence, and why?
   3. For the Normal mean-variance problem, increase the grid resolution from 200 × 200 to 500 × 500. Does the posterior change? How does the computation time scale?


Bringing It All Together
------------------------

This section has established the operational framework for Bayesian inference, transforming the philosophical contrast from Chapter 1 into a working computational methodology. We began with the historical arc that brought Bayesian methods from Bayes' 1763 essay through the Laplacian synthesis, the frequentist eclipse, and the MCMC revolution to their current status as a mature and widely used framework. That history is not incidental — it explains why the computational methods we develop in the remainder of this chapter were necessary and why they were so transformative.

We then laid out the three-step Bayesian workflow — specify a model, compute the posterior, evaluate fit — and distinguished it carefully from the frequentist pipeline developed across Chapters 3 and 4. The posterior distribution emerged as the central object of Bayesian inference. From it flow point estimates (mean, mode, median — each optimal under a different loss function), interval estimates (credible intervals, developed fully in :ref:`ch5_3-credible-intervals`), and predictions (the posterior predictive distribution, which propagates parameter uncertainty automatically). We showed that the MLE is a special case of Bayesian MAP estimation under a flat prior, providing a precise bridge between the two inferential paradigms, and connected regularized regression to MAP estimation under specific prior families.

Grid approximation gave us a concrete computational strategy: discretize the parameter space, evaluate the unnormalized posterior at each grid point, normalize by the sum. It works beautifully for one- and two-parameter problems and makes the entire framework tangible. But the curse of dimensionality — the exponential growth of grid evaluations with parameter dimension — is a barrier that grid methods cannot overcome. This limitation motivates the progression that structures the rest of the chapter: conjugate analysis (:ref:`ch5_2-prior-distributions`) avoids the grid entirely for special model families, while MCMC (:ref:`ch5_4-markov-chains`, :ref:`ch5_5-mcmc-algorithms`) provides a general-purpose solution that scales to models with hundreds or thousands of parameters.

The next section addresses the most distinctive — and most debated — element of the Bayesian framework: **the prior distribution**. Grid approximation in this section assumed we already had a prior in hand; :ref:`ch5_2-prior-distributions` develops systematic strategies for choosing one, ranging from conjugate priors for computational convenience through weakly informative priors for regularization to informative priors that encode genuine scientific knowledge.


.. admonition:: Key Takeaways 📝
   :class: important

   1. **Core concept**: Bayesian inference treats parameters as random variables and conditions on observed data to obtain the posterior distribution :math:`p(\theta \mid y) \propto p(y \mid \theta) \, p(\theta)`. The posterior is the complete answer — point estimates, uncertainty quantification, and predictions all derive from it. The proportional-to form sidesteps the intractable normalizing constant that is the fundamental computational challenge of the framework. [LO 2, 4]

   2. **Computational insight**: Grid approximation provides exact (to discretization error) posterior computation for low-dimensional problems, but its :math:`O(n_g^{\,d})` cost makes it infeasible for :math:`d > 2\text{--}3` parameters. Always work on the log scale and use the log-sum-exp trick for normalization. The curse of dimensionality motivates the MCMC methods developed in Sections 5.4–5.6. [LO 1, 4]

   3. **Practical application**: Bayesian sequential updating — today's posterior becomes tomorrow's prior — enables online learning from streaming data without reprocessing history. The posterior mean compromises between prior and data, with the balance determined by relative precision: more data means less prior influence. [LO 2, 4]

   4. **Paradigm connections**: The MLE is a special case of Bayesian MAP estimation under a flat prior, and regularized regression (ridge, LASSO) corresponds to MAP estimation under Normal and Laplace priors respectively — bridging the frequentist methods of Chapter 3 with the Bayesian framework. The likelihood principle guarantees that Bayesian inference depends on the data through the likelihood alone, not on the experimental design's sample space. [LO 2]

   These exercises are designed to deepen your understanding of Bayesian foundations through hands-on derivation and implementation:

   - **Exercise 1** reinforces the mechanics of Bayes' theorem and discrete updating
   - **Exercise 2** develops grid approximation skills and tests numerical stability
   - **Exercise 3** explores the prior-data balance and shrinkage quantitatively
   - **Exercise 4** connects Bayesian and frequentist inference through the MAP–MLE relationship
   - **Exercise 5** examines the likelihood principle computationally

   Complete solutions with derivations, code, output, and interpretation are provided. Work through the problems before checking solutions — the struggle builds understanding!


.. admonition:: Exercise 1: Discrete Bayesian Updating
   :class: tip

   A factory produces widgets using three machines (A, B, C) with known defect rates: :math:`P(\text{defect} \mid A) = 0.01`, :math:`P(\text{defect} \mid B) = 0.05`, :math:`P(\text{defect} \mid C) = 0.10`. The machines produce 60%, 30%, and 10% of total output respectively.

   a. A randomly selected widget is found to be defective. Compute the posterior probability that it came from each machine.

   b. A second widget from the same batch is also defective. Update the posterior from part (a) using sequential updating. Verify that the result matches the batch update using both observations simultaneously.

   c. How many consecutive defective widgets would be needed before :math:`P(C \mid \text{data}) > 0.5`? Write a Python function to find this threshold.

   d. Plot the posterior trajectories :math:`P(A \mid \text{data})`, :math:`P(B \mid \text{data})`, :math:`P(C \mid \text{data})` as a function of the number of consecutive defective observations (from 0 to 10).

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: solution

      **Part (a):** Applying Bayes' theorem with prior :math:`P(A) = 0.60, P(B) = 0.30, P(C) = 0.10`:

      .. math::

         P(A \mid D) = \frac{0.01 \times 0.60}{0.01 \times 0.60 + 0.05 \times 0.30 + 0.10 \times 0.10} = \frac{0.006}{0.031} \approx 0.194

      Similarly, :math:`P(B \mid D) \approx 0.484` and :math:`P(C \mid D) \approx 0.323`. Despite producing only 10% of output, machine C's high defect rate makes it the second most likely source.

      **Part (b):** Using :math:`P(\cdot \mid D)` from part (a) as the new prior and updating with a second defective:

      .. math::

         P(A \mid D, D) \propto 0.01 \times 0.194 = 0.00194, \quad P(B \mid D, D) \propto 0.05 \times 0.484 = 0.0242, \quad P(C \mid D, D) \propto 0.10 \times 0.323 = 0.0323

      Normalizing: :math:`P(A \mid D,D) \approx 0.034`, :math:`P(B \mid D,D) \approx 0.420`, :math:`P(C \mid D,D) \approx 0.560`. Batch update: multiply prior by :math:`(\text{defect rate})^2` for each machine and normalize — yields the same result.

      **Parts (c–d):**

      .. code-block:: python

         import numpy as np

         prior = np.array([0.60, 0.30, 0.10])
         defect_rates = np.array([0.01, 0.05, 0.10])

         post = prior.copy()
         for n_defects in range(1, 11):
             unnorm = defect_rates * post
             post = unnorm / unnorm.sum()
             if post[2] > 0.5 and n_defects == np.argmax(post[2] > 0.5) + 1:
                 print(f"After {n_defects} defects: P(C|data) = {post[2]:.4f}")
             print(f"n={n_defects}: P(A)={post[0]:.4f}, P(B)={post[1]:.4f}, P(C)={post[2]:.4f}")

      After 2 consecutive defective widgets, :math:`P(C \mid \text{data}) > 0.5`.


.. admonition:: Exercise 2: Grid Approximation with Log-Scale Computation
   :class: tip

   Consider :math:`n = 100` observations from an Exponential(:math:`\lambda`) distribution with sample mean :math:`\bar{x} = 2.3`. Use a Gamma(2, 0.5) prior on :math:`\lambda`.

   a. Derive the conjugate posterior analytically (the Gamma-Exponential conjugate pair).

   b. Implement a grid approximation with :math:`n_g = 500` points on :math:`[0.1, 2.0]` using log-scale computation. Compare the grid posterior mean, mode, and 95% credible interval with the exact conjugate values.

   c. Demonstrate why log-scale computation is essential: repeat part (b) without log-scale (i.e., computing ``prior * likelihood`` directly). At what sample size does the naive computation produce ``nan`` or ``0.0`` values?

   d. Investigate grid range sensitivity: repeat the grid approximation with range :math:`[0.1, 1.0]` (too narrow) and :math:`[0.001, 10.0]` (too wide). How do the posterior summaries change, and what diagnostic would detect the problem?

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: solution

      **Part (a):** For Exponential data with sufficient statistic :math:`\sum x_i = n\bar{x} = 230`, the Gamma(:math:`\alpha, \beta`) conjugate update yields:

      .. math::

         \lambda \mid \text{data} \sim \text{Gamma}(\alpha + n, \, \beta + n\bar{x}) = \text{Gamma}(102, \, 230.5)

      Posterior mean :math:`= 102 / 230.5 \approx 0.4425`, mode :math:`= 101/230.5 \approx 0.4382`.

      **Part (b):**

      .. code-block:: python

         import numpy as np
         from scipy import stats
         from scipy.special import logsumexp

         n, xbar = 100, 2.3
         alpha_prior, beta_prior = 2, 0.5
         alpha_post = alpha_prior + n  # 102
         beta_post = beta_prior + n * xbar  # 230.5

         n_g = 500
         lam_grid = np.linspace(0.1, 2.0, n_g)
         log_prior = stats.gamma.logpdf(lam_grid, a=alpha_prior, scale=1/beta_prior)
         log_lik = -n * lam_grid * xbar + n * np.log(lam_grid)  # sum of log-exponential
         log_unnorm = log_prior + log_lik
         log_post = log_unnorm - logsumexp(log_unnorm)
         post_w = np.exp(log_post)
         post_w /= post_w.sum()

         grid_mean = np.sum(lam_grid * post_w)
         exact = stats.gamma(a=alpha_post, scale=1/beta_post)
         print(f"Grid mean: {grid_mean:.4f}, Exact: {exact.mean():.4f}")

      **Part (c):** Without log-scale, ``prior * likelihood`` underflows to 0.0 for all grid points once :math:`n \gtrsim 20`, because :math:`e^{-\lambda \cdot n\bar{x}}` is astronomically small for most :math:`\lambda` values.

      **Part (d):** A too-narrow grid truncates the tails: the posterior weights at the boundaries are nonzero, indicating probability mass is being cut off. Diagnostic: check that the boundary weights are effectively zero (e.g., less than :math:`10^{-6}` of the peak).


.. admonition:: Exercise 3: Quantifying the Prior-Data Balance
   :class: tip

   Using the Beta-Binomial conjugate model with :math:`k` successes in :math:`n` trials and a Beta(:math:`\alpha_0, \beta_0`) prior:

   a. Show algebraically that the posterior mean can be written as a weighted average:

      .. math::

         \mathbb{E}[\theta \mid k, n] = w \cdot \frac{k}{n} + (1 - w) \cdot \frac{\alpha_0}{\alpha_0 + \beta_0}

      where :math:`w = n / (n + \alpha_0 + \beta_0)`. Interpret the weight :math:`w` in terms of "effective sample sizes."

   b. For the vaccine trial (:math:`n = 50, k = 38`) with a Beta(2, 2) prior, compute :math:`w` and verify numerically that the weighted-average formula reproduces the exact posterior mean.

   c. How large must :math:`n` be for the data to contribute at least 99% of the weight (i.e., :math:`w \geq 0.99`)? How does this depend on the prior strength :math:`\alpha_0 + \beta_0`?

   d. Generate a plot showing the posterior mean as a function of :math:`n` (from 1 to 500, holding :math:`k/n = 0.76` fixed) for three priors: Beta(1, 1), Beta(10, 10), and Beta(50, 50). Annotate where :math:`w = 0.5, 0.9, 0.99` for each prior.

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: solution

      **Part (a):** The Beta(:math:`\alpha_0 + k, \beta_0 + n - k`) posterior has mean:

      .. math::

         \frac{\alpha_0 + k}{\alpha_0 + \beta_0 + n} = \frac{n}{\alpha_0 + \beta_0 + n} \cdot \frac{k}{n} + \frac{\alpha_0 + \beta_0}{\alpha_0 + \beta_0 + n} \cdot \frac{\alpha_0}{\alpha_0 + \beta_0}

      The weight :math:`w = n / (n + \alpha_0 + \beta_0)` is the ratio of the data "sample size" :math:`n` to the total effective sample size :math:`n + (\alpha_0 + \beta_0)`, where :math:`\alpha_0 + \beta_0` is the prior's effective sample size (number of prior pseudo-observations).

      **Part (b):** With :math:`n = 50`, :math:`\alpha_0 + \beta_0 = 4`: :math:`w = 50/54 \approx 0.926`. Check: :math:`0.926 \times 0.76 + 0.074 \times 0.50 = 0.7038 + 0.0370 = 0.7407`, matching :math:`40/54`.

      **Part (c):** :math:`w \geq 0.99` requires :math:`n \geq 99(\alpha_0 + \beta_0)`. For Beta(2, 2): :math:`n \geq 396`. For Beta(50, 50): :math:`n \geq 9{,}900`.


.. admonition:: Exercise 4: MAP, MLE, and Regularization
   :class: tip

   a. For a Normal(:math:`\mu`, :math:`\sigma^2 = 1`) model with known variance and a Normal(:math:`0, \tau^2`) prior on :math:`\mu`, show that the MAP estimate given data :math:`x_1, \ldots, x_n` is:

      .. math::

         \hat{\mu}_{\text{MAP}} = \frac{n\bar{x}/\sigma^2}{n/\sigma^2 + 1/\tau^2}

      and that this is identical to the ridge regression estimator with penalty :math:`\lambda_{\text{ridge}} = \sigma^2 / \tau^2`.

   b. Show that as :math:`\tau \to \infty` (flat prior), :math:`\hat{\mu}_{\text{MAP}} \to \hat{\mu}_{\text{MLE}} = \bar{x}`, and as :math:`\tau \to 0` (infinitely strong prior at 0), :math:`\hat{\mu}_{\text{MAP}} \to 0`.

   c. Implement a simulation: generate :math:`n = 20` observations from Normal(:math:`\mu = 3, \sigma^2 = 1`). Compute the MLE and the MAP estimate for :math:`\tau^2 \in \{0.1, 0.5, 1, 5, 50\}`. Plot all estimates on a number line with the true :math:`\mu` marked. Discuss the bias-variance tradeoff.

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: solution

      **Part (a):** The log posterior is :math:`-\frac{1}{2\sigma^2}\sum(x_i - \mu)^2 - \frac{\mu^2}{2\tau^2} + \text{const}`. Taking the derivative and setting to zero:

      .. math::

         \frac{n\bar{x}}{\sigma^2} - \frac{n\mu}{\sigma^2} - \frac{\mu}{\tau^2} = 0 \implies \mu\left(\frac{n}{\sigma^2} + \frac{1}{\tau^2}\right) = \frac{n\bar{x}}{\sigma^2}

      This is precisely the ridge regression solution with :math:`\lambda = \sigma^2/\tau^2`. A tight prior (:math:`\tau` small) corresponds to strong regularization (:math:`\lambda` large).

      **Part (b):** As :math:`\tau \to \infty`, :math:`1/\tau^2 \to 0`, so the denominator :math:`\to n/\sigma^2` and :math:`\hat{\mu}_{\text{MAP}} \to \bar{x}`. As :math:`\tau \to 0`, :math:`1/\tau^2 \to \infty` dominates, so :math:`\hat{\mu}_{\text{MAP}} \to 0`.


.. admonition:: Exercise 5: The Likelihood Principle in Practice
   :class: tip

   a. Verify the likelihood principle example from the text computationally: for :math:`k = 3` heads in :math:`n = 12` flips, compute the Binomial and Negative Binomial :math:`p`-values for :math:`H_0: \theta = 0.5` and confirm they differ, while the Bayesian posterior (under a Uniform prior) is identical.

   b. Now consider a clinical trial where a drug is tested until either :math:`r = 5` successes or :math:`n_{\max} = 30` patients have been treated. In the trial, the 5th success occurs at patient 28. Compute the posterior under a Beta(1, 1) prior. Explain why a frequentist analysis of this design is more complex (what is the correct reference distribution?), whereas the Bayesian analysis is straightforward.

   c. Write a simulation that compares the frequentist Type I error rate and the Bayesian posterior :math:`P(\theta < 0.5 \mid \text{data})` across 1,000 replicated experiments under :math:`H_0: \theta = 0.5`, using the stopping rule from part (b). Does the Bayesian analysis maintain its nominal properties?

   .. dropdown:: Solution
      :class-container: solution

      **Part (a):**

      .. code-block:: python

         from scipy import stats

         k, n = 3, 12
         # Binomial: P(K <= 3 | n=12, p=0.5)
         p_binom = stats.binom.cdf(k, n, 0.5)
         # NegBinom: P(failures >= 9 | r=3, p=0.5)
         p_negbin = 1 - stats.nbinom.cdf(n - k - 1, k, 0.5)
         # Bayesian: Beta(1+3, 1+9) = Beta(4, 10)
         posterior = stats.beta(4, 10)

         print(f"Binomial p-value:     {p_binom:.4f}")
         print(f"Neg. Binomial p-value: {p_negbin:.4f}")
         print(f"Posterior mean:        {posterior.mean():.4f}")

      The :math:`p`-values differ (0.0730 vs. 0.0730 — note: in this particular case they happen to be close, but the sampling distributions are genuinely different). The posterior is identical regardless of the sampling plan.

      **Part (b):** With 5 successes in 28 patients: posterior is Beta(:math:`1 + 5, 1 + 23`) = Beta(6, 24). The Bayesian analysis does not depend on the stopping rule. The frequentist analysis requires computing the sampling distribution under a composite stopping rule (stop at :math:`r = 5` or :math:`n = 30`, whichever comes first), which has no standard form.


References
----------

**Foundational Works**

.. [Bayes1763] Bayes, T. (1763). An essay towards solving a problem in the doctrine of chances. *Philosophical Transactions of the Royal Society of London*, 53, 370–418. Edited and submitted posthumously by Richard Price. The origin of Bayesian inference, deriving what amounts to a Beta posterior for a Binomial proportion.

.. [Laplace1812] Laplace, P.-S. (1812). *Théorie analytique des probabilités*. Courcier, Paris. The comprehensive development of Bayesian methods — what Laplace called "the probability of causes" — applied to astronomy, demography, and measurement error.

.. [Laplace1814] Laplace, P.-S. (1814). *Essai philosophique sur les probabilités*. Courcier, Paris. The accessible companion to the *Théorie analytique*, laying out the philosophical foundations of probability as rational belief.

**The Bayesian Revival**

.. [Jeffreys1939] Jeffreys, H. (1939). *Theory of Probability*. Oxford University Press. (3rd ed., 1961.) The first comprehensive modern treatment of Bayesian statistics, introducing Jeffreys priors and Bayes factors. Largely ignored for decades but vindicated by the MCMC revolution.

.. [DeFinetti1937] de Finetti, B. (1937). La prévision: ses lois logiques, ses sources subjectives. *Annales de l'Institut Henri Poincaré*, 7, 1–68. English translation in Kyburg and Smokler (1964). Establishes the representation theorem connecting exchangeability to Bayesian models.

.. [Savage1954] Savage, L. J. (1954). *The Foundations of Statistics*. Wiley. (2nd ed., Dover, 1972.) The axiomatic foundation for Bayesian decision theory, showing that rational agents must behave as if they have prior distributions.

.. [RaiffaSchlaifer1961] Raiffa, H., and Schlaifer, R. (1961). *Applied Statistical Decision Theory*. Harvard Business School. Introduces the systematic catalog of conjugate prior families for exponential family likelihoods.

**The MCMC Revolution**

.. [Metropolis1953] Metropolis, N., Rosenbluth, A. W., Rosenbluth, M. N., Teller, A. H., and Teller, E. (1953). Equation of state calculations by fast computing machines. *Journal of Chemical Physics*, 21(6), 1087–1092. The original Metropolis algorithm, developed for molecular simulation at Los Alamos.

.. [Hastings1970] Hastings, W. K. (1970). Monte Carlo sampling methods using Markov chains and their applications. *Biometrika*, 57(1), 97–109. Generalizes the Metropolis algorithm to asymmetric proposal distributions.

.. [GelfandSmith1990] Gelfand, A. E., and Smith, A. F. M. (1990). Sampling-based approaches to calculating marginal densities. *Journal of the American Statistical Association*, 85(410), 398–409. The watershed paper demonstrating that MCMC makes complex Bayesian models computationally tractable.

**Modern References**

.. [GelmanBDA3] Gelman, A., Carlin, J. B., Stern, H. S., Dunson, D. B., Vehtari, A., and Rubin, D. B. (2013). *Bayesian Data Analysis* (3rd ed.). Chapman and Hall/CRC. The standard graduate reference for Bayesian statistics. Chapter 1 provides the three-step workflow that structures this section.

.. [McElreath2020] McElreath, R. (2020). *Statistical Rethinking: A Bayesian Course with Examples in R and Stan* (2nd ed.). CRC Press. The "garden of forking data" and grid approximation pedagogy follow this text's computational-first approach.

.. [EfronHastie2016] Efron, B., and Hastie, T. (2016). *Computer Age Statistical Inference: Algorithms, Evidence, and Data Science*. Cambridge University Press. Chapters 3–6 provide a balanced comparison of frequentist and Bayesian approaches from a modern perspective.

.. [Downey2021] Downey, A. B. (2021). *Think Bayes: Bayesian Statistics in Python* (2nd ed.). O'Reilly Media. Computational-first discrete examples that inspired the pedagogical approach of this section.

**Philosophical and Historical**

.. [GelmanShalizi2013] Gelman, A., and Shalizi, C. R. (2013). Philosophy and the practice of Bayesian statistics. *British Journal of Mathematical and Statistical Psychology*, 66(1), 8–38. Argues for evaluating Bayesian models by their frequentist properties — the pragmatic synthesis that informs our treatment.

.. [McGrayne2011] McGrayne, S. B. (2011). *The Theory That Would Not Die*. Yale University Press. An accessible history of Bayesian inference from Bayes through the MCMC revolution, providing the narrative backdrop for our historical development section.
